# Sample Problem 23.06: Gauss’ law and an upward streamer in a lightning storm

## Problem
A woman standing on a lookout platform high in Sequoia National Park was under a large storm cloud. Some of the conduction electrons in her body were driven into the ground by the cloud’s negatively charged base, leaving her positively charged. Her hair strands repelled one another and extended away from her along the electric field lines produced by the charge on her.

Lightning did not strike the woman, but she was in extreme danger because that field magnitude along her body had exceeded the critical value \(E_c = 2.4 \text{ MN/C}\). What value of \(Q\) would have put the air along her body on the verge of breakdown?

Let’s model her body as a narrow vertical cylinder of height \(L = 1.8 \text{ m}\) and radius \(R = 0.10 \text{ m}\). Assume that charge \(Q\) was uniformly distributed along the cylinder and that electrical breakdown would have occurred if the electric field was on the verge of causing electrical breakdown in the surrounding air.

## Key ideas
Because \(R \ll L\), we can approximate the charge distribution as a long line of charge. Further, because we assume that the charge is uniformly distributed along this line, we can approximate the magnitude of the electric field along the side of her body with
\[
E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi \varepsilon_0 r}.
\]

## Solution
Substituting the critical value \(E_c\) for \(E\), the cylinder radius \(R\) for radial distance \(r\), and the ratio \(Q/L\) for linear charge density \(\lambda\), we have
\[
E_c = \frac{Q/L}{2\pi \varepsilon_0 R}.
\]
Or,
\[
Q = 2\pi \varepsilon_0 R L E_c.
\]

Substituting the given data then gives
\[
Q = (2\pi)(8.85 \times 10^{-12} \text{ C}^2/\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)(0.10 \text{ m})(1.8 \text{ m})(2.4 \times 10^{6} \text{ N/C}).
\]

## Answer
\[
Q = 2.4 \times 10^{-5} \text{ C} \approx 24 \,\mu\text{C}.
\]

## Key concepts used
- Gauss’ law
- Electric field of a long line of charge
- Critical electric field for air breakdown
- Linear charge density \(\lambda = Q/L\)
