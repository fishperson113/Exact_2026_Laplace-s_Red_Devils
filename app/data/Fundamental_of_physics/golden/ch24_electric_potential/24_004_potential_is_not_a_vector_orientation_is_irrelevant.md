# Sample Problem 24.04: Potential is not a vector, orientation is irrelevant

## Problem
(a) In Fig. 24-12a, 12 electrons (of charge \( -e \)) are equally spaced and fixed around a circle of radius \(R\). Relative to \(V = 0\) at infinity, what are the electric potential and electric field at the center \(C\) of the circle due to these electrons?

## Key ideas
- The electric potential \(V\) at \(C\) is the algebraic sum of the electric potentials contributed by all the electrons.
- Potential is a scalar and orientation is irrelevant.

## Solution
Because electric potential is a scalar, the orientations of the particles do not matter.

From Eq. 24-27, we have
\[
V = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\sum_i \frac{q_i}{r}.
\]

Here each electron is the same distance \(R\) from the center, so the distance \(r\) is \(R\). The sum of the charges is
\[
q_1 + q_2 + \cdots + q_{12} = 12(-e).
\]

Thus,
\[
V = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{12(-e)}{R}.
\]

By symmetry, the electric field contributions from the 12 equally spaced electrons cancel at the center, so the net electric field is zero.

## Answer
\[
V = -\frac{12e}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R}, \qquad \vec{E} = 0.
\]

## Key concepts used
- Electric potential is a scalar quantity.
- Potentials add algebraically.
- The electric field is a vector and cancels by symmetry at the center of a uniformly spaced ring of identical charges.
