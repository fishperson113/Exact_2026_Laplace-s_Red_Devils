# Sample Problem 24.05: Finding the field from the potential

## Problem
The electric potential at any point on the central axis of a uniformly charged disk is given by Eq. 24-37. Starting with this expression, derive an expression for the electric field at any point on the axis of the disk.

## Key ideas
We want the electric field as a function of distance \(z\) along the axis of the disk. For any value of \(z\), the direction of \(\vec E\) must be along that axis because of the disk’s circular symmetry. The component of \(\vec E\) in any direction is the negative of the rate at which the electric potential changes with distance in that direction.

## Solution
If we take the \(s\) axis to be, in turn, the \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) axes, we find that the \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) components of \(\vec E\) at any point are

\[
E_x = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}; \qquad
E_y = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial y}; \qquad
E_z = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial z}.
\]

Thus, if we know \(V\) for all points in the region around a charge distribution—that is, if we know the function \(V(x,y,z)\)—we can find the components of \(\vec E\), and thus \(\vec E\) itself, at any point by taking partial derivatives.

For the simple situation in which the electric field \(\vec E\) is uniform, Eq. 24-40 becomes

\[
E = -\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta s},
\]

where \(s\) is perpendicular to the equipotential surfaces. The component of the electric field is zero in any direction parallel to the equipotential surfaces because there is no change in potential along the surfaces.

## Answer
\[
E_x = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}; \qquad
E_y = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial y}; \qquad
E_z = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial z}.
\]

For a uniform field,
\[
E = -\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta s}.
\]

## Key concepts used
- Electric field from electric potential
- Components of \(\vec E\) from partial derivatives of \(V(x,y,z)\)
- Field direction normal to equipotential surfaces
- Circular symmetry of a uniformly charged disk
