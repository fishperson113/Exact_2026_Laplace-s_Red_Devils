# Sample Problem 24.07: Conservation of mechanical energy with electric potential energy

## Problem
An alpha particle (two protons, two neutrons) moves into a stationary gold atom (79 protons, 118 neutrons), passing through the electron region that surrounds the gold nucleus like a shell and headed directly toward the nucleus (Fig. 24-20). The alpha particle slows until it momentarily stops when its center is at radial distance \(r \approx 9.23\ \mathrm{fm}\) from the nuclear center. Then it moves back along its incoming path. (Because the gold nucleus is much more massive than the alpha particle, we can assume the gold nucleus does not move.) What was the kinetic energy \(K_i\) of the alpha particle when it was initially far away (hence external to the gold atom)? Assume that the only force acting between the alpha particle and the gold nucleus is the (electrostatic) Coulomb force and treat each as a single charged particle.

## Key ideas
During the entire process, the mechanical energy of the alpha particle–gold atom system is conserved.

Reasoning: When the alpha particle is outside the atom, the system’s initial electric potential energy \(U_i\) is zero because the atom has an equal number of electrons and protons, which produce a net electric field of zero. However, once the alpha particle passes through the electron region surrounding the nucleus on its way to the nucleus, the electric field due to the electrons goes to zero. The reason is that the electrons act like a closed spherical shell of uniform negative charge and, as discussed in Module 23-6, such a shell produces zero electric field in the space it encloses. The alpha particle still experiences the electric field of the protons in the nucleus, which produces a repulsive force on the protons within the alpha particle.

## Solution
Because the alpha particle momentarily stops at its closest approach, its final kinetic energy is
\[
K_f = 0.
\]

Thus, the initial kinetic energy is converted into electric potential energy:
\[
K_i = U_f - U_i.
\]

Since \(U_i = 0\) and the interaction is between the alpha particle charge \(+2e\) and the gold nucleus charge \(+79e\),
\[
U_f = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{(2e)(79e)}{r}.
\]

Using
\[
e = 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}, \qquad
r = 9.23\ \mathrm{fm} = 9.23\times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m},
\]
we get
\[
K_i = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{(2)(79)e^2}{r}.
\]

Substituting the constants,
\[
K_i = (8.99\times 10^9\ \mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C^2})
\frac{(158)(1.60\times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C})^2}{9.23\times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}}.
\]

This gives
\[
K_i \approx 3.9\times 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{J}.
\]

## Answer
\[
\boxed{K_i \approx 3.9\times 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{J}}
\]

## Key concepts used
- Conservation of mechanical energy
- Electric potential energy of two point charges
- Coulomb force
- Nuclear charge \(= +79e\)
- Alpha particle charge \(= +2e\)
