## Worked solution
The problem statement appears to be missing the numerical value of the electric field strength after the words “in an electric field of.” Since the source answer provided is only qualitative, we can still determine the direction of the electron’s acceleration.

For a charge $q $ in an electric field $\vec E $, the electric force is

$$ 
\vec F = q\vec E
 $$

For an electron, $q=-e $, so the force is opposite the direction of the field:

$$ 
\vec F = -e\vec E
 $$

Using Newton’s second law,

$$ 
\vec a = \frac{\vec F}{m_e} = \frac{-e\vec E}{m_e}
 $$

Thus, the acceleration of the electron points opposite the electric field.

If the missing field magnitude were available, the acceleration magnitude would be

$$ 
a = \frac{eE}{m_e}
 $$

but the OCR is incomplete here, so the numerical magnitude cannot be reliably computed from the text provided.

Therefore, the direction of the acceleration is opposite to the field.

## Key concepts used
- Electric force on a charge: $\vec F = q\vec E $
- Electron charge is negative: $q=-e $
- Newton’s second law: $\vec a = \vec F/m $
- Negative charge accelerates opposite the electric field direction
