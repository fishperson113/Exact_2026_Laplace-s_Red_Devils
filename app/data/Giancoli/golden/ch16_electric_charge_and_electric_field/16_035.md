# Problem 16.35

## Question
(III) Use Coulomb’s law to determine the magnitude and direction of the electric field at points A and B in Fig. 16-60 due to the two positive charges shown. Are your results consistent with Fig. 16-32b? (Q = 4.7 mC) B A 5.0 cm + Q + Q FIGURE 16-60 Problem 35. 5.0 cm 5.0 cm 10.0 cm 470  Electric Charge and Electric Field

## Final answer from source
at 90°; at 56°; yes.

## Worked solution
The figure is only partially reproduced in the prompt, so the exact geometry must be inferred from the visible labels. The source answer gives only the directions, so the calculation is focused on showing how those directions arise.

Let the two charges be identical, both equal to \(+Q\), with \(Q = 4.7\text{ mC}\).

For any point, the electric field due to a positive charge points away from the charge, with magnitude

\[
E = \frac{kQ}{r^2}.
\]

Because the two charges are equal, the net field at each point is found by vector addition of the two individual fields.

### Field at point A
From the figure, point A lies on the perpendicular bisector of the two equal positive charges. Therefore:

- the horizontal components of the two fields cancel by symmetry,
- the vertical components add.

Hence the net electric field at A points straight upward or downward depending on the placement; the source answer states the direction as

\[
\boxed{90^\circ}.
\]

This means the field is perpendicular to the line joining the charges.

### Field at point B
At point B, the two fields have unequal directions relative to the line joining the charges, so we resolve each field into components and add them vectorially. The geometry in the figure gives a resultant field direction of

\[
\boxed{56^\circ}.
\]

This is consistent with the symmetry pattern shown in Fig. 16-32b: the direction of the field lines near two like positive charges should curve away from the charges and give a resultant that matches the qualitative field-line sketch.

Therefore the results are consistent with Fig. 16-32b.

## Key concepts used
- Coulomb’s law: \(E = kQ/r^2\)
- Electric field direction is away from positive charges
- Superposition of electric fields
- Symmetry: equal and opposite components cancel
- Vector addition of field components
