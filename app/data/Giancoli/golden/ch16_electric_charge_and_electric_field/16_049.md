# Problem 16.49

## Question
A water droplet of radius 0.018 mm remains stationary in the air. If the downward-directed electric field of the Earth is how many excess electron charges must the water droplet have? 150 N(cid:2)C,

## Final answer from source
electron charges.

## Worked solution
This problem asks for the number of excess electrons needed so that the electric force balances the droplet’s weight.

Since the droplet is stationary, the net force is zero:

- downward gravitational force: \(F_g = mg\)
- electric force: \(F_E = qE\)

Because the Earth’s electric field is **downward**, the droplet must have a **negative charge** so that the electric force is upward and can balance gravity.

Set magnitudes equal:

\[
|q|E = mg
\]

So

\[
|q| = \frac{mg}{E}
\]

Now compute the mass of the droplet from its radius.

Radius:
\[
r = 0.018\ \text{mm} = 1.8\times10^{-5}\ \text{m}
\]

Assuming the droplet is water, with density
\[
\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3
\]

Volume of a sphere:
\[
V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3
\]

\[
V = \frac{4}{3}\pi (1.8\times10^{-5})^3
  \approx 2.44\times10^{-14}\ \text{m}^3
\]

Mass:
\[
m = \rho V \approx 1000(2.44\times10^{-14})
  = 2.44\times10^{-11}\ \text{kg}
\]

Now use \(g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\) and the Earth’s electric field magnitude
\[
E = 150\ \text{N/C}
\]

\[
|q| = \frac{(2.44\times10^{-11})(9.8)}{150}
\approx 1.59\times10^{-12}\ \text{C}
\]

Each electron has charge magnitude
\[
e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}
\]

Thus the number of excess electrons is

\[
N = \frac{|q|}{e}
  = \frac{1.59\times10^{-12}}{1.60\times10^{-19}}
  \approx 9.9\times10^6
\]

So the droplet must have about

\[
\boxed{1.0\times10^7\ \text{excess electron charges}}
\]

Because the field is downward, these are negative charges.

## Key concepts used
- Force balance for an object at rest: electric force equals weight
- Electric force magnitude: \(F = |q|E\)
- Mass from density and volume of a sphere
- Converting total charge to number of electrons using \(e = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}\)
