# Problem 17.31

## Question
(III) In the Bohr model of the hydrogen atom, an electron orbits a proton (the nucleus) in a circular orbit of radius 0.53 * 10-10 m. (a) What is the electric potential at the electron’s orbit due to the proton? (b) What is the kinetic energy of the electron? (c) What is the total energy of the electron in its orbit? (d) What is the ionization energy- that is, the energy required to remove the electron from the at rest? Express the results of atom and take it to parts (b), (c), and (d) in joules and eV. r = q, *17-6 Electric Dipoles

## Final answer from source
(a) 27 V; (b) or 14 eV; (c) or (d) or 14 eV.

## Worked solution
This problem appears to have OCR corruption in the statement and in the source answer line, so the exact textbook wording and some answer entries are incomplete. The standard Bohr-model results for hydrogen can still be derived.

For a proton and electron separated by radius  
$$ 
r=0.53\times 10^{-10}\ \text{m},
 $$
the electric potential due to the proton at the electron’s orbit is
$$ 
V=\frac{kq}{r},
 $$
with
$$ 
k=8.99\times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2,\qquad q=+e=1.60\times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}.
 $$

### (a) Electric potential
$$ 
V=\frac{(8.99\times 10^9)(1.60\times 10^{-19})}{0.53\times 10^{-10}}
\approx 27\ \text{V}.
 $$

### (b) Kinetic energy
For a circular Bohr orbit, the Coulomb force provides centripetal force:
$$ 
\frac{mv^2}{r}=\frac{k e^2}{r^2}.
 $$
Thus
$$ 
mv^2=\frac{k e^2}{r}.
 $$
The kinetic energy is
$$ 
K=\frac12 mv^2=\frac12 \frac{k e^2}{r}.
 $$
But $\frac{k e^2}{r}=eV $, so
$$ 
K=\frac12 eV.
 $$
Using $V\approx 27\ \text{V} $,
$$ 
K\approx \frac12 (27\ \text{eV})=13.5\ \text{eV}\approx 14\ \text{eV}.
 $$
In joules:
$$ 
K\approx 13.5(1.60\times 10^{-19})\approx 2.16\times 10^{-18}\ \text{J}.
 $$

### (c) Total energy
The electrostatic potential energy is
$$ 
U=-\frac{k e^2}{r}=-eV\approx -27\ \text{eV}.
 $$
Therefore
$$ 
E=K+U=\frac12 eV-eV=-\frac12 eV.
 $$
So
$$ 
E\approx -13.5\ \text{eV}\approx -14\ \text{eV}.
 $$
In joules:
$$ 
E\approx -2.16\times 10^{-18}\ \text{J}.
 $$

### (d) Ionization energy
To remove the electron from the atom at rest means taking it from the bound state to $E=0 $. The required energy is
$$ 
E_{\text{ion}}=0-E=-E\approx 13.5\ \text{eV}\approx 14\ \text{eV}.
 $$
In joules:
$$ 
E_{\text{ion}}\approx 2.16\times 10^{-18}\ \text{J}.
 $$

So the results are:
- (a) $27\ \text{V} $
- (b) $2.16\times 10^{-18}\ \text{J} \approx 14\ \text{eV} $
- (c) $-2.16\times 10^{-18}\ \text{J} \approx -14\ \text{eV} $
- (d) $2.16\times 10^{-18}\ \text{J} \approx 14\ \text{eV} $

## Key concepts used
- Electric potential of a point charge: $V=kq/r $
- Potential energy of two point charges: $U=kq_1q_2/r $
- Bohr model circular-orbit condition
- For Coulomb orbits: $K=\tfrac12 k e^2/r $ and $U=-k e^2/r $
- Total energy in a bound orbit: $E=K+U=-K $
- Ionization energy is the energy difference between the bound state and zero energy at infinity
