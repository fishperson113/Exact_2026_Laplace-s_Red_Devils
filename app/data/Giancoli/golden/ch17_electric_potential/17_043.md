# Problem 17.43

## Question
(II) To get an idea how big a farad is, suppose you want to make a 1-F air-filled parallel-plate capacitor for a circuit you are building. To make it a reasonable size, suppose you 1.0 cm2. limit the plate area to What would the gap have to be between the plates? Is this practically achievable?

## Final answer from source
no.

## Worked solution
For a parallel-plate capacitor filled with air, the capacitance is

$$ 
C=\varepsilon_0 \frac{A}{d}
 $$

where:
- $C = 1.0\ \text{F} $
- $A = 1.0\ \text{cm}^2 = 1.0\times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2 $
- $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85\times 10^{-12}\ \text{F/m} $

Solve for the plate separation $d $:

$$ 
d=\varepsilon_0 \frac{A}{C}
 $$

Substitute the values:

$$ 
d=(8.85\times 10^{-12})\frac{1.0\times 10^{-4}}{1.0}
 $$

$$ 
d=8.85\times 10^{-16}\ \text{m}
 $$

This gap is extraordinarily small, far smaller than atomic dimensions, so it is not practically achievable for an air-filled capacitor.

Therefore, the answer is: **no**.

## Key concepts used
- Parallel-plate capacitor formula $C=\varepsilon_0 A/d $
- Unit conversion: $\text{cm}^2 $ to $\text{m}^2 $
- Solving for plate spacing $d $
- Physical feasibility of extremely small separations
