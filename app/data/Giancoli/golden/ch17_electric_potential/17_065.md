# Problem 17.65

## Question
(II) Figure 17-44 is a photograph of a computer screen During shot by a camera set at an exposure time of the exposure the cursor arrow was moved around by the mouse, and we see it 15 times. (a) Explain why we see the cursor 15 times. (b) What is the refresh rate of the screen? s. 1 4

## Final answer from source
(b) 56 Hz.

## Worked solution
This problem depends on details in Figure 17-44, which is not provided here. The figure seems to show a camera exposure of a computer screen during which the cursor was moved and appears 15 times. Since the exact exposure time is not fully readable from the OCR text, the solution for part (b) must rely on the relationship between the number of visible cursor positions and the screen refresh rate.

### (a) Why the cursor appears 15 times
A computer screen refreshes periodically. If the cursor is moved while the camera shutter remains open, the camera records successive positions of the cursor as the screen is updated. Because the exposure spans multiple refresh cycles, the cursor appears multiple times in the photograph, once for each visible update position captured during the exposure.

### (b) Refresh rate of the screen
The refresh rate is the number of screen updates per second.

If the exposure time is \(T\) and the cursor appears \(N=15\) times, then the refresh period is approximately
\[
\Delta t = \frac{T}{N-1}
\]
or equivalently the refresh rate is
\[
f = \frac{N-1}{T}
\]
depending on whether the first and last cursor images correspond to the endpoints of the exposure. The source answer gives the refresh rate directly as

\[
\boxed{56\ \text{Hz}}
\]

so the screen refreshes 56 times per second.

## Key concepts used
- A camera with finite exposure records motion as multiple positions when the object/screen changes during the exposure.
- Screen refresh rate is the reciprocal of the refresh period.
- The number of visible cursor images in a long exposure is related to the number of refresh cycles captured.
- When a figure or exposure-time detail is missing, the exact derivation cannot be fully reconstructed from OCR alone.
