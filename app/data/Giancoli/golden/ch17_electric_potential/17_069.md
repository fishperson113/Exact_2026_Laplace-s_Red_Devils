# Problem 17.69

## Question
In an older television tube, electrons are accelerated by thousands of volts through a vacuum. If a television set were laid on its back, would electrons be able to move upward against the force of gravity? What potential difference, acting over a distance of 2.4 cm, would be needed to balance the downward force of gravity so that an electron would remain stationary? Assume that the electric field is uniform.

## Final answer from source
Yes,

## Worked solution
An electron has two relevant forces acting on it:

- downward gravitational force: $F_g = mg $
- electric force: $F_E = |q|E $

To keep the electron stationary, the electric force must balance gravity:

$$ 
|q|E = mg
 $$

So the required electric field is

$$ 
E = \frac{mg}{|q|}
 $$

Using
- $m_e = 9.11\times10^{-31}\,\text{kg} $
- $g = 9.80\,\text{m/s}^2 $
- $|q| = 1.60\times10^{-19}\,\text{C} $

we get

$$ 
E = \frac{(9.11\times10^{-31})(9.80)}{1.60\times10^{-19}}
\approx 5.6\times10^{-11}\,\text{N/C}
 $$

For a uniform electric field over a distance $d = 2.4\,\text{cm} = 2.4\times10^{-2}\,\text{m} $, the needed potential difference is

$$ 
\Delta V = Ed
 $$

so

$$ 
\Delta V = (5.6\times10^{-11})(2.4\times10^{-2})
\approx 1.3\times10^{-12}\,\text{V}
 $$

This is an extraordinarily small potential difference, showing that gravity is negligible compared with typical TV-tube electric effects. Therefore, yes, electrons would be able to move upward against gravity.

## Key concepts used
- Electric force on a charge: $F = |q|E $
- Weight of a particle: $F_g = mg $
- Equilibrium condition: electric force balances gravitational force
- Uniform electric field relation: $\Delta V = Ed $
- Electron mass and charge values
