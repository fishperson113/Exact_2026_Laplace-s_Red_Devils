# Problem 17.81

## Question
A ±38 mC point charge is placed 36 cm from an identical ±38 mC charge is moved from point A to point B as shown in Fig. 17-47. What is the change in potential energy? charge. A -1.5 mC B FIGURE 17-47 Problem 81. 38 mC 14 cm 12 cm 24 cm A 38 mC K = 3.7

## Worked solution
The OCR for the problem statement and figure is incomplete, so the exact charge signs, geometry, and distances must be inferred cautiously. The source answer is taken as fixed.

For a point charge moved in the electric field of another point charge, the change in electric potential energy is

\[
\Delta U = q\,\Delta V = k\,Qq\left(\frac{1}{r_f}-\frac{1}{r_i}\right),
\]

where:
- \(Q\) is the fixed source charge,
- \(q\) is the moving charge,
- \(r_i\) is the initial distance from \(Q\),
- \(r_f\) is the final distance from \(Q\).

From the figure text we can identify:
- a charge of magnitude \(38\ \text{mC}\),
- a moving charge of \(-1.5\ \text{mC}\),
- distances labeled \(14\ \text{cm}\), \(12\ \text{cm}\), and \(24\ \text{cm}\),
- and a note indicating \(k \approx 3.7\) (likely a rounded OCR fragment of the Coulomb constant scaling in the worked source).

Because the figure is not fully readable, the exact geometry cannot be reconstructed with certainty. However, the source answer indicates the computed potential-energy change is positive and equal to \(1.8\ \text{J}\). That sign is consistent with moving a negative charge to a position of lower electric potential (more positive potential energy increase) or with a configuration in which the interaction energy becomes less negative.

Thus, consistent with the source, the change in potential energy is

\[
\boxed{\Delta U = 1.8\ \text{J}}.
\]

## Key concepts used
- Electric potential energy of two point charges: \(U = kQq/r\)
- Change in potential energy for motion in an electrostatic field: \(\Delta U = q\Delta V\)
- Superposition and dependence on initial/final separation
- Sign of \(\Delta U\) depends on charge signs and whether the final state is more or less favorable electrostatically
