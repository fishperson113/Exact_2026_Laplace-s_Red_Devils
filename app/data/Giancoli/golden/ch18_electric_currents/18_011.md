# Problem 18.11

## Question
When a light switch is turned on, the light comes on immediately because  
(a) the electrons coming from the power source move through the initially empty wires very fast.  
(b) the electrons already in the wire are instantly “pushed” by a voltage difference.  
(c) the lightbulb may be old with low resistance. It would take longer if the bulb were new and had high resistance.  
(d) the electricity bill is paid. The electric company can make it take longer when the bill is unpaid.  

## Final answer from source
(a) 4.8 A; (b) 6.6 A.

## Worked solution
Use Ohm’s law:
\[
V=IR
\]

First find the resistance of the device from the original operating conditions:
\[
R=\frac{V}{I}=\frac{240\ \text{V}}{5.60\ \text{A}}=42.9\ \Omega
\]

### (a) Voltage drops by 15%
A 15% drop from 240 V gives
\[
V' = 0.85(240\ \text{V}) = 204\ \text{V}
\]

Assuming the resistance stays the same:
\[
I'=\frac{V'}{R}=\frac{204\ \text{V}}{42.9\ \Omega}=4.76\ \text{A}
\]

Rounded appropriately:
\[
I' \approx 4.8\ \text{A}
\]

### (b) Resistance reduced by 15% at 240 V
A 15% reduction in resistance gives
\[
R' = 0.85R = 0.85(42.9\ \Omega)=36.4\ \Omega
\]

Then
\[
I'=\frac{V}{R'}=\frac{240\ \text{V}}{36.4\ \Omega}=6.59\ \text{A}
\]

Rounded appropriately:
\[
I' \approx 6.6\ \text{A}
\]

## Key concepts used
- Ohm’s law: \(V=IR\)
- Current is proportional to voltage when resistance is constant
- Current increases when resistance decreases at fixed voltage
- Percent change calculations
