# Problem 18.67

## Question
A small city requires about 15 MW of power. Suppose that instead of using high-voltage lines to supply the power, the power is delivered at 120 V. Assuming a two-wire line of 0.50-cm-diameter copper wire, estimate the cost of the energy lost to heat per hour per meter. Assume the cost of electricity is about 12 cents per kWh.  523

## Final answer from source
$3200 per hour per meter.

## Worked solution
We estimate the resistive loss in the two-wire line, then convert that lost power into cost.

### 1) Current needed to deliver 15 MW at 120 V
The city requires
\[
P = 15\times 10^6\ \text{W}
\]
at a delivery voltage of
\[
V = 120\ \text{V}.
\]
So the current must be
\[
I=\frac{P}{V}=\frac{15\times 10^6}{120}=1.25\times 10^5\ \text{A}.
\]

### 2) Resistance of 1 m of the two-wire line
The wire diameter is \(0.50\ \text{cm}\), so the radius is
\[
r = 0.25\ \text{cm} = 2.5\times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}.
\]
Cross-sectional area of one wire:
\[
A=\pi r^2=\pi(2.5\times 10^{-3})^2 \approx 1.96\times 10^{-5}\ \text{m}^2.
\]

For copper, use
\[
\rho \approx 1.7\times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}.
\]
Thus the resistance of 1 m of one wire is
\[
R_1=\frac{\rho L}{A}
=\frac{(1.7\times 10^{-8})(1)}{1.96\times 10^{-5}}
\approx 8.7\times 10^{-4}\ \Omega.
\]

Since the line is two-wire, the current goes out and back, so the resistance per meter of line is
\[
R = 2R_1 \approx 1.7\times 10^{-3}\ \Omega/\text{m}.
\]

### 3) Power lost as heat per meter
The resistive power loss is
\[
P_{\text{loss}} = I^2R
= (1.25\times 10^5)^2(1.7\times 10^{-3})
\approx 2.7\times 10^7\ \text{W per meter}.
\]

So each meter of line dissipates about
\[
2.7\times 10^7\ \text{J/s}
\]
as heat.

### 4) Cost of the lost energy per hour per meter
In one hour, the wasted energy per meter is
\[
E = P_{\text{loss}}(3600\ \text{s})
\approx (2.7\times 10^7)(3600)
\approx 9.8\times 10^{10}\ \text{J}.
\]
Convert to kWh:
\[
1\ \text{kWh}=3.6\times 10^6\ \text{J},
\]
so
\[
E \approx \frac{9.8\times 10^{10}}{3.6\times 10^6}
\approx 2.7\times 10^4\ \text{kWh}.
\]
At 12 cents per kWh,
\[
\text{cost} \approx (2.7\times 10^4)(0.12)\approx 3.2\times 10^3\ \text{dollars}.
\]

So the cost of energy lost is approximately
\[
\boxed{\$3200\ \text{per hour per meter}.}
\]

## Key concepts used
- Electric power relation: \(P=IV\)
- Resistance of a wire: \(R=\rho L/A\)
- Resistive heating: \(P_{\text{loss}}=I^2R\)
- Unit conversion between J, kWh, and cost per kWh
- Two-wire transmission line means the resistance is doubled by the return wire
