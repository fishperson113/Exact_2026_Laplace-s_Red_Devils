# Problem 19.47

## Question
(II) A circuit contains a single 250-pF capacitor hooked across a battery. It is desired to store four times as much energy in a combination of two capacitors by adding a single capacitor to this one. How would you hook it up, and what would its value be?

## Final answer from source
In parallel, 750 pF.

## Worked solution
For a capacitor connected across the same battery voltage \(V\), the stored energy is

\[
U=\frac{1}{2}CV^2
\]

Since the battery voltage stays the same, storing four times as much energy means the equivalent capacitance must be four times larger:

\[
U' = 4U \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2}C_{\text{eq}}V^2 = 4\left(\frac{1}{2}C_1V^2\right)
\]

So,

\[
C_{\text{eq}} = 4C_1 = 4(250\ \text{pF}) = 1000\ \text{pF}
\]

We already have one capacitor of \(250\ \text{pF}\), so the added capacitor must make the total equivalent capacitance \(1000\ \text{pF}\).

For capacitors in parallel,

\[
C_{\text{eq}} = C_1 + C_2
\]

Thus,

\[
1000\ \text{pF} = 250\ \text{pF} + C_2
\]

\[
C_2 = 750\ \text{pF}
\]

So the added capacitor should be connected in parallel with the 250-pF capacitor, and its capacitance should be \(750\ \text{pF}\).

## Key concepts used
- Energy stored in a capacitor: \(U=\tfrac{1}{2}CV^2\)
- With fixed battery voltage, stored energy is proportional to capacitance
- Capacitors in parallel add directly: \(C_{\text{eq}}=C_1+C_2\)
