# Problem 20.1

## Question
(I) (a) What is the force per meter of length on a straight wire carrying a 6.40-A current when perpendicular to a 0.90-T uniform magnetic field? (b) What if the angle between the wire and field is 35.0°?

## Final answer from source
(a) (b)

## Worked solution
The magnetic force on a current-carrying straight wire is

\[
F = I L B \sin\theta
\]

where:
- \(I\) is the current,
- \(L\) is the length of wire in the field,
- \(B\) is the magnetic field strength,
- \(\theta\) is the angle between the wire (current direction) and the magnetic field.

We want the force **per meter of length**, so divide by \(L\):

\[
\frac{F}{L} = I B \sin\theta
\]

Given:
- \(I = 6.40\ \text{A}\)
- \(B = 0.90\ \text{T}\)

### (a) Wire perpendicular to the field
Perpendicular means \(\theta = 90^\circ\), so \(\sin 90^\circ = 1\).

\[
\frac{F}{L} = (6.40)(0.90)(1) = 5.76\ \text{N/m}
\]

To match the source style, this is reported as:

\[
\boxed{5.8\ \text{N/m}}
\]

### (b) Wire at \(35.0^\circ\) to the field
Now \(\theta = 35.0^\circ\), so

\[
\frac{F}{L} = (6.40)(0.90)\sin 35.0^\circ
\]

\[
\frac{F}{L} = 5.76(0.574) \approx 3.31\ \text{N/m}
\]

Rounded appropriately:

\[
\boxed{3.3\ \text{N/m}}
\]

## Key concepts used
- Magnetic force on a current-carrying wire: \(F = I L B \sin\theta\)
- Force per unit length: \(\frac{F}{L} = I B \sin\theta\)
- Perpendicular orientation gives maximum force (\(\sin 90^\circ = 1\))
- Use of trigonometric factor for nonperpendicular angle
