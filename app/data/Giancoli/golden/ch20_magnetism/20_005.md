# Problem 20.5

## Question
(I) The magnetic force per meter on a wire is measured to be only 45% of its maximum possible value. What is the angle between the wire and the magnetic field?

## Final answer from source
27°.

## Worked solution
The magnetic force on a straight current-carrying wire in a magnetic field is

\[
F = I L B \sin\theta
\]

where:
- \(I\) is the current,
- \(L\) is the wire length,
- \(B\) is the magnetic field strength,
- \(\theta\) is the angle between the wire and the magnetic field.

The maximum possible force occurs when the wire is perpendicular to the field, so \(\sin\theta = 1\). Thus,

\[
F_{\max} = I L B
\]

We are told the force per meter is \(45\%\) of the maximum possible value, so

\[
\frac{F}{F_{\max}} = 0.45 = \sin\theta
\]

Now solve for the angle:

\[
\theta = \sin^{-1}(0.45)
\]

\[
\theta \approx 26.7^\circ
\]

Rounding to the nearest degree:

\[
\boxed{27^\circ}
\]

## Key concepts used
- Magnetic force on a current-carrying wire: \(F = I L B \sin\theta\)
- Maximum force occurs at \(\theta = 90^\circ\)
- Ratio of actual force to maximum force gives \(\sin\theta\)
- Inverse sine to find the angle
