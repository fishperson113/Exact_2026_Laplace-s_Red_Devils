# Problem 20.9

## Question
A proton enters a uniform magnetic field that is perpendicular to the proton’s velocity (Fig. 20-51). What happens to the kinetic energy of the proton? (a) It increases. (b) It decreases. (c) It stays the same. (d) It depends on the velocity direction. (e) It depends on the B field direction. FIGURE 20-51 MisConceptual Question 9. (I) Determine the magnitude and direction of the force on 7.75 * 105 m(cid:2)s an electron traveling horizontally to the east in a vertically upward magnetic field of strength 0.45 T.

## Final answer from source
north.

## Worked solution
The source text appears to include two separate items: a conceptual question about a proton in a magnetic field, and an additional force-direction question for an electron. The provided final answer, “north,” clearly corresponds to the electron-force question, not the proton kinetic-energy question. Because the figure and full wording may be incomplete or merged by OCR, this requires review.

For the proton question:
- The magnetic force on a charge is $ \vec F = q\,\vec v \times \vec B  $.
- Since $ \vec B \perp \vec v  $, the force is always perpendicular to the proton’s velocity.
- A force perpendicular to velocity does no work:
  $$ 
  W = \vec F \cdot \vec d = 0
   $$
  so the proton’s speed does not change.
- Therefore its kinetic energy stays constant.

So the answer to the conceptual question is:
- **(c) It stays the same.**

For the electron-force question implied by the source answer:
- The electron travels east, and the magnetic field is vertically upward.
- For a positive charge, $ \vec v \times \vec B  $ would point one way; for an electron (negative charge), the force is opposite.
- Applying the right-hand rule gives the force direction as **north**, matching the source answer.

## Key concepts used
- Magnetic force on a moving charge: $ \vec F = q\,\vec v \times \vec B  $
- Magnetic force is perpendicular to velocity
- Perpendicular force does no work
- Constant speed implies constant kinetic energy
- Right-hand rule and sign reversal for negative charge
