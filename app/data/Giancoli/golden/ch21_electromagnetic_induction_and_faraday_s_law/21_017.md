## Worked solution
The OCR of the problem statement is incomplete and garbled, so the exact resistances and possibly other details are missing. Because of that, only part of the source answer can be checked reliably from the visible data. The source answer explicitly gives:

- (a) induced emf = 0.17 V
- (c) external force is to the right

The missing part for (b) is not recoverable from the provided OCR, so the numerical current cannot be derived with certainty from the statement alone.

Still, the standard physics for a rod moving on rails in a uniform magnetic field is:

1. **Induced emf**
   \[
   \mathcal{E}=BLv
   \]
   where
   - \(B=0.35\ \text{T}\),
   - \(L=0.300\ \text{m}\) is the rail separation,
   - \(v=1.6\ \text{m/s}\).

   So,
   \[
   \mathcal{E}=(0.35)(0.300)(1.6)=0.168\ \text{V}\approx 0.17\ \text{V}
   \]
   which matches the source.

2. **Current**
   The current would be
   \[
   I=\frac{\mathcal{E}}{R_{\text{total}}}
   \]
   where \(R_{\text{total}}\) is the total resistance of the circuit. The OCR does not clearly preserve the resistances, so the current cannot be computed reliably here without reviewing the figure or a cleaner scan.

3. **External force direction**
   By Lenz’s law, the magnetic force on the moving rod opposes the motion. Therefore, to keep the rod moving at constant speed, the applied external force must be **to the right**, matching the source answer.

If the resistances from the original figure were available, the current and force magnitude could be found from
\[
F_{\text{ext}}=F_B=ILB
\]
with direction to the right.

## Key concepts used
- Motional emf: \(\mathcal{E}=BLv\)
- Ohm’s law for the circuit current: \(I=\mathcal{E}/R_{\text{total}}\)
- Magnetic force on a current-carrying rod: \(F=ILB\)
- Lenz’s law for direction of induced current and opposing force
- Constant-velocity condition: external force balances magnetic drag
