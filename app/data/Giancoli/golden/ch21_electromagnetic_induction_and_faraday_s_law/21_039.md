# Problem 21.39

## Question
(I) If the current in a 160-mH coil changes steadily from 25.0 A to 10.0 A in 350 ms, what is the magnitude of the induced emf?

## Final answer from source
6.9 V.

## Worked solution
For an inductor, the magnitude of the induced emf is

\[
|\mathcal{E}| = L\left|\frac{\Delta I}{\Delta t}\right|
\]

Given:
- Inductance: \(\,L = 160\,\text{mH} = 0.160\,\text{H}\)
- Initial current: \(\,I_i = 25.0\,\text{A}\)
- Final current: \(\,I_f = 10.0\,\text{A}\)
- Time interval: \(\,\Delta t = 350\,\text{ms} = 0.350\,\text{s}\)

Compute the change in current:

\[
\Delta I = I_f - I_i = 10.0 - 25.0 = -15.0\,\text{A}
\]

Its magnitude is:

\[
|\Delta I| = 15.0\,\text{A}
\]

Now calculate the induced emf magnitude:

\[
|\mathcal{E}| = 0.160 \times \frac{15.0}{0.350}
\]

\[
|\mathcal{E}| = 0.160 \times 42.857 \approx 6.86\,\text{V}
\]

Rounded to two significant figures:

\[
\boxed{6.9\ \text{V}}
\]

## Key concepts used
- Self-induction in an inductor
- Induced emf formula: \(|\mathcal{E}| = L\left|\Delta I/\Delta t\right|\)
- Unit conversion: mH to H, ms to s
- Magnitude of a decreasing current change
