# Problem 21.51

## Question
(II) How many time constants does it take for the potential difference across the resistor in an LR circuit like that in Fig. 21-37 to drop to 2.5% of its original value, after the switch is moved to the upper position, removing from the circuit?

## Final answer from source
3.7.

## Worked solution
In an LR circuit, after the current source is removed, the current decays exponentially:

$$ 
I(t)=I_0 e^{-t/\tau}
 $$

where the time constant is

$$ 
\tau=\frac{L}{R}.
 $$

The potential difference across the resistor is

$$ 
V_R(t)=IR = I_0R\, e^{-t/\tau},
 $$

so it also decays with the same exponential form:

$$ 
\frac{V_R(t)}{V_{R,0}}=e^{-t/\tau}.
 $$

We are told the resistor voltage drops to $2.5\% $ of its original value:

$$ 
\frac{V_R(t)}{V_{R,0}}=0.025.
 $$

Thus,

$$ 
0.025=e^{-t/\tau}.
 $$

Take the natural logarithm of both sides:

$$ 
\ln(0.025)=-\frac{t}{\tau}.
 $$

So

$$ 
\frac{t}{\tau}=-\ln(0.025).
 $$

Now evaluate:

$$ 
-\ln(0.025)=\ln\!\left(\frac{1}{0.025}\right)=\ln(40)\approx 3.69.
 $$

Therefore, the time required is

$$ 
\boxed{3.7 \text{ time constants}}
 $$

which matches the source answer.

## Key concepts used
- Exponential decay in an LR circuit
- Time constant $\tau=L/R $
- Resistor voltage follows the same decay as current
- Solving $e^{-t/\tau}=0.025 $ using logarithms
