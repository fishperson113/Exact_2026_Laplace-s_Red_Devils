# Problem 21.65

## Question
(II) An ac voltage source is connected in series with a 1.0-mF 650-(cid:3) resistor. Using a digital ac voltmeter, the amplitude of the voltage source is measured to be 4.0 V rms, while the voltages across the resistor and across the capacitor are found to be 3.0 V rms and 2.7 V rms, respectively. Determine the frequency of the ac voltage source. Why is the voltage measured across the voltage source not equal to the sum of the voltages measured across the resistor and across the capacitor?

## Final answer from source
270 Hz; the voltages are out of phase.

## Worked solution
For a series \(RC\) circuit, the rms voltage across the resistor, capacitor, and source satisfy vector addition:

\[
V_{\text{source}}^2 = V_R^2 + V_C^2
\]

because \(V_R\) is in phase with the current, while \(V_C\) lags the current by \(90^\circ\), so the two voltages are perpendicular phasors.

### 1) Find the capacitor reactance
Given:

- \(V_R = 3.0\ \text{V rms}\)
- \(V_C = 2.7\ \text{V rms}\)
- \(R = 650\ \Omega\)
- \(C = 1.0\ \mu\text{F}\)  \(\) 

From the resistor relation,

\[
V_R = I R \quad \Rightarrow \quad I = \frac{V_R}{R}
\]

\[
I = \frac{3.0}{650} = 4.62\times 10^{-3}\ \text{A}
\]

For the capacitor,

\[
V_C = I X_C
\]

so

\[
X_C = \frac{V_C}{I} = \frac{2.7}{4.62\times 10^{-3}} \approx 5.84\times 10^2\ \Omega
\]

### 2) Use the capacitive reactance formula
For a capacitor,

\[
X_C = \frac{1}{2\pi f C}
\]

Solve for \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{1}{2\pi C X_C}
\]

Substitute \(C = 1.0\times 10^{-6}\ \text{F}\) and \(X_C \approx 5.84\times 10^2\ \Omega\):

\[
f = \frac{1}{2\pi (1.0\times 10^{-6})(5.84\times 10^2)}
\approx 2.7\times 10^2\ \text{Hz}
\]

\[
\boxed{f \approx 270\ \text{Hz}}
\]

### 3) Why the source voltage is not the sum of \(V_R\) and \(V_C\)
The resistor voltage and capacitor voltage are not in phase:

- \(V_R\) is in phase with the current.
- \(V_C\) is \(90^\circ\) out of phase with the current.

Therefore they must be added as phasors, not as ordinary scalar voltages. That is why the source voltage is **not** equal to \(V_R + V_C\).

## Key concepts used
- Series \(RC\) circuit current is the same through all components
- RMS voltages in AC circuits
- Ohm’s law for AC: \(V_R = IR\), \(V_C = IX_C\)
- Capacitive reactance: \(X_C = \dfrac{1}{2\pi f C}\)
- Phasor addition of voltages
- Voltage across \(R\) and \(C\) are out of phase
