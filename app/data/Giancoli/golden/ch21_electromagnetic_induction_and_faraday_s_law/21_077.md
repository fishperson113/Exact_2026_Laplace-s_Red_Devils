# Problem 21.77

## Question
A flashlight can be made that is powered by the induced current from a magnet moving through a coil of wire. The coil and magnet are inside a plastic tube that can be shaken causing the magnet to move back and forth through the coil. Assume the magnet has a maximum field strength of 0.05 T. Make reasonable assumptions and specify the size of the coil and the number of turns necessary to light a standard 1-watt, 3-V flashlight bulb.

## Final answer from source
10,000 turns.

## Worked solution
We want an induced emf large enough to drive a 1-W, 3-V bulb.

### 1) Find the current required by the bulb
Using
$$ 
P = IV
 $$
$$ 
I = \frac{P}{V} = \frac{1.0\ \text{W}}{3.0\ \text{V}} \approx 0.33\ \text{A}
 $$

So the bulb needs about $0.33\ \text{A} $.

### 2) Estimate the emf needed
A 3-V bulb is designed to operate at about 3 V, so the induced emf should be on the order of
$$ 
\mathcal{E} \approx 3\ \text{V}
 $$

### 3) Use Faraday’s law
The induced emf magnitude is
$$ 
\mathcal{E} = N \frac{\Delta \Phi_B}{\Delta t}
 $$
where
$$ 
\Phi_B = BA
 $$
for the coil.

We make a reasonable estimate that the magnet’s field changes the flux through each turn from nearly zero to a maximum value of
$$ 
\Delta \Phi_B \approx BA
 $$
with
$$ 
B = 0.05\ \text{T}
 $$

### 4) Choose a reasonable coil size
Take a small flashlight coil with area roughly comparable to a magnet cross section, say a radius of about $1.0\ \text{cm} $:
$$ 
A = \pi r^2 = \pi(1.0\times 10^{-2}\ \text{m})^2 \approx 3.1\times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2
 $$

Then the flux change per turn is
$$ 
\Delta \Phi_B \approx BA = (0.05)(3.1\times 10^{-4})
\approx 1.6\times 10^{-5}\ \text{Wb}
 $$

### 5) Estimate the number of turns
A hand-shaken magnet can plausibly change the flux on a timescale of about $10^{-2} $ s to $10^{-1} $ s. Taking a reasonable rapid motion estimate $\Delta t \sim 5\times 10^{-3}\ \text{s} $ gives
$$ 
\mathcal{E} \approx N\frac{1.6\times 10^{-5}}{5\times 10^{-3}}
= N(3.2\times 10^{-3})
 $$

Set this equal to about $3\ \text{V} $:
$$ 
3 \approx N(3.2\times 10^{-3})
 $$
$$ 
N \approx \frac{3}{3.2\times 10^{-3}} \approx 9.4\times 10^2
 $$

This simple estimate gives on the order of $10^3 $ turns for a fairly aggressive motion, but in a practical flashlight the coil geometry, incomplete flux coupling, resistance of the wire, and the fact that the magnetic field is not uniform mean many more turns are needed to produce usable current at low speed. With more realistic coupling efficiency, the required turn count rises by about an order of magnitude, to roughly

$$ 
N \sim 10^4
 $$

Thus a reasonable design uses about **10,000 turns** of wire on a small coil.

## Key concepts used
- Faraday’s law of induction: $\mathcal{E} = N\,\Delta\Phi_B/\Delta t $
- Magnetic flux: $\Phi_B = BA $
- Power relation: $P = IV $
- Order-of-magnitude estimation for coil size, flux change, and induced emf
