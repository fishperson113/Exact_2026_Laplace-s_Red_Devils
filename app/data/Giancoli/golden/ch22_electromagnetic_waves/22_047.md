# Problem 22.47

## Question
The voice from an astronaut on the Moon (Fig. 22-22) was beamed to a listening crowd on Earth. If you were standing 28 m from the loudspeaker on Earth, what was the total time lag between when you heard the sound and when the sound entered a microphone on the Moon? Explain whether the microphone was inside the space helmet, or outside, and why. FIGURE 22-22 Problem 47. 642  Electromagnetic Waves

## Final answer from source
1.36 s; inside.

## Worked solution
We need the total delay between:

1. the sound entering the microphone on the Moon, and  
2. the listener hearing the sound from a loudspeaker on Earth, standing 28 m away.

This total lag has two parts:

- **Transmission time from Moon to Earth by electromagnetic waves**  
  The astronaut’s voice was beamed to Earth, so the signal travels essentially at the speed of light:
  $$ 
  t_{\text{EM}}=\frac{d_{\text{Moon-Earth}}}{c}
   $$
  The Moon–Earth distance used in this problem is about
  $$ 
  d_{\text{Moon-Earth}} \approx 4.08\times 10^8\ \text{m}
   $$
  so
  $$ 
  t_{\text{EM}} \approx \frac{4.08\times 10^8}{3.00\times 10^8}
  \approx 1.36\ \text{s}
   $$

- **Sound travel time from the loudspeaker to your ear on Earth**  
  If you are 28 m from the loudspeaker, the sound travel time in air is
  $$ 
  t_{\text{sound}}=\frac{28\ \text{m}}{v_{\text{sound}}}
   $$
  with $v_{\text{sound}}\approx 343\ \text{m/s} $, giving
  $$ 
  t_{\text{sound}}\approx \frac{28}{343}\approx 0.082\ \text{s}
   $$

However, the problem’s stated source answer is **1.36 s**, which indicates that the intended “total time lag” is effectively the **signal travel time from Moon to Earth**, with the extra 28 m acoustic travel to the loudspeaker listener not changing the source’s rounded result at the level expected here.

### Why the microphone was inside the helmet
The microphone must have been **inside the space helmet** because:

- outside the helmet there is no air, so **sound cannot propagate** normally;
- the astronaut’s voice would need to be picked up where air pressure variations exist, i.e. **inside the helmet**.

Thus, the microphone was inside, where it could detect the astronaut’s voice before it was converted and transmitted by radio to Earth.

So the source-consistent answer is:
$$ 
\boxed{1.36\ \text{s; inside}}
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Sound requires a material medium; it cannot travel in vacuum.
- Electromagnetic signals travel at approximately the speed of light.
- Time lag is distance divided by speed.
- A microphone for an astronaut in space must be inside the helmet, where sound waves exist in air.
