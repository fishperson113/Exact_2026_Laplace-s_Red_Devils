# 5.1 Friction

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 5, Section 5.1

## Concept explanation
Friction is a force that opposes relative motion between surfaces in contact. It acts parallel to the contact surface and always in a direction that opposes motion or attempted motion of the objects relative to each other.

There are two main types of friction:
- **Static friction** acts when the surfaces are not moving relative to each other. It adjusts to match the applied force up to a maximum value.
- **Kinetic friction** acts when the surfaces are sliding relative to each other.

Static friction is usually greater than kinetic friction. That is why it is often harder to start an object moving than to keep it moving once it has started.

The size of friction depends on:
- the nature of the surfaces in contact,
- the normal force pressing the surfaces together.

For many situations, friction is approximately independent of speed at small but nonzero speeds. Lubrication reduces friction by reducing the effective interaction between surfaces. Even very smooth surfaces are not friction-free because adhesive forces between molecules contribute to friction.

The coefficient of friction is a unitless quantity, usually between 0 and 1.0, and depends on the pair of surfaces in contact.

## Key formulas
When there is no motion between the objects, the magnitude of static friction is

\[
f_s \le \mu_s N
\]

The maximum static friction is

\[
f_{s,\max} = \mu_s N
\]

Once the applied force exceeds the maximum static friction, the object moves.

Once an object is moving, the magnitude of kinetic friction is

\[
f_k = \mu_k N
\]

For an object sliding down an incline at constant velocity, the kinetic friction can be found by using the balance between the component of weight down the slope and friction:

\[
f_k = mg\sin\theta
\]

and since

\[
f_k = \mu_k N
\]

with

\[
N = mg\cos\theta
\]

then

\[
\mu_k = \tan\theta
\]

## Variables and units
- \(f_s\): static friction force, in newtons (N)
- \(f_{s,\max}\): maximum static friction force, in newtons (N)
- \(f_k\): kinetic friction force, in newtons (N)
- \(\mu_s\): coefficient of static friction, unitless
- \(\mu_k\): coefficient of kinetic friction, unitless
- \(N\): normal force, in newtons (N)
- \(m\): mass, in kilograms (kg)
- \(g\): acceleration due to gravity, in meters per second squared (m/s\(^2\))
- \(\theta\): incline angle relative to the horizontal, in radians or degrees

## Worked example
A skier with a mass of 62 kg is sliding down a snowy slope. Find the coefficient of kinetic friction for the skier if friction is known to be 45.0 N.

**Strategy:**  
Use the relation \(f_k = \mu_k N\). On a slope, the normal force is the component of weight perpendicular to the slope. Since there is no motion perpendicular to the surface,

\[
N = mg\cos\theta
\]

In the source example, this leads directly to solving for \(\mu_k\) from the known friction force and normal force.

**Solution:**  
\[
\mu_k = \frac{f_k}{N}
\]

Substituting the given values as shown in the source example gives

\[
\mu_k \approx 0.06
\]

**Discussion:**  
This value is slightly smaller than the coefficient listed in the table for waxed wood on snow, but it is still reasonable because friction coefficients can vary greatly.

## Key concepts used
- Friction opposes relative motion or attempted motion.
- Friction acts parallel to the contact surface.
- Static friction adjusts up to a maximum value.
- Kinetic friction applies when surfaces slide.
- Static friction is usually greater than kinetic friction.
- Friction depends on the normal force and the materials in contact.
- The coefficients of friction are unitless.
- Lubrication reduces friction.
- On an incline, the normal force is the component of weight perpendicular to the surface.
- For constant velocity on an incline, the downhill component of weight equals kinetic friction.
