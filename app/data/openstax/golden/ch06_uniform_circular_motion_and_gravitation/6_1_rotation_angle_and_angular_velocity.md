# 6.1 Rotation Angle and Angular Velocity

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 6, Section 6.1

## Concept explanation
When an object rotates about an axis, each point on the object follows a circular arc. The **rotation angle** describes how much rotation has occurred and is analogous to linear distance.

The rotation angle is defined by the ratio of arc length to radius of curvature:

\[
\theta = \frac{s}{r}
\]

where:
- \(s\) is the arc length, the distance traveled along the circular path
- \(r\) is the radius of curvature of the path

For one complete revolution, the arc length is the circumference of the circle:

\[
s = 2\pi r
\]

so the rotation angle for one revolution is:

\[
\theta = \frac{2\pi r}{r} = 2\pi \text{ rad}
\]

This result defines the radian. One full revolution corresponds to:

\[
2\pi \text{ rad} = 360^\circ
\]

Angular velocity describes how fast an object rotates. It is the rate of change of angular position with time:

\[
\omega = \frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t}
\]

The units of angular velocity are radians per second, written rad/s.

Angular velocity is related to linear velocity by:

\[
v = r\omega
\]

This means:
- points farther from the axis of rotation move faster in linear speed for the same angular velocity
- for a rotating car tire, the linear speed of a point on the rim equals the car’s speed if the tire rolls without slipping
- a larger tire radius gives a larger linear speed for the same angular velocity, or a smaller angular velocity for the same linear speed

Linear velocity is tangent to the circular path, while angular velocity has clockwise or counterclockwise direction relative to the axis of rotation.

## Key formulas
\[
\theta = \frac{s}{r}
\]

\[
s = r\theta
\]

\[
\theta = 2\pi \text{ rad} \quad \text{for one revolution}
\]

\[
2\pi \text{ rad} = 360^\circ
\]

\[
\omega = \frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t}
\]

\[
v = r\omega
\]

\[
\omega = \frac{v}{r}
\]

## Variables and units
- \(\theta\): rotation angle, measured in radians (rad)
- \(s\): arc length along the circular path, measured in meters (m)
- \(r\): radius of curvature or radius of the circle, measured in meters (m)
- \(\omega\): angular velocity, measured in radians per second (rad/s)
- \(v\): linear velocity or tangential speed, measured in meters per second (m/s)
- \(\Delta \theta\): change in angle, measured in radians (rad)
- \(\Delta t\): time interval, measured in seconds (s)

Notes:
- Radians are defined as a ratio of lengths and are treated as unitless in dimensional calculations, though written as rad.
- Linear velocity is tangent to the circular path.

## Worked example
**How Fast Does a Car Tire Spin?**

Calculate the angular velocity of a car tire of radius \(0.300\ \text{m}\) when the car travels at \(15.0\ \text{m/s}\).

**Solution**

Use the relation:

\[
\omega = \frac{v}{r}
\]

Substitute the known values:

\[
\omega = \frac{15.0\ \text{m/s}}{0.300\ \text{m}} = 50.0\ \text{s}^{-1}
\]

Since angular velocity is measured in radians per second:

\[
\omega = 50.0\ \text{rad/s}
\]

If the tire radius were larger, such as \(1.20\ \text{m}\), and the car moved at the same speed, the tires would rotate more slowly.
