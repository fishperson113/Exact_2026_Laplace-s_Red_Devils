# 6.2 Centripetal Acceleration

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 6, Section 6.2

## Concept explanation
In uniform circular motion, the velocity changes direction continuously even if its magnitude stays constant. Because acceleration is any change in velocity, there is always an acceleration associated with uniform circular motion.

This acceleration points directly toward the center of the circular path, so it is called centripetal acceleration. Centripetal means “toward the center” or “center seeking.”

The magnitude of centripetal acceleration can be found from the geometry of the velocity change in circular motion. Using similar triangles, the result is

- \(a_c = \dfrac{v^2}{r}\)

where \(v\) is the linear or tangential speed and \(r\) is the radius of the circle.

Because \(v = r\omega\), the acceleration can also be written as

- \(a_c = r\omega^2\)

Centripetal acceleration increases with the square of speed and decreases with increasing radius. It is larger for faster motion and tighter curves.

A centrifuge is a rotating device used to separate specimens of different densities. Large centripetal acceleration reduces the time needed for separation and can make separation possible with small samples. Centrifuges are used in science and medicine, including separation of bacteria, viruses, blood cells, DNA, and protein from a solution or liquid medium. They are often rated by centripetal acceleration relative to Earth’s gravity.

## Key formulas
- \(a_c = \dfrac{v^2}{r}\)
- \(a_c = r\omega^2\)
- \(v = r\omega\)

## Variables and units
- \(a_c\): centripetal acceleration, units of \(\text{m/s}^2\)
- \(v\): linear or tangential speed, units of \(\text{m/s}\)
- \(r\): radius of the circular path, units of \(\text{m}\)
- \(\omega\): angular velocity, units of \(\text{rad/s}\)
- \(g\): acceleration due to gravity, used for comparison with centripetal acceleration

Direction:
- Centripetal acceleration points toward the center of curvature.

## Worked example
Example 6.2: How Does the Centripetal Acceleration of a Car Around a Curve Compare with That Due to Gravity?

Given:
- Radius \(r = 500\ \text{m}\)
- Speed \(v = 25.0\ \text{m/s}\)

Use \(a_c = \dfrac{v^2}{r}\):

- \(a_c = \dfrac{(25.0\ \text{m/s})^2}{500\ \text{m}} = 1.25\ \text{m/s}^2\)

Comparison with gravity:
- \(a_c/g = 1.25/9.80 \approx 0.128\)

So the centripetal acceleration is about \(0.128g\), which is noticeable, especially without a seat belt.
