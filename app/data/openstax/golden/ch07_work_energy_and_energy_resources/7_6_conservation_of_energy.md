# 7.6 Conservation of Energy

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 7, Section 7.6

## Concept explanation
Energy is conserved, making it one of the most important physical quantities in nature. The law of conservation of energy states:

Total energy is constant in any process. It may change in form or be transferred from one system to another, but the total remains the same.

Energy appears in many forms, not only mechanical energy. In this section, the text groups all non-mechanical forms into a single category called other energy. Mechanical energy can include kinetic energy and potential energy, while other energy accounts for forms not treated as mechanical potential energy.

The conservation of energy principle is useful because many situations are best understood in terms of energy, and many problems are most easily solved by considering energy rather than forces alone.

The section also lists several forms of energy:
- Electrical energy
- Chemical energy
- Radiant energy, or electromagnetic radiation, including visible light, infrared, and ultraviolet radiation
- Nuclear energy
- Thermal energy, related to the random motion of atoms and molecules and to temperature

Energy transformation examples include:
- Food chemical energy converted to kinetic, potential, and thermal energy in the human body
- Sunlight converted to electrical energy by solar cells
- Chemical energy in coal converted to thermal energy, then to mechanical energy in a turbine, and finally to electrical energy in a generator

Efficiency is defined for an energy conversion process as the fraction of input energy or work that becomes useful output, rather than being transformed into less useful forms such as thermal energy.

## Key formulas
Total energy conservation, general form:
$$ 
\text{initial energy} = \text{final energy}
 $$
as represented in the text by the general conservation of energy equation.

Conservation of mechanical energy, when all forces are conservative:
$$ 
K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f
 $$

General conservation of energy, when other forms of energy and/or nonconservative work are included:
$$ 
K_i + U_i + W_{\text{nc}} = K_f + U_f + E_{\text{other}}
 $$
with the understanding that the section states all types of energy and work can be included in this very general statement.

Efficiency:
$$ 
\text{efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful output energy or work}}{\text{energy input}}
 $$

## Variables and units
- $K $: kinetic energy, in joules (J)
- $U $: potential energy, in joules (J)
- $W_{\text{nc}} $: work done by nonconservative forces, in joules (J)
- $E_{\text{other}} $: other energy, in joules (J)
- efficiency: dimensionless fraction, often reported as a percentage (%)
- energy: joules (J)

The text emphasizes keeping track of whether energy terms are mechanical, nonmechanical, or transferred by nonconservative work, and checking that signs and units are consistent.
