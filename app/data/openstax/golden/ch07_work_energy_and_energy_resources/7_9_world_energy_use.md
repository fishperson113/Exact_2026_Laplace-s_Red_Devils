# 7.9 World Energy Use

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 7, Section 7.9

## Concept explanation
Energy is an important ingredient in all phases of society. Adequate and reliable energy resources are crucial for economic growth and for maintaining quality of life, but current levels of energy consumption and production are not sustainable.

The principal energy resources used in the world are dominated by oil, although natural gas and solar contributions are increasing. Renewable forms of energy are those sources that cannot be used up, such as water, wind, solar, and biomass. Nonrenewable energy sources include fossil fuels such as oil, natural gas, and coal.

About 85% of world energy comes from nonrenewable fossil fuels. The possible link between global warming and fossil fuel use, through carbon dioxide produced by combustion, has made a shift to non-fossil fuels important, though not easy.

World energy consumption continues to rise, especially in developing countries. Global demand for energy has tripled in the past 50 years and might triple again in the next 30 years. Much of this growth is expected to come from China and India, while many developed countries are expanding renewable energy use.

Examples from the source section include:
- Germany plans to meet 65% of its power and 30% of its overall energy needs with renewable resources by 2030.
- China surpassed Japan as the world’s second largest consumer of oil in 2003 and surpassed the United States as the largest generator of electricity in 2009.
- In India, the main energy resources are biomass and coal, and about 70% of electricity is generated by coal.
- New Zealand gets about two-thirds of its electricity demand from hydroelectric power.
- The United States obtains only about 10% of its energy from renewable sources, mostly hydroelectric power.

Economic well-being is dependent upon energy use, and in most countries higher GDP per capita is matched by higher energy consumption per capita. Increased efficiency of energy use can change this dependency. Energy resource development must be balanced against environmental effects. Renewable energy can also improve economic opportunity and stability, and it can increase resiliency during natural disasters, conflict, or other disruptions.

The conservation of energy and energy conservation are different ideas. The law of conservation of energy states that the total energy in an isolated system remains constant. Energy conservation is the philosophy of decreasing energy use by reduced activities and/or improved efficiency. Even though energy is conserved in the physical sense, practical energy use matters because energy transformations usually end as waste heat and other less useful forms, so the ability to do useful work is degraded.

## Key formulas
- Law of conservation of energy: total energy in an isolated system remains constant.
- Efficiency:
  $$ 
  \text{efficiency}=\frac{\text{useful work output}}{\text{energy consumed}}
   $$
- Energy consumption units in the source table include exajoules:
  $$ 
  1\ \text{EJ}=10^{18}\ \text{J}
   $$

## Variables and units
- Energy: joule (J)
- Exajoule: EJ $=10^{18}\,\text{J} $
- GDP per capita: gross domestic product per person
- Power: watt (W)
- Kilowatt-hour: energy unit used especially for electrical energy
- Renewable sources: water, wind, solar, biomass
- Nonrenewable fossil fuels: oil, natural gas, coal

Selected quantitative facts from the section:
- About 31–35% of the world’s energy comes from oil.
- The United States, with 4.25% of the world’s population, consumes 21% of the world’s oil production per year.
- About 85% of energy comes from nonrenewable fossil fuels.
- About 10% of U.S. electricity is generated by renewable resources, primarily hydroelectric power.
- In the table for 2020 commercial energy mix, the world total consumption is 557.1 EJ.
