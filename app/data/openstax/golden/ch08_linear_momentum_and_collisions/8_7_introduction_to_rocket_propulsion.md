# 8.7 Introduction to Rocket Propulsion

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 8, Section 8.7

## Concept explanation
Newton’s third law of motion states that to every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Rocket propulsion, jet engines, deflating balloons, squids, octopuses, and even gun recoil are explained by the same principle: matter is forcefully ejected from a system, producing an equal and opposite reaction on what remains.

For a rocket accelerating straight up, the rocket plus expelled gas is a system. As gas is ejected downward, the remaining rocket gains upward velocity. The center of mass of the whole system is in free fall, but part of the system can accelerate upward by rapidly expelling mass.

A common misconception is that rocket exhaust pushes on the ground. Instead, thrust is the force exerted on the rocket by the exhaust gases. A rocket’s thrust is greater in outer space than in the atmosphere or on the launch pad, because gases are easier to expel into a vacuum.

A rocket’s acceleration depends on three major factors:
1. Greater exhaust velocity of the gases relative to the rocket gives greater acceleration.
2. Faster fuel burn rate gives greater acceleration.
3. Smaller rocket mass gives greater acceleration.

The quantity of thrust, with units of newtons, is the rate at which mass is ejected times the exhaust velocity. As fuel is burned, rocket mass decreases, so acceleration increases continuously and reaches a maximum just before fuel is exhausted.

To achieve very high speeds, a rocket must have as small a nonfuel mass as possible. Multistage rockets solve this by discarding each stage after its fuel is burned, so later stages carry less mass and more favorable payload-to-fuel ratios. The space shuttle used reusable parts such as solid fuel boosters and the orbiter itself, while the large liquid fuel tank was expended. Rockets with satellites can also be launched from airplanes, which gives the rocket an initial speed above zero and helps it avoid much of the atmosphere’s resistance.

## Key formulas
Acceleration of a rocket:
\[
a=\frac{v_{\text{exhaust}}}{m}\frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t}-g
\]

Final velocity of a one-stage rocket initially at rest, neglecting air resistance and gravity:
\[
\Delta v=v_{\text{exhaust}}\ln\!\left(\frac{m_i}{m_f}\right)
\]

Where:
- \(a\) is the rocket’s acceleration
- \(v_{\text{exhaust}}\) is the exhaust velocity relative to the rocket
- \(m\) is the mass of the rocket
- \(\Delta m\) is the mass of gas ejected
- \(\Delta t\) is the time over which the gas is ejected
- \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity
- \(m_i\) is the initial mass of the rocket
- \(m_f\) is the final mass of the rocket after fuel is exhausted

For a rocket starting from rest, \(\Delta v\) equals the final velocity.

## Variables and units
- \(a\): rocket acceleration, SI unit \( \text{m/s}^2 \)
- \(v_{\text{exhaust}}\): exhaust velocity relative to the rocket, SI unit \( \text{m/s} \)
- \(m\): rocket mass, SI unit \( \text{kg} \)
- \(\Delta m\): ejected gas mass, SI unit \( \text{kg} \)
- \(\Delta t\): ejection time, SI unit \( \text{s} \)
- \(g\): gravitational acceleration, SI unit \( \text{m/s}^2 \)
- Thrust: force exerted on the rocket by exhaust gases, SI unit newton (\(\text{N}\))
- \(\Delta v\): change in velocity, SI unit \( \text{m/s} \)
- \(m_i/m_f\): initial-to-final mass ratio, dimensionless
- \(\ln\): natural logarithm, dimensionless

## Worked example
Example 8.8: Calculating Acceleration: Initial Acceleration of a Moon Launch

A Saturn V’s mass at liftoff was given, its fuel-burn rate was given, and the exhaust velocity was given. The problem asks for the initial acceleration.

Using the rocket acceleration expression,
\[
a=\frac{v_{\text{exhaust}}}{m}\frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t}-g
\]
the given values are substituted to obtain the initial acceleration.

The section notes that this initial acceleration is fairly small, even though it increases steadily as fuel burns because \(m\) decreases while the other terms remain constant. From this acceleration and the mass of the rocket, the thrust of the engines was found.

The section also gives an example of the velocity needed to escape Earth’s gravity. Neglecting air resistance and gravity, the final velocity of a one-stage rocket initially at rest is
\[
\Delta v=v_{\text{exhaust}}\ln\!\left(\frac{m_i}{m_f}\right)
\]
Using Earth escape speed and an exhaust velocity, the result shows that only a small fraction of the initial mass can remain after the fuel is burned; the section states that about 0.56% of the mass is left, meaning 98.9% of the rocket is fuel and 1.10% is payload, engines, fuel tanks, and other components. The section concludes that multistage rockets are the practical solution.
