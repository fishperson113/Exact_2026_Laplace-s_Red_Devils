# 9.1 The First Condition for Equilibrium

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 9, Section 9.1

## Concept explanation
The first condition required for equilibrium is that the net external force on the system must be zero.

This means that if the net force is zero, then the net external force in any direction is also zero. In particular, the net external forces along the typical x- and y-axes are zero.

Two important kinds of equilibrium are shown:

- Static equilibrium: the object is motionless.
- Dynamic equilibrium: the object moves at constant velocity.

In static equilibrium, the forces acting on the object add up to zero.

In dynamic equilibrium, there are horizontal and vertical forces, but the net external force in any direction is zero. For the car example, the applied force between the tires and the road is balanced by air friction, and the weight of the car is supported by the normal forces, which are shown as equal for all four tires.

It is not sufficient for the net external force of a system to be zero for a system to be in equilibrium.

## Key formulas
- First condition for equilibrium:
  
  \[
  \sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = 0
  \]

- Equivalent component form along typical axes:
  
  \[
  \sum F_x = 0, \qquad \sum F_y = 0
  \]

## Variables and units
- \(\sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}}\): net external force on the system, in newtons (N)
- \(\sum F_x\): sum of force components in the x-direction, in newtons (N)
- \(\sum F_y\): sum of force components in the y-direction, in newtons (N)
