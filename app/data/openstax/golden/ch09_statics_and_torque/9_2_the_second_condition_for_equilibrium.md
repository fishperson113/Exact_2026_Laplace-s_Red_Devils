# 9.2 The Second Condition for Equilibrium

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 9, Section 9.2

## Concept explanation
The first condition for equilibrium is zero net external force, but that alone is not enough. A system can have zero net force and still rotate with changing angular velocity. The second condition for equilibrium is that the net external torque on the system must be zero.

Torque describes the turning or twisting effectiveness of a force. It depends on:
- the magnitude of the force,
- the distance from the pivot point to the point where the force is applied,
- the angle between the force and the line from the point of application to the pivot point.

For a door, a larger force makes rotation more effective, pushing farther from the hinges is more effective than pushing near them, and pushing perpendicular to the door is most effective.

Torque depends on the chosen pivot point. For rotation in a plane, torque can be clockwise or counterclockwise relative to that pivot. A common sign convention is:
- counterclockwise torque: positive
- clockwise torque: negative

If the net external torque is zero, the system satisfies the second condition for equilibrium. This condition is valid for any choice of pivot point in an inertial frame of reference.

## Key formulas
\[
\tau = rF\sin\theta
\]

\[
\tau = F\ell
\]

where the perpendicular lever arm is

\[
\ell = r\sin\theta
\]

The second condition for equilibrium is

\[
\sum \tau = 0
\]

For the seesaw example, the torque balance is written using the two children’s weights:
\[
m_1 g r_1 = m_2 g r_2
\]

The first condition for equilibrium used in the example is
\[
\sum F_y = 0
\]

## Variables and units
- \(\tau\): torque, SI unit \( \text{N}\cdot\text{m} \)
- \(F\): force, SI unit newton (N)
- \(r\): distance from pivot point to the point where the force is applied, SI unit meter (m)
- \(\theta\): angle between the force and the vector from point of application to pivot point
- \(\ell\): perpendicular lever arm, SI unit meter (m)
- \(m\): mass, SI unit kilogram (kg)
- \(g\): acceleration due to gravity
- \(\sum \tau\): net external torque
- \(\sum F_y\): net vertical force

## Worked example
The section gives a seesaw example with two children on a seesaw of negligible mass.

Given:
- \(m_1 = 26.0\ \text{kg}\)
- \(r_1 = 1.60\ \text{m}\)
- \(m_2 = 32.0\ \text{kg}\)

### (a) Find the distance of the second child from the pivot
Because the seesaw is in equilibrium, the net torque is zero:
\[
m_1 g r_1 = m_2 g r_2
\]

Solving for \(r_2\):
\[
r_2 = \frac{m_1 r_1}{m_2}
\]

Substitute the values:
\[
r_2 = \frac{(26.0)(1.60)}{32.0} = 1.30\ \text{m}
\]

So the heavier child sits closer to the pivot.

### (b) Find the supporting force exerted by the pivot
Using the first condition for equilibrium:
\[
\sum F_y = 0
\]

The pivot’s upward force must balance the total weight of the two children:
\[
F = (m_1 + m_2)g
\]

Substituting the given masses:
\[
F = (26.0 + 32.0)g
\]

The section states that the pivot supports the total weight of the system.
