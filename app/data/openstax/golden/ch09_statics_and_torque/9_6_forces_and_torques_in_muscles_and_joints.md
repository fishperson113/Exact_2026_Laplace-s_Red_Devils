# 9.6 Forces and Torques in Muscles and Joints

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 9, Section 9.6

## Concept explanation
Muscles, bones, and joints are important applications of statics. Muscles can only contract, so they occur in pairs: the biceps is a flexor that closes the limb, and the triceps is an extensor that opens the limb. In many skeletal systems, muscles exert much larger forces within the body than the limbs apply to the outside world because most muscles are attached to bones via tendons close to joints. This gives mechanical advantages much less than 1, so the input force is much greater than the output force.

In the forearm example, the biceps must exert a force much larger than the weight of the forearm plus its load. The force depends on the angle between the forearm and upper arm, and also on the muscle length. The force a muscle can exert is smaller when the muscle is shorter than when it is stretched.

Very large forces are also created in the joints. In the forearm example, the downward force exerted by the humerus at the elbow joint is 407 N, or 6.38 times the total weight supported. Because muscles can contract but not expand beyond their resting length, joint and muscle forces often act in opposite directions and subtract to support the load.

These forces become especially large when the load is far from the joint. This is important in racquet sports, where the mass times lever arm of the racquet affects the torque and the load on the elbow. Repetitive motion, undue torques, and poor equipment selection can contribute to joint deterioration and tendon damage such as tennis elbow. Proper technique and sweet-spot impact reduce vibration and impact force.

Knowledge of these force and torque relationships is used in physical therapy and coaching. Exercise routines and underwater exercise can apply larger forces to strengthen muscles, but connecting tissues, tendons, cartilage, and joints can also be damaged by large forces.

The back is more complicated than the arm or leg, but the same principles apply. Back muscles typically have mechanical advantages less than 1, so they must exert very large forces that are borne by the spinal column. Discs crushed by mere exertion are common. The jaw is an exception: the masseter muscles have a mechanical advantage greater than 1 for the back teeth, allowing very large biting forces. Clenching teeth can cause stress headaches because the sustained large force fatigues the muscles around the skull.

Bad posture causes back strain because the upper body’s center of gravity must be balanced over the hips and feet. With good posture, the upper body’s cg is directly above the pivot in the hips, so the upper body’s weight exerts no torque about the hips and no muscle action is required to hold the body up. With poor posture, the upper body’s cg is in front of the pivot, creating a clockwise torque around the hips that is counteracted by muscles in the lower back. These muscles must exert large forces because their perpendicular lever arm is small. Similar strain occurs when sitting poorly at a desk. When carrying loads, people lean to keep the center of gravity of the entire body above the base of support.

Lifting objects with the back can cause muscle strain and damage to discs and vertebrae because abnormally large forces are created in the back muscles and spine. Proper lifting with the back erect and using the legs reduces the forces in the back.

Having most skeletal muscles attached close to joints has benefits. One advantage is speed, since small muscle contractions can produce large limb movements in a short time. Other advantages are flexibility and agility because many joints operate over a wide range of motion. Real muscle-bone-joint systems are more complex: the pivot point in a joint can change as the joint flexes, so perpendicular lever arms and mechanical advantage change too. The force the biceps must exert changes as the forearm flexes. Similar mechanisms explain reduced leg strain when a bicycle seat is adjusted properly.

## Key formulas
- Torque relation:
  $$ 
  \tau = rF\sin\theta
   $$
- Using perpendicular lever arm:
  $$ 
  \tau = F\ell
   $$
  where $\ell $ is the perpendicular lever arm.
- Static equilibrium conditions:
  $$ 
  \sum F = 0
   $$
  $$ 
  \sum \tau = 0
   $$
- Mechanical advantage:
  $$ 
  \text{mechanical advantage} = \frac{\text{output force}}{\text{input force}}
   $$

## Variables and units
- $\tau $: torque, SI unit newton-meter, written N·m
- $F $: force, in newtons (N)
- $r $: distance from pivot point to point where the force is applied
- $\theta $: angle between $\vec r $ and $\vec F $
- $\ell $: perpendicular lever arm, the shortest distance from the pivot point to the line along which the force acts
- $m $: mass, in kilograms (kg)
- $g $: gravitational field strength, used for weight $mg $
- Center of gravity: point where the total weight of the body is assumed to be concentrated

## Worked example
**Example 9.4: Muscles Exert Bigger Forces Than You Might Think**

Calculate the force the biceps muscle must exert to hold the forearm and its load as shown in Figure 9.25, and compare this force with the weight of the forearm plus its load. The angle between the forearm and upper arm is $90^\circ $.

Using the second condition for equilibrium about the elbow joint:
$$ 
\sum \tau = 0
 $$
the torques from the weights are clockwise and the torque from the biceps is counterclockwise. Solving gives the biceps force in terms of the known quantities. Substituting the values from the figure yields the muscle force and the comparison with the total supported weight.

The combined weight of the arm and its load is 111 N, and the ratio of the force exerted by the biceps to the total weight is:
$$ 
\frac{F_{\text{biceps}}}{W} = 7.38
 $$
So the biceps muscle exerts a force 7.38 times the weight supported.

A related statement from the text notes that the downward force exerted by the humerus at the elbow joint is 407 N, or 6.38 times the total weight supported.

## Key concepts used
- Muscles can only contract, so they often work in pairs.
- Most skeletal muscles attach close to joints, giving mechanical advantages less than 1.
- Large torques arise when loads are far from the pivot.
- Static equilibrium requires zero net force and zero net torque.
- Good posture reduces torque about the hips and back strain.
- Bad posture, lifting with the back, and repetitive sports motions can create large muscle, joint, and tendon forces.
- The back generally has mechanical advantages less than 1, while the masseter muscle in the jaw can have mechanical advantage greater than 1.
- Real biological lever systems change with joint angle and muscle length.
