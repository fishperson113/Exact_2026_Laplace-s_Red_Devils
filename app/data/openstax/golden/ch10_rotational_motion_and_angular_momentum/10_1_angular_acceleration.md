# 10.1 Angular Acceleration

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 10, Section 10.1

## Concept explanation
Uniform circular motion is motion in a circle at constant speed, so the angular velocity is constant. Angular velocity describes the rate of change of angle, and for circular motion it is related to linear speed by
\[
v=r\omega
\]
where \(r\) is the radius of curvature.

When the angular velocity changes, the motion is non-uniform circular motion and the object has angular acceleration. Angular acceleration is the rate of change of angular velocity:
\[
\alpha=\frac{\Delta\omega}{\Delta t}
\]
If \(\omega\) increases, then \(\alpha\) is positive. If \(\omega\) decreases, then \(\alpha\) is negative.

In circular motion, linear acceleration tangent to the path is called tangential acceleration. Tangential acceleration changes the magnitude of the velocity, while centripetal acceleration changes the direction of the velocity. These two accelerations are perpendicular and independent.

The relationship between tangential acceleration and angular acceleration is
\[
a=r\alpha
\]
This shows that linear acceleration and angular acceleration are directly proportional, and the radius also affects the size of the linear acceleration.

## Key formulas
\[
\omega=\frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t}
\]

\[
v=r\omega
\]

\[
\alpha=\frac{\Delta\omega}{\Delta t}
\]

\[
a=\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}
\]

\[
a_t=r\alpha
\]

\[
v=r\omega
\]

\[
a_t=r\alpha
\]

## Variables and units
- \(\theta\): angle of rotation, measured in radians
- \(t\): time, measured in seconds \((\text{s})\)
- \(\omega\): angular velocity, measured in radians per second \((\text{rad/s})\)
- \(\alpha\): angular acceleration, measured in radians per second squared \((\text{rad/s}^2)\)
- \(v\): linear speed or tangential speed, measured in meters per second \((\text{m/s})\)
- \(a\): linear acceleration, measured in meters per second squared \((\text{m/s}^2)\)
- \(a_t\): tangential acceleration, measured in meters per second squared \((\text{m/s}^2)\)
- \(r\): radius, measured in meters \((\text{m})\)

Radians are dimensionless, but they appear in angular relationships.

## Worked example
### Calculating the Angular Acceleration and Deceleration of a Bike Wheel
A teenager starts a bicycle rear wheel spinning from rest to a final angular velocity of 250 rpm in 5.00 s.

**(a) Find the angular acceleration.**

Use
\[
\alpha=\frac{\Delta\omega}{\Delta t}
\]
with initial angular velocity \(\omega_i=0\) and final angular velocity \(\omega_f=250\ \text{rpm}\).

Convert to standard units:
\[
250\ \text{rpm} = 250\left(\frac{2\pi\ \text{rad}}{60\ \text{s}}\right)=26.2\ \text{rad/s}
\]

Then
\[
\alpha=\frac{26.2-0}{5.00}=5.24\ \text{rad/s}^2
\]

**(b) If the wheel is then brought to rest by braking, how long does it take to stop?**

Now the wheel slows from \(26.2\ \text{rad/s}\) to \(0\). Using
\[
\alpha=\frac{\Delta\omega}{\Delta t}
\]
and solving for time gives
\[
\Delta t=\frac{\Delta\omega}{\alpha}
\]
The source states the braking angular acceleration is negative and large in magnitude; the stopping time is found from the given angular acceleration and initial angular velocity.
