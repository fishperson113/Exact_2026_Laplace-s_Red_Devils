# 10.2 Kinematics of Rotational Motion

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 10, Section 10.2

## Concept explanation
Kinematics is the description of motion without regard to forces or masses. In rotational motion, the kinematic quantities are rotation angle, angular velocity, angular acceleration, and time.

Rotational kinematics is completely analogous to translational, one-dimensional kinematics. If angular acceleration is constant, the same mathematical structure used for straight-line motion can be applied to rotation.

The main rotational and translational quantities correspond as follows:
- rotation angle ↔ displacement
- angular velocity ↔ velocity
- angular acceleration ↔ acceleration
- time ↔ time

When solving rotational kinematics problems:
1. Identify that rotational motion is involved.
2. Determine the unknown quantity.
3. List the known quantities.
4. Choose the appropriate kinematic equation.
5. Substitute values, using radians for angles.
6. Check whether the answer is reasonable.

The section also emphasizes that linear and rotational quantities are connected. For example, a point on the edge of a rotating object has a linear speed related to angular velocity by the radius.

## Key formulas
Constant-angular-acceleration rotational kinematics:
- \(\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t\)
- \(\theta = \theta_0 + \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2\)
- \(\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha(\theta - \theta_0)\)
- \(\theta - \theta_0 = \frac{1}{2}(\omega_0 + \omega)t\)

Connections between rotational and linear quantities:
- \(v = r\omega\)
- \(x = r\theta\)

For uniform angular acceleration, these equations are valid when \(\alpha\) is constant.

## Variables and units
- \(\theta\): rotation angle, measured in radians
- \(\theta_0\): initial angle, in radians
- \(\omega\): angular velocity, in rad/s
- \(\omega_0\): initial angular velocity, in rad/s
- \(\omega\) final may be found from \(\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t\)
- \(\alpha\): angular acceleration, in rad/s\(^2\)
- \(t\): time, in s
- \(r\): radius, in m
- \(v\): linear speed, in m/s
- \(x\): linear displacement, in m

Angles used in calculations relating linear and angular quantities must be in radians. Radians are dimensionless.

## Worked example
### Example 10.3: Calculating the Acceleration of a Fishing Reel
A deep-sea fisherman hooks a big fish that swims away from the boat pulling the fishing line from his fishing reel. The whole system is initially at rest and the fishing line unwinds from the reel at a radius of 4.50 cm from its axis of rotation. The reel is given an angular acceleration of \(110\ \text{rad/s}^2\) for \(2.00\ \text{s}\).

(a) What is the final angular velocity of the reel?  
(b) At what speed is fishing line leaving the reel after 2.00 s elapses?  
(c) How many revolutions does the reel make?  
(d) How many meters of fishing line come off the reel in this time?

**Solution**
- (a) Use \(\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t\). Since the reel starts from rest, \(\omega_0 = 0\). Thus \(\omega = (110)(2.00) = 220\ \text{rad/s}\).
- (b) Use \(v = r\omega\) with \(r = 4.50\ \text{cm} = 0.0450\ \text{m}\). Thus \(v = (0.0450)(220) = 9.90\ \text{m/s}\).
- (c) Use \(\theta = \theta_0 + \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2\). With \(\theta_0 = 0\) and \(\omega_0 = 0\), \(\theta = \frac{1}{2}(110)(2.00)^2 = 220\ \text{rad}\). Convert to revolutions:
  \[
  \text{revolutions} = \frac{220}{2\pi} \approx 35.0
  \]
- (d) Use \(x = r\theta\). With \(r = 0.0450\ \text{m}\) and \(\theta = 220\ \text{rad}\),
  \[
  x = (0.0450)(220) = 9.90\ \text{m}
  \]

## Key concepts used
- Rotational kinematics describes motion without using forces or masses.
- Constant angular acceleration allows direct use of rotational kinematic equations.
- Rotational motion is mathematically analogous to one-dimensional translational motion.
- Linear and angular quantities are connected through the radius of rotation.
- Radians must be used when relating angular and linear quantities.
