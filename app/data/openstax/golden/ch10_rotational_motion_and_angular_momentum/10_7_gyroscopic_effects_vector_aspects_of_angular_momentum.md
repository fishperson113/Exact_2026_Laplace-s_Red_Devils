# 10.7 Gyroscopic Effects: Vector Aspects of Angular Momentum

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 10, Section 10.7

## Concept explanation
Angular momentum is a vector, so it has both magnitude and direction. Torque affects both the direction and the magnitude of angular momentum. The direction of angular momentum for a rotating object is found using the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction of rotation, and your thumb points in the direction of the angular velocity vector and the angular momentum vector. These vectors are perpendicular to the plane of rotation and point along the axis of rotation.

Because angular momentum is related to angular velocity by \( \vec{L} = I\vec{\omega} \), the direction of \(\vec{L}\) is the same as the direction of \(\vec{\omega}\). Torque changes angular momentum according to
\[
\vec{\tau} = \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt}.
\]
This means the change in angular momentum has the same direction as the torque that produces it.

Gyroscopic effects follow from the vector nature of angular momentum. If a torque acts perpendicular to the angular momentum of a spinning object, the torque changes the direction of \(\vec{L}\) without changing its magnitude. A spinning bicycle wheel, for example, responds in a direction perpendicular to the applied forces rather than in the direction one might expect. This same reasoning explains gyroscope precession: the gyroscope’s angular momentum changes direction, and the gyroscope precesses around a vertical axis because the torque is horizontal and perpendicular to \(\vec{L}\).

If a gyroscope is not spinning, it acquires angular momentum in the direction of the torque and falls over in the usual way, rotating about a horizontal axis. Earth also behaves like a gigantic gyroscope. Its angular momentum points along its axis toward Polaris, and Earth precesses slowly due to the torque exerted by the Sun and Moon on Earth’s nonspherical shape.

Rotational kinetic energy is not a vector. Energy is always a scalar, regardless of whether the motion is rotational or translational.

## Key formulas
\[
\vec{L} = I\vec{\omega}
\]

\[
\vec{\tau} = \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt}
\]

\[
\text{If net external torque is zero, angular momentum is conserved.}
\]

## Variables and units
- \(\vec{L}\): angular momentum, vector quantity
- \(I\): moment of inertia, scalar; SI unit: \(\mathrm{kg\cdot m^2}\)
- \(\vec{\omega}\): angular velocity, vector quantity; SI unit: \(\mathrm{rad/s}\)
- \(\vec{\tau}\): torque, vector quantity; SI unit: \(\mathrm{N\cdot m}\)

Right-hand rule:
- Curl fingers in the direction of rotation.
- Thumb points in the direction of \(\vec{\omega}\) and \(\vec{L}\).

## Key concepts used
- Angular momentum is a vector quantity.
- Angular velocity and angular momentum point along the axis of rotation.
- Right-hand rule gives the direction of \(\vec{\omega}\) and \(\vec{L}\).
- Torque changes angular momentum according to \(\vec{\tau} = d\vec{L}/dt\).
- A torque perpendicular to \(\vec{L}\) changes direction, not magnitude, of angular momentum.
- Gyroscopic precession results from this perpendicular torque.
- A nonspinning gyroscope falls over rather than precessing.
- Earth precesses like a gyroscope due to solar and lunar torque.
