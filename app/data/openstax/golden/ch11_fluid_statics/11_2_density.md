# 11.2 Density

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 11, Section 11.2

## Concept explanation
Density is the mass per unit volume of a substance or object. It is an important characteristic of substances and helps determine whether an object sinks or floats in a fluid.

In equation form, density is defined as
\[
\rho = \frac{m}{V}
\]
where \(\rho\) is the symbol for density, \(m\) is the mass, and \(V\) is the volume occupied by the substance.

A ton of feathers and a ton of bricks have the same mass, but the feathers occupy much more volume because their density is much lower. Density can also help identify the composition of a material. For example, the density of gold is about 2.5 times the density of iron, which is about 2.5 times the density of aluminum.

The SI unit of density is \( \text{kg/m}^3 \). The metric system was originally devised so that water would have a density of \(1.00\ \text{g/cm}^3\), equivalent to \(1000\ \text{kg/m}^3\). This is why the kilogram was first devised to be the mass of 1000 mL of water, which has a volume of 1000 cm\(^3\).

Density also reflects the phase and substructure of matter. The densities of liquids and solids are roughly comparable because their atoms are in close contact. The densities of gases are much smaller because the atoms in gases are separated by large amounts of empty space.

## Key formulas
\[
\rho = \frac{m}{V}
\]

Rearranged for mass:
\[
m = \rho V
\]

For a reservoir with uniform average depth:
\[
V = A d
\]
where \(A\) is surface area and \(d\) is average depth.

Substituting into the mass relation:
\[
m = \rho A d
\]

## Variables and units
- \(\rho\): density, SI unit \( \text{kg/m}^3 \)
- \(m\): mass, SI unit kg
- \(V\): volume, SI unit m\(^3\)
- \(A\): surface area, SI unit m\(^2\)
- \(d\): depth, SI unit m

Representative densities mentioned in the section include:
- Water (4°C): \(1.000\ \text{g/cm}^3\)
- Air: much lower than liquids and solids

## Worked example
### Calculating the Mass of a Reservoir From Its Volume

A reservoir has a surface area of \(3.0 \times 10^7\ \text{m}^2\) and an average depth of \(40.0\ \text{m}\). What mass of water is held behind the dam?

**Strategy**

Calculate the volume from the reservoir’s dimensions, then use the density of water from Table 11.1 and the definition of density:
\[
m = \rho V
\]

**Solution**

First find the volume:
\[
V = A d = (3.0 \times 10^7\ \text{m}^2)(40.0\ \text{m}) = 1.2 \times 10^9\ \text{m}^3
\]

The density of water is:
\[
\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3
\]

Now compute the mass:
\[
m = \rho V = (1000\ \text{kg/m}^3)(1.2 \times 10^9\ \text{m}^3) = 1.2 \times 10^{12}\ \text{kg}
\]

**Discussion**

A large reservoir contains a very large mass of water. Its weight is \(mg\), where \(g \approx 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2\). However, the dam does not need to supply a force equal to the full weight of the water it holds back.

## Key concepts used
- Density is mass per unit volume.
- The density formula is \(\rho = m/V\).
- Mass can be found from \(m = \rho V\).
- Volume can be found from geometric dimensions such as \(V = Ad\).
- Materials with greater density pack more mass into the same volume.
- Liquids and solids usually have similar densities; gases are much less dense.
- Density can help identify substances and explain floating and sinking.
