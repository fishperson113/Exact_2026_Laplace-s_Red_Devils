# 11.4 Variation of Pressure with Depth in a Fluid

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 11, Section 11.4

## Concept explanation
Pressure in a fluid can be understood as the effect of the fluid’s weight. At the Earth’s surface, atmospheric pressure is caused by the weight of the air above a point. As altitude increases, the amount of air above decreases, so pressure decreases. Under water, pressure increases with depth because there is more water above the point, and the pressure includes both the water’s weight and the atmosphere’s pressure at the surface.

For a fluid at rest, the pressure due to the fluid’s weight at depth \(h\) below the surface is

\[
P = \rho h g
\]

where \(\rho\) is the fluid density and \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity. This relationship is valid generally for a fluid of average density \(\rho\) below its surface. For liquids, which are nearly incompressible, the equation holds to great depths. For gases, which are compressible, it applies only when density changes are small over the depth considered.

The derivation comes from considering a column of fluid in a container. The bottom supports the weight of the fluid above it, and the area cancels when pressure is written as force per area.

Atmospheric pressure is another example of pressure due to fluid weight. At sea level, the standard atmospheric pressure is

\[
P_{\text{atm}} = 1.01\times 10^5\ \text{Pa} = 1.00\ \text{atm}
\]

This means the column of air above \(1.00\ \text{m}^2\) of Earth’s surface has a weight equal to \(1.01\times 10^5\ \text{N}\).

## Key formulas
\[
P = \frac{F}{A}
\]

\[
m = \rho V
\]

\[
V = Ah
\]

\[
P = \rho h g
\]

\[
F = PA
\]

\[
P_{\text{atm}} = 1.01\times 10^5\ \text{Pa} = 1.00\ \text{atm}
\]

\[
\rho = \frac{P}{hg}
\]

## Variables and units
- \(P\): pressure, in pascals \((\text{Pa}) = \text{N}/\text{m}^2\)
- \(F\): force, in newtons \((\text{N})\)
- \(A\): area, in square meters \((\text{m}^2)\)
- \(m\): mass, in kilograms \((\text{kg})\)
- \(\rho\): density, in kilograms per cubic meter \((\text{kg}/\text{m}^3)\)
- \(V\): volume, in cubic meters \((\text{m}^3)\)
- \(h\): depth or height of a fluid column, in meters \((\text{m})\)
- \(g\): acceleration due to gravity, in \(\text{m}/\text{s}^2\)

## Worked example
### Example 11.3: Calculating the Average Pressure and Force Exerted: What Force Must a Dam Withstand?
A dam is 500 m wide, and the water is 80.0 m deep at the dam.

**(a)** What is the average pressure on the dam due to the water?  
**(b)** Calculate the force exerted against the dam and compare it with the weight of the water in the reservoir.

**Strategy for (a)**  
The average pressure due to the weight of the water is the pressure at the average depth of 40.0 m, since pressure increases linearly with depth.

**Solution for (a)**  
Use

\[
P = \rho h g
\]

with the density of water and \(h = 40.0\ \text{m}\), giving

\[
P = 3.92\times 10^5\ \text{Pa}
\]

**Strategy for (b)**  
The force exerted on the dam by the water is the average pressure times the area of contact:

\[
F = PA
\]

The area of the dam is its width times its depth:

\[
A = (500\ \text{m})(80.0\ \text{m})
\]

**Discussion**  
The resulting force is large, but it is small compared with the weight of the water in the reservoir. The pressure in part (a) is independent of the width and length of the lake; it depends only on the average depth at the dam. Therefore, the force depends on the water’s average depth and the dimensions of the dam, not on the horizontal extent of the reservoir.

### Example 11.4: Calculating Average Density: How Dense Is the Air?
The atmosphere extends to an altitude of 120 km. Using the relation \(P = \rho h g\), solve for density:

\[
\rho = \frac{P}{hg}
\]

With atmospheric pressure at sea level and \(h = 120\ \text{km}\), the result is an average atmospheric density of about \(0.11\ \text{kg}/\text{m}^3\).

This is much smaller than the density of air at sea level listed in Table 11.1, which is about 15 times larger than this average value. Because air is compressible, its density is greatest near Earth’s surface and decreases rapidly with altitude.

## Key concepts used
- Pressure in a static fluid is due to the weight of the fluid above.
- Pressure increases with depth in a fluid.
- In liquids, pressure can be treated with \(P = \rho h g\) because liquids are nearly incompressible.
- In gases, the same relation is approximate and valid only when density changes are small over the depth considered.
- Atmospheric pressure at sea level is standardly taken as \(1.01\times 10^5\ \text{Pa}\) or \(1.00\ \text{atm}\).
- The force on a surface in a fluid is found from pressure times area.
