# 11.6 Gauge Pressure, Absolute Pressure, and Pressure Measurement

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 11, Section 11.6

## Concept explanation
Gauge pressure is pressure measured relative to atmospheric pressure. It is positive when the pressure is above atmospheric pressure and negative when it is below atmospheric pressure. This is why tire gauges read zero at atmospheric pressure: they are calibrated to ignore atmospheric pressure and show only pressure above or below it.

Absolute pressure is the total pressure in a fluid. It is the sum of gauge pressure and atmospheric pressure. In fluids not enclosed in a rigid container, atmospheric pressure contributes to the total pressure because pressure is transmitted through the fluid by Pascal’s principle.

Most fluids cannot have negative absolute pressure, because fluids push rather than pull. Therefore the smallest possible absolute pressure is zero, which corresponds to the smallest possible gauge pressure of negative atmospheric pressure.

Mechanical pressure gauges convert pressure into a force and then into a readable output. One common class of pressure-measuring devices is the manometer, which uses the pressure due to the weight of a fluid column.

An open-tube manometer has one side open to the atmosphere. If the fluid levels are unequal, the pressure difference is related to the height difference of the fluid columns. This makes the manometer an ideal device for measuring gauge pressure.

A mercury barometer measures atmospheric pressure, not gauge pressure, because the space above the mercury is nearly a vacuum. Atmospheric pressure supports a column of mercury in the tube. Since atmospheric pressure changes with altitude, the barometer can also function as an altimeter.

Blood pressure is often measured with a mercury manometer and an inflatable cuff. The maximum blood pressure during a heartbeat is called systolic pressure, and the minimum is called diastolic pressure. Blood pressure is commonly quoted as systolic over diastolic, such as 120/80.

## Key formulas
$$ 
P = P_{\text{g}} + P_{\text{atm}}
 $$

where:
- $P $ is absolute pressure
- $P_{\text{g}} $ is gauge pressure
- $P_{\text{atm}} $ is atmospheric pressure

For a fluid column in a manometer:
$$ 
P = \rho g h
 $$

where the pressure due to the weight of a fluid depends on the fluid density, gravitational acceleration, and height of the column.

For an open-tube manometer, the gauge pressure is found from the height difference $h $ of the fluid columns:
$$ 
P_{\text{g}} = \rho g h
 $$
with the sign determined by whether the pressure on the connected side is above or below atmospheric pressure.

## Variables and units
- $P_{\text{g}} $: gauge pressure
- $P $: absolute pressure
- $P_{\text{atm}} $: atmospheric pressure
- $\rho $: fluid density
- $g $: acceleration due to gravity
- $h $: height difference or fluid column height

Units used in the section include:
- pascal (Pa) in SI units
- psi (pounds per square inch)
- kPa
- mm Hg

Useful values mentioned:
- atmospheric pressure $= 14.7\ \text{psi} $
- mercury density $= 13.6 $ times the density of water

## Worked example
### Example 11.7: Calculating Height of IV Bag: Blood Pressure and Intravenous Infusions
An intravenous infusion is to be delivered using gravity. The fluid has density $1.00\ \text{g/mL} $. At what height should the IV bag be placed above the entry point so that the fluid just enters the vein if the blood pressure in the vein is $18\ \text{mm Hg} $ above atmospheric pressure? Assume the IV bag is collapsible.

**Strategy**
The fluid just enters the vein when the pressure at the entry point from the IV fluid is equal to the blood pressure in the vein. So the required fluid column must produce a gauge pressure of $18\ \text{mm Hg} $.

**Solution**
Convert the pressure to SI units and use the pressure due to a fluid column:
$$ 
P = \rho g h
 $$
Rearranging for height:
$$ 
h = \frac{P}{\rho g}
 $$
Substituting the given values gives:
$$ 
h = 0.24\ \text{m}
 $$

**Discussion**
The IV bag must be placed $0.24\ \text{m} $ above the entry point for the fluid to just enter the vein. In practice, IV bags are usually placed higher than this. Blood-collection bags are placed below the donor so blood flows from the arm to the bag.

## Key concepts used
- Gauge pressure is pressure relative to atmospheric pressure.
- Absolute pressure equals gauge pressure plus atmospheric pressure.
- Atmospheric pressure contributes to total pressure in fluids not enclosed in rigid containers.
- Pressure due to a fluid column is given by $\rho g h $.
- An open-tube manometer measures gauge pressure from fluid height differences.
- A mercury barometer measures atmospheric pressure.
- Blood pressure is described by systolic and diastolic pressures.
