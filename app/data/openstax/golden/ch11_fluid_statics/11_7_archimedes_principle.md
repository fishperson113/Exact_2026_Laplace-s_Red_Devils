# 11.7 Archimedes’ Principle

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 11, Section 11.7

## Concept explanation
The buoyant force is the net upward force on any object in any fluid. It arises because fluid pressure increases with depth, so the upward force on the bottom of an object is greater than the downward force on the top. The difference between these forces is the buoyant force.

If the buoyant force is greater than the object’s weight, the object rises and floats. If the buoyant force is less than the object’s weight, the object sinks. If the buoyant force equals the object’s weight, the object remains suspended at that depth.

Archimedes’ principle states that the buoyant force on an object equals the weight of the fluid it displaces. This is valid for any object in any fluid, whether partially or totally submerged.

Shape affects buoyancy because a differently shaped object can displace a different amount of fluid. For example, clay sinks as a lump but can float when shaped like a boat because the boat displaces more water and thus experiences a greater buoyant force.

Density is central to floating and sinking. If an object’s average density is less than the surrounding fluid’s density, it floats. If its average density is greater, it sinks. For a floating object, the fraction submerged depends on the ratio of the object’s average density to the fluid density. Specific gravity is defined as the ratio of the density of an object to the density of a fluid, usually water. If an object floats, its specific gravity is less than 1; if it sinks, its specific gravity is greater than 1; if it is exactly 1, it remains suspended.

Submerged objects appear lighter because of the buoyant force. The apparent weight loss equals the weight of the displaced fluid. This principle can be used to determine the density of an object or a fluid by weighing the object in air and while submerged.

## Key formulas
Archimedes’ principle:
\[
F_B = w_{\text{fluid displaced}}
\]

Density:
\[
\rho = \frac{m}{V}
\]

Fraction submerged for a floating object:
\[
\frac{V_{\text{sub}}}{V_{\text{obj}}} = \frac{\rho_{\text{obj}}}{\rho_{\text{fluid}}}
\]

Specific gravity:
\[
\text{specific gravity} = \frac{\rho_{\text{obj}}}{\rho_{\text{water}}}
\]

Apparent weight loss when submerged:
\[
\Delta w = F_B
\]

Apparent mass loss when using a balance that measures mass:
\[
\Delta m = m_{\text{fluid displaced}}
\]

## Variables and units
- \(F_B\): buoyant force, in newtons (N)
- \(w_{\text{fluid displaced}}\): weight of displaced fluid, in newtons (N)
- \(\rho\): density, in kilograms per cubic meter (kg/m\(^3\))
- \(m\): mass, in kilograms (kg)
- \(V\): volume, in cubic meters (m\(^3\))
- \(V_{\text{sub}}\): submerged volume, in m\(^3\)
- \(V_{\text{obj}}\): object volume, in m\(^3\)
- \(\rho_{\text{obj}}\): average density of the object, in kg/m\(^3\)
- \(\rho_{\text{fluid}}\): density of the surrounding fluid, in kg/m\(^3\)
- \(\rho_{\text{water}}\): density of water at \(4.00^\circ\text{C}\), in kg/m\(^3\)
- Specific gravity: dimensionless

Conditions:
- Floating occurs when \(F_B > وزن\) of object.
- Sinking occurs when \(F_B <\) object weight.
- Suspension occurs when \(F_B =\) object weight.
- For a floating object, the fraction submerged equals its specific gravity.

## Worked example
### Example 11.8: Calculating buoyant force; dependency on shape
A solid steel object of mass 10,000 metric tons is completely submerged in water.

(a) Find the buoyant force and compare it with the steel’s weight.

(b) Find the maximum buoyant force if the same steel is shaped into a boat that could displace \(10{,}000\ \text{m}^3\) of water.

**Strategy for (a)**  
Use the density of steel to find the object’s volume. Since it is completely submerged, the displaced water has the same volume. Then find the mass and weight of the displaced water. By Archimedes’ principle, this is the buoyant force.

**Solution for (a)**  
Using the definition of density, the steel volume is found from its mass and density. Because the steel is completely submerged, this is also the volume of displaced water. The mass of displaced water is found from its density and volume. By Archimedes’ principle, the buoyant force equals the weight of the displaced water.

The steel’s weight is much greater than the buoyant force, so the steel remains submerged.

**Strategy for (b)**  
The given maximum displaced volume determines the maximum buoyant force, since buoyant force equals the weight of the displaced water.

**Solution for (b)**  
Find the mass of the displaced water from its density and the maximum displaced volume, then convert that mass to weight.

**Discussion**  
The maximum buoyant force is ten times the steel’s weight, meaning the ship can carry a load nine times its own weight without sinking.

## Key concepts used
- Fluid pressure increases with depth.
- Buoyant force is the net upward force from pressure differences in a fluid.
- Archimedes’ principle: buoyant force equals the weight of displaced fluid.
- Floating, sinking, and suspension depend on the relation between buoyant force and object weight.
- Object shape affects how much fluid is displaced.
- Average density determines whether an object floats or sinks.
- Fraction submerged depends on density ratio.
- Specific gravity is the density ratio relative to water.
- Apparent weight decreases when an object is submerged due to buoyancy.
- Weighing an object in air and in a fluid can be used to determine density.
