# 11.8 Cohesion and Adhesion in Liquids: Surface Tension and Capillary Action

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 11, Section 11.8

## Concept explanation
Attractive forces between molecules in liquids produce two important effects:

- **Cohesive forces** are attractive forces between molecules of the same type. These forces help hold a liquid together and allow liquids to be held in open containers.
- **Adhesive forces** are attractive forces between molecules of different types. These forces cause effects such as liquid drops clinging to surfaces.

**Surface tension** is the tendency of a liquid surface to contract to the smallest possible surface area because cohesive forces pull surface molecules inward. Molecules inside the liquid experience zero net force because they have neighbors on all sides.

Surface tension can be understood as a macroscopic effect caused by intermolecular forces. It can support small objects such as insects or a needle on water by forces in the stretched surface, even though the objects are not floating.

Surface tension is also responsible for the spherical shape of bubbles and droplets. For a spherical bubble, the pressure inside is greater than the pressure outside because the surface tension pulls inward. Smaller bubbles have greater internal gauge pressure than larger bubbles.

In the lungs, tiny mucus-lined sacs called **alveoli** behave like very small bubbles. Surface tension in the alveoli helps expel air during exhalation. A surfactant in the alveolar lining reduces surface tension in a way that depends on surface area, helping prevent small alveoli from collapsing and large alveoli from overexpanding. If water enters the lungs, surface tension becomes too large for inhalation. Newborn infants without this surfactant can develop **hyaline membrane disease**. In **emphysema**, alveolar walls deteriorate and larger sacs form, reducing the pressure produced by surface tension and making exhalation more difficult.

Adhesion and cohesion together determine the behavior of a liquid on a surface. The **contact angle** is the angle between the tangent to the liquid surface and the surface. A larger contact angle indicates stronger cohesion relative to adhesion, so the liquid tends to bead up. A smaller contact angle indicates stronger adhesion relative to cohesion, so the liquid spreads out.

**Capillary action** is the tendency of a fluid to be raised or suppressed in a narrow tube. If the contact angle is less than 90°, the fluid rises in the tube; if it is greater than 90°, the fluid is suppressed. The curved surface of a fluid in a tube is called a **meniscus**. Surface tension acts to reduce surface area, so it can pull a liquid upward in one case or downward in another.

The section also notes that capillary action alone cannot explain sap rising to the tops of tall trees. The required capillary tube radius would be unrealistically small compared with actual xylem vessels. A negative pressure associated with transpiration is suggested as part of the explanation.

## Key formulas
Surface tension is defined as force per unit length:
$$ 
\gamma = \frac{F}{l}
 $$

Gauge pressure inside a spherical bubble:
$$ 
P = \frac{4\gamma}{r}
 $$

Capillary rise or suppression height:
$$ 
h = \frac{2\gamma \cos\theta}{\rho g r}
 $$

Where:
- $\gamma $ is surface tension
- $F $ is force
- $l $ is length
- $P $ is gauge pressure
- $r $ is bubble radius or tube radius, depending on context
- $h $ is height of rise or suppression
- $\theta $ is contact angle
- $\rho $ is fluid density
- $g $ is gravitational acceleration

## Variables and units
- $\gamma $: surface tension, units N/m
- $F $: force, units N
- $l $: length, units m
- $P $: gauge pressure, units Pa
- $r $: radius, units m
- $h $: height, units m
- $\theta $: contact angle, units degrees or radians
- $\rho $: density, units kg/m$^3 $
- $g $: acceleration due to gravity, units m/s$^2 $

Other stated quantities and conditions:
- Surface tension values depend on the liquid and are tabulated in N/m.
- For the bubble-pressure relation, the result is gauge pressure.
- Capillary rise depends on the sign of $\cos\theta $: rise if $\theta < 90^\circ $, suppression if $\theta > 90^\circ $.

## Worked example
**Example 11.11: Surface Tension: Pressure Inside a Bubble**

Calculate the gauge pressure inside a soap bubble with radius $r $ using the surface tension for soapy water in Table 11.3. Convert this pressure to mm Hg.

**Strategy**

The radius is given and the surface tension can be found in Table 11.3, so the gauge pressure can be found directly from
$$ 
P = \frac{4\gamma}{r}.
 $$

**Solution**

Substituting the given values into the equation yields the gauge pressure. A conversion factor is then used to express the pressure in mm Hg.

**Discussion**

If a hole is made in the bubble, air is forced out, the radius decreases, the gauge pressure reduces to zero, and the absolute pressure inside decreases to atmospheric pressure, 760 mm Hg.

**Example 11.12: Calculating Radius of a Capillary Tube: Capillary Action: Tree Sap**

Can capillary action be solely responsible for sap rising in trees? To answer this question, calculate the radius of a capillary tube that would raise sap 100 m to the top of a giant redwood, assuming that sap’s density is $\rho $, its contact angle is zero, and its surface tension is the same as that of water at $20^\circ\text{C} $.

**Strategy**

The height to which a liquid will rise as a result of capillary action is given by
$$ 
h = \frac{2\gamma \cos\theta}{\rho g r},
 $$
and every quantity is known except for $r $.

**Solution**

Solving for $r $ and substituting the known values gives the required capillary radius.

**Discussion**

The result is unreasonable. Sap in trees moves through the xylem, which forms tubes with radii as small as $2\times 10^{-5}\,\text{m} $. This is about 180 times as large as the radius found necessary to raise sap 100 m, so capillary action alone cannot be solely responsible for sap reaching the tops of trees. The text suggests that sap is pulled upward by cohesion and transpiration-related negative pressure.
