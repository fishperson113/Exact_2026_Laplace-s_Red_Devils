# 11.9 Pressures in the Body

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 11, Section 11.9

## Concept explanation
Measuring blood pressure is one of the most common medical examinations. Pressures in different parts of the body can be measured and can serve as important medical indicators.

Typical arterial blood pressure values are about 120 mm Hg for systolic pressure and 80 mm Hg for diastolic pressure. Systolic pressure is the maximum arterial blood pressure; diastolic pressure is the minimum arterial blood pressure. High systolic pressure increases the risk of stroke and heart attack, while too low a systolic pressure can cause fainting. Systolic pressure rises during exercise and returns to normal afterward.

Diastolic pressure can indicate fluid balance. Low diastolic pressure may indicate internal hemorrhaging and a need for transfusion. High diastolic pressure can indicate excess fluid in the circulatory system or insufficient dilation of blood vessels, which can strain the heart.

Blood pressure decreases as blood flows from the aorta to smaller arteries and veins. For a person standing upright, pressure at the feet is greater than at the heart because of the weight of the blood column. This can cause blood to accumulate in the legs, leading to swelling or fainting if standing for long periods. Elastic bandages and tight stockings can help prevent this accumulation.

The heart acts as two pumps: the right side forces blood through the lungs, and the left side forces blood through the rest of the body. Right-heart failure raises pressure in the vena cavae and lowers pressure in the arteries to the lungs. Left-heart failure raises pressure entering the left side of the heart and lowers aortal pressure.

The eye is maintained by fluid pressure called intraocular pressure, normally in the range 12.0 to 24.0 mm Hg. If fluid circulation in the eye is blocked, pressure can build up and cause glaucoma. Glaucoma can permanently damage the optic nerve.

Pressure in the ear can damage the eardrum if it differs greatly from atmospheric pressure. In the example section, a 3.00-N force is used with the area of the eardrum to find the maximum tolerable gauge pressure, and then the depth in freshwater is found from the relation between pressure and depth, assuming the gauge pressure in the middle ear is zero.

Pressure inside the lungs varies with breathing. During inhalation, lung pressure drops below atmospheric pressure, causing air to flow in. During exhalation, pressure rises above atmospheric pressure, forcing air out. Diaphragm and rib-cage muscles increase lung volume for inhalation, reducing pressure. Surface tension in the alveoli creates a positive pressure opposing inhalation. The lungs are attached to the chest wall by liquid adhesion, so the pressure in the liquid between lungs and chest wall is negative. If air enters the chest cavity, this attachment can break and a lung may collapse; suction can reestablish the negative pressure.

Cerebrospinal fluid surrounding the brain and filling the spinal column normally has a pressure of about 5 to 12 mm Hg. This fluid provides flotation for the brain, whose density is nearly equal to that of the fluid, so buoyant force nearly balances the brain’s weight. If fluid is lost, the brain can rest against the skull, causing headaches, reduced blood flow, and serious damage.

Bladder pressure rises from zero to about 25 mm Hg as the bladder fills. This pressure triggers the micturition reflex, producing the sensation of needing to urinate and causing muscles around the bladder to contract, raising pressure above 100 mm Hg. Pressure can also rise because of coughing, straining, tight clothes, cold weather, or nervous tension. Excess pressure can force urine back into the kidneys.

Pressures in the skeletal system can be very large because forces are large and act on small areas. Improper lifting can create a force of 5000 N between vertebrae and a pressure of about 50 atm if applied to a small enough area, damaging spinal discs and vertebrae. Even normal vertebral forces can produce pressures of several atmospheres.

Other pressures in the body include pressures in the digestive tract, stomach, intestines, and middle ear. Pressure in the relaxed esophagus is normally negative because chest cavity pressure is normally negative. Positive stomach pressure can force acid into the esophagus, causing heartburn. The Eustachian tubes connect the middle ear to the throat and allow pressure equalization.

## Key formulas
Pressure is defined as force per unit area:
\[
P = \frac{F}{A}
\]

For fluid pressure due to depth:
\[
P = \rho g h
\]
where \(P\) is the pressure due to the fluid, \(\rho\) is the fluid density, \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity, and \(h\) is depth or fluid height.

For the eye-pressure discussion, force is calculated from:
\[
F = PA
\]

To convert mm Hg to pressure in SI units, use the standard mercury-pressure relation consistent with the definition of pressure due to a fluid column.

For pressure differences in a standing person, pressure increases with the height of the blood column relative to the heart.

## Variables and units
- \(P\): pressure, in pascals (Pa) or mm Hg when stated medically
- \(F\): force, in newtons (N)
- \(A\): area, in square meters (m\(^2\))
- \(\rho\): density, in kg/m\(^3\)
- \(g\): acceleration due to gravity, in m/s\(^2\)
- \(h\): depth or height of a fluid column, in meters (m)

Common medical pressure units in this section:
- mm Hg for blood pressure, eye pressure, spinal fluid pressure, bladder pressure, and other bodily pressures
- Gauge pressure means pressure relative to atmospheric pressure

Typical values stated in the section:
- Resting arterial blood pressure: systolic 100–140 mm Hg, diastolic 60–90 mm Hg
- Common arterial blood pressure measurements: about 120 mm Hg systolic and 80 mm Hg diastolic
- Blood pressure in large veins: 4–15 mm Hg
- Eye pressure: 12–24 mm Hg
- Brain and spinal fluid pressure: 5–12 mm Hg when lying down
- Bladder pressure while filling: 0–25 mm Hg
- Full bladder pressure: 100–150 mm Hg
- Chest cavity pressure between lungs and ribs: −8 to −4 mm Hg
- Pressure inside lungs: −2 to +3 mm Hg
- Esophagus pressure: about −2 mm Hg
- Stomach pressure: 0–20 mm Hg
- Intestines: 10–20 mm Hg
- Middle ear pressure: less than 1 mm Hg

## Key concepts used
- Pressure in the human body is often medically significant.
- Systolic pressure is the maximum arterial blood pressure.
- Diastolic pressure is the minimum arterial blood pressure and can indicate fluid balance.
- Blood pressure decreases from the heart through the circulatory system.
- Standing increases pressure in the feet because of the weight of the blood column.
- Intraocular pressure maintains eye shape.
- Glaucoma is caused by buildup of fluid pressure in the eye.
- Lung pressure can be negative during inhalation and positive during exhalation.
- Surface tension in the alveoli opposes inhalation.
- Negative pressure between lungs and chest wall helps keep the lungs attached to the chest wall.
- Cerebrospinal fluid supports and cushions the brain.
- Bladder pressure triggers the micturition reflex.
- Skeletal-system pressures can be very large because force acts on small areas.
- Pressure in the digestive tract and middle ear can also have medical effects.
