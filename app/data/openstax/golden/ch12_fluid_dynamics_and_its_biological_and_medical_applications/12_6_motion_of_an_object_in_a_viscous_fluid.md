# 12.6 Motion of an Object in a Viscous Fluid

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 12, Section 12.6

## Concept explanation
A moving object in a viscous fluid is equivalent to a stationary object in a flowing fluid stream. For example, riding a bicycle at 10 m/s in still air is equivalent to being stationary in a 10-m/s wind.

Flow around a moving object may be laminar, turbulent, or a combination of both. The Reynolds number is used to predict when a moving object creates turbulence. For an object moving in a fluid:

- If Reynolds number is less than about 1, flow around the object can be laminar, particularly if the object has a smooth shape.
- If Reynolds number is between 1 and about 10, the transition to turbulent flow occurs, depending on surface roughness and related factors.
- For an intermediate range, the flow may be either laminar or turbulent and may oscillate between the two.
- For Reynolds number greater than about 10, the flow is entirely turbulent, even at the surface of the object.

Laminar flow occurs mostly when the objects in the fluid are small, such as raindrops, pollen, and blood cells in plasma.

One consequence of viscosity is a resistance force called viscous drag that is exerted on a moving object. This drag typically depends on the object’s speed, unlike simple friction. For laminar flow, viscous drag is proportional to speed. For intermediate Reynolds numbers, viscous drag is proportional to speed squared. For larger Reynolds numbers, drag increases dramatically and behaves with greater complexity.

For laminar flow around a sphere moving slowly in a fluid, Stoke’s law gives the drag force. The drag force is proportional to the fluid viscosity, the object’s characteristic size, and the object’s speed.

An object falling through a fluid will not continue to accelerate indefinitely. Instead, viscous drag increases and slows acceleration until a critical speed, called the terminal speed, is reached and the acceleration becomes zero. After that, the object continues to fall at constant speed. Terminal speed depends on the viscosity of the fluid, the size of the object, and the density of the object relative to the fluid. Terminal speed is greatest for low-viscosity fluids and objects with high densities and small sizes.

## Key formulas
Reynolds number for an object moving in a fluid:

\[
R = \frac{2 \rho v r}{\eta}
\]

For a small sphere of radius \(r\) moving slowly in a fluid of viscosity \(\eta\), Stoke’s law gives the drag force:

\[
F_{\text{drag}} = 6 \pi \eta r v
\]

## Variables and units
- \(R\): Reynolds number, dimensionless
- \(\rho\): fluid density
- \(v\): object’s speed in the fluid, in m/s
- \(r\): characteristic length of the object; for a sphere, the radius, in m
- \(\eta\): fluid viscosity
- \(F_{\text{drag}}\): viscous drag force, in newtons (N)
- \(\pi\): mathematical constant

## Worked example
**Example 12.10: Does a Ball Have a Turbulent Wake?**

Calculate the Reynolds number \(R\) for a ball with a 7.40-cm diameter thrown at 40.0 m/s.

**Strategy**

Use the Reynolds number equation to calculate \(R\), since the needed values are given or can be found in tables of density and viscosity.

**Discussion**

The computed Reynolds number is sufficiently high to imply a turbulent wake. Most large objects, such as airplanes and sailboats, create significant turbulence as they move. The Bernoulli principle gives only qualitatively correct results in such situations.
