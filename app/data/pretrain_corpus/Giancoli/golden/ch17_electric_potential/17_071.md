# Problem 17.71

## Question
How does the energy stored in an isolated capacitor change if (a) the potential difference is doubled, or (b) the separation of the plates is doubled?

## Final answer from source
(a) Multiplied by 2; (b) multiplied by 2.

## Worked solution
For an isolated capacitor, the charge $Q $ remains constant.

The energy stored in a capacitor can be written as
$$ 
U=\frac{1}{2}CV^2
 $$
or, using $Q=CV $,
$$ 
U=\frac{Q^2}{2C}.
 $$

### (a) Potential difference is doubled
If the capacitor is isolated, $Q $ is fixed. Since
$$ 
V=\frac{Q}{C},
 $$
doubling $V $ means the capacitance must effectively remain the same while the stored energy changes as
$$ 
U \propto V^2
 $$
for fixed $C $, or equivalently, with fixed $Q $,
$$ 
U=\frac{1}{2}QV.
 $$
Thus, if $V $ is doubled, $U $ is doubled.

So the energy is **multiplied by 2**.

### (b) Separation of the plates is doubled
For a parallel-plate capacitor,
$$ 
C=\frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d},
 $$
so doubling the plate separation $d $ halves the capacitance:
$$ 
C \to \frac{C}{2}.
 $$

Since the capacitor is isolated, $Q $ stays constant, and
$$ 
U=\frac{Q^2}{2C}.
 $$
If $C $ is halved, then $U $ doubles:
$$ 
U \to 2U.
 $$

So the energy is **multiplied by 2**.

## Key concepts used
- Energy stored in a capacitor: $U=\tfrac12 CV^2=\tfrac{Q^2}{2C} $
- Isolated capacitor means charge $Q $ remains constant
- Parallel-plate capacitance: $C=\varepsilon_0 A/d $
- Doubling $V $ doubles $U $ when $C $ is unchanged
- Doubling plate separation halves $C $, which doubles $U $ when $Q $ is fixed
