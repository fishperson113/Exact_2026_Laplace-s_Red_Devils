# Problem 18.21

## Question
(II) How much would you have to raise the temperature of a copper wire (originally at 20°C) to increase its resistance by 12%?

## Final answer from source
18C°.

## Worked solution
For a metal wire, resistance varies approximately linearly with temperature:

$$ 
R = R_0\left[1+\alpha (T-T_0)\right]
 $$

where:
- $R_0 $ is the resistance at the initial temperature $T_0 $,
- $\alpha $ is the temperature coefficient of resistivity for copper,
- $T-T_0 $ is the temperature increase.

We are told the resistance increases by 12%, so:

$$ 
\frac{R-R_0}{R_0}=0.12
 $$

Thus,

$$ 
0.12=\alpha \Delta T
 $$

For copper, the temperature coefficient is approximately

$$ 
\alpha \approx 0.0065\ ^\circ\text{C}^{-1}
 $$

So,

$$ 
\Delta T=\frac{0.12}{0.0065}\approx 18.5^\circ\text{C}
 $$

Rounding to the nearest whole degree gives:

$$ 
\Delta T \approx 18^\circ\text{C}
 $$

So the wire must be raised by about $18^\circ\text{C} $.

## Key concepts used
- Temperature dependence of resistance in metals
- Linear approximation: $R = R_0[1+\alpha(T-T_0)] $
- Fractional resistance change: $\Delta R/R_0 = \alpha \Delta T $
- Copper’s temperature coefficient of resistivity
