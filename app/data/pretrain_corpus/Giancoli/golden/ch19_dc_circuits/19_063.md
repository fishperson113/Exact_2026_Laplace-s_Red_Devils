## Worked solution
The problem statement appears to have OCR corruption and is missing some symbols/values in the resistor description and in the meter wording. Since the exact circuit and the question about “the % error” are not fully recoverable from the text provided, the source answer can only be matched partially. The answer source clearly gives a voltage reading of **4.8 V**, but the current and percentage-error values are truncated in the supplied OCR.

Using the visible data as best as possible:

- Battery emf: $ \mathcal{E} = 12.0\text{ V}  $
- Internal resistance of battery: $ r = 1.0\,\Omega  $
- Two series resistors appear to include $7.5\,\text{k}\Omega $ each, though the OCR is incomplete.
- Voltmeter internal resistance: $R_V = 15\,\text{k}\Omega $

If the voltmeter is across one of the $7.5\,\text{k}\Omega $ resistors, then that resistor is in parallel with the voltmeter:

$$ 
R_{\parallel}=\frac{(7.5\,\text{k}\Omega)(15\,\text{k}\Omega)}{7.5\,\text{k}\Omega+15\,\text{k}\Omega}
=\frac{112.5}{22.5}\,\text{k}\Omega
=5.0\,\text{k}\Omega
 $$

That parallel combination is then in series with the other $7.5\,\text{k}\Omega $ resistor, giving an external resistance of

$$ 
R_{\text{ext}} = 7.5\,\text{k}\Omega + 5.0\,\text{k}\Omega = 12.5\,\text{k}\Omega
 $$

Including the battery’s internal resistance $r=1.0\,\Omega $ changes the total by a negligible amount compared with kΩ-scale resistances, so

$$ 
R_{\text{tot}} \approx 12.5\,\text{k}\Omega
 $$

Thus the circuit current is approximately

$$ 
I \approx \frac{12.0\text{ V}}{12.5\times 10^3\Omega}
=9.6\times10^{-4}\text{ A}
=0.96\text{ mA}
 $$

The voltmeter reads the drop across the parallel branch:

$$ 
V = I R_{\parallel}
= (0.96\text{ mA})(5.0\text{ k}\Omega)
=4.8\text{ V}
 $$

This matches the source’s visible final-answer value.

To find the “without meters” value for the same resistor, first note that without the voltmeter the two resistors would simply be in series:

$$ 
R_0 = 7.5\,\text{k}\Omega + 7.5\,\text{k}\Omega = 15.0\,\text{k}\Omega
 $$

So the no-meter current would be

$$ 
I_0 \approx \frac{12.0\text{ V}}{15.0\times10^3\Omega}
=0.80\text{ mA}
 $$

And the no-meter voltage across one $7.5\,\text{k}\Omega $ resistor would be

$$ 
V_0 = I_0(7.5\,\text{k}\Omega)= (0.80\text{ mA})(7.5\text{ k}\Omega)=6.0\text{ V}
 $$

Percentage errors would then be:

$$ 
\%\,\text{error in current}
=\frac{I-I_0}{I_0}\times 100\%
=\frac{0.96-0.80}{0.80}\times 100\%
=20\%
 $$

$$ 
\%\,\text{error in voltage}
=\frac{V-V_0}{V_0}\times 100\%
=\frac{4.8-6.0}{6.0}\times 100\%
=-20\%
 $$

So the meter load causes the ammeter reading to be higher and the voltmeter reading to be lower than the ideal no-meter values.

## Key concepts used
- Internal resistance of meters affects the circuit being measured
- Voltmeter resistance appears in parallel with the component being measured
- Series–parallel reduction of resistors
- Ohm’s law: $V=IR $
- Percentage error relative to the no-meter (“ideal”) value
