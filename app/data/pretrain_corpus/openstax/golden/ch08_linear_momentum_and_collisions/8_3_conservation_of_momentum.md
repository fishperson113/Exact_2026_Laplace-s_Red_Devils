# 8.3 Conservation of Momentum

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 8, Section 8.3

## Concept explanation
Momentum is an important quantity because it is conserved. In a small system, momentum of individual objects can change during an interaction, but the total momentum of a sufficiently large system remains constant.

A common example is a collision between two cars. If friction is negligible, the only unbalanced force on each car is the force of the collision. Car 1 slows down and loses momentum, while car 2 speeds up and gains momentum. The total momentum of the two-car system remains constant because the changes in momentum are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

Using impulse,
- the change in momentum of car 1 is due to the force on car 1 by car 2,
- the change in momentum of car 2 is due to the force on car 2 by car 1.

By Newton’s third law, these forces are equal and opposite. If the collision duration is the same for both cars, then the momentum changes cancel.

The conservation of momentum principle is valid for any isolated system, meaning a system for which the net external force is zero. For such a system, the total momentum before an interaction equals the total momentum after it.

Momentum can be conserved separately along different dimensions. For example, in projectile motion with negligible air resistance, horizontal momentum is conserved because the net horizontal force is zero. Vertical momentum is not conserved for the projectile alone because the net vertical force is not zero. If the projectile-Earth system is considered, total momentum is conserved in the vertical direction as well.

The principle applies to many systems, from car collisions and projectile motion to comets striking Earth, gas molecules, and subatomic particle collisions. Momentum is also a key idea in atomic and subatomic physics, where collision experiments are used to infer particle properties. Momentum is a property of all subatomic particles, including massless particles such as photons.

## Key formulas
$$ 
\Delta p_1 = F_{21}\Delta t
 $$

$$ 
\Delta p_2 = F_{12}\Delta t
 $$

With Newton’s third law,
$$ 
F_{21} = -F_{12}
 $$

So the changes in momentum satisfy
$$ 
\Delta p_1 = -\Delta p_2
 $$

For a two-object system,
$$ 
p_1 + p_2 = p_1' + p_2'
 $$

For an isolated system,
$$ 
p_{\text{tot}} = p_{\text{tot}}'
 $$

Equivalent statement:
$$ 
\sum p = \sum p'
 $$

Using Newton’s second law in momentum form,
$$ 
\sum F = \frac{dp}{dt}
 $$

For an isolated system,
$$ 
\sum F = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{dp}{dt} = 0
 $$
so total momentum is constant.

## Variables and units
- $p $: momentum
- $p_1, p_2 $: momenta of objects 1 and 2
- $p_1', p_2' $: final momenta after the collision
- $p_{\text{tot}} $: total momentum of the system
- $F_{21} $: force on object 1 due to object 2
- $F_{12} $: force on object 2 due to object 1
- $\Delta p $: change in momentum
- $\Delta t $: collision duration or time interval
- $\sum F $: net external force
- Units of momentum: $\text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s} $
- Units of force: newtons, $\text{N} $
- Units of time: seconds, $\text{s} $

Conditions and assumptions:
- Momentum conservation applies to isolated systems with zero net external force.
- In the car-collision example, friction is assumed negligible.
- The same collision duration is assumed for both objects.
- For projectile motion, horizontal momentum is conserved if air resistance is negligible.
- At ordinary speeds, the collision time can be treated as the same for both objects; near the speed of light, this assumption must be modified, though momentum conservation still holds.

## Worked example
Two cars move in the same direction. Car 1 is struck from behind by car 2. If friction is negligible, the only unbalanced force on each car is the collision force. Car 1 slows down and loses momentum, while car 2 speeds up and gains momentum. Since the collision forces are equal and opposite and act for the same time interval, the momentum change of car 1 is equal and opposite to the momentum change of car 2. Therefore, the total momentum of the two-car system remains constant before and after the collision.
