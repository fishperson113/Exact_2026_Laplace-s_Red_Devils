# Example 28.7: Field of a long, straight, current-carrying conductor

## Problem
In Section 28.6 we derived Ampere’s law using Eq. (28.9) for the field of a long, straight, current-carrying conductor. Reverse this process, and use Ampere’s law to find the field for this situation.

## Setup
The situation has cylindrical symmetry, so in Ampere’s law we take our integration path to be a circle with radius $r $ centered on the conductor and lying in a plane perpendicular to it, as in Fig. 28.16a. The field $\mathbf{B} $ is everywhere tangent to this circle and has the same magnitude $B $ everywhere on the circle.

## Solution
With our choice of integration path, Ampere’s law [Eq. (28.20)] becomes

$$ 
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = B \oint dl = B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I
 $$

so Eq. (28.9),

$$ 
B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r},
 $$

follows immediately.

Ampere’s law determines the direction of $\mathbf{B} $ as well as its magnitude. Since we chose to go counterclockwise around the integration path, the positive direction for current is out of the plane of Fig. 28.16a; this is the same as the actual current direction in the figure, so $I $ is positive and the integral $\oint \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{l} $ is also positive.

Since the $\mathbf{B} $’s run counterclockwise, the direction of $\mathbf{B} $ must be counterclockwise as well, as shown in Fig. 28.16a.

## Evaluation
Our results are consistent with those in Section 28.6.

## Key concepts used
- Ampere’s law
- Cylindrical symmetry
- Circular Amperian loop centered on a long straight conductor
- Magnetic field magnitude constant on the loop
- Right-hand rule / circulation direction from current direction
