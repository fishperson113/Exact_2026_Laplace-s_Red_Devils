# Example 31.1: Current in a personal computer

## Problem
The plate on the back of a personal computer says that it draws 2.7 A from a 120-V, 60-Hz line. For this computer, what are:
(a) the average current,
(b) the average of the square of the current, and
(c) the current amplitude?

## Setup
IDENTIFY and SET UP: This example is about alternating current.

In part (a) we find the average, over a complete cycle, of the alternating current. In part (b) we recognize that the 2.7-A current draw of the computer is the rms value, that is, the square root of the mean (average) of the square of the current, $I_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\langle i^2 \rangle} $. In part (c) we use Eq. (31.4) to relate $I_{\mathrm{rms}} $ to the current amplitude.

## Solution
EXECUTE:

(a) The average of any sinusoidally varying quantity, over any whole number of cycles, is zero.

(b) We are given $I_{\mathrm{rms}} = 2.7\ \mathrm{A} $. From the definition of rms value,
$$ 
\langle i^2 \rangle = I_{\mathrm{rms}}^2 = (2.7\ \mathrm{A})^2 = 7.3\ \mathrm{A}^2 .
 $$

(c) From Eq. (31.4), the current amplitude is
$$ 
I = \sqrt{2}\, I_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{2}(2.7\ \mathrm{A}) = 3.8\ \mathrm{A}.
 $$

Figure 31.6 shows graphs of $i $ and $i^2 $ versus time $t $.

## Evaluation
Why would we be interested in the average of the square of the current? Recall that the rate at which energy is dissipated in a resistor equals $i^2R $. This rate varies if the current is alternating, so it is best described by its average value $\langle i^2 \rangle R = I_{\mathrm{rms}}^2 R $. We’ll use this idea in Section 31.4.

## Key concepts used
- Alternating current over a complete cycle has zero average.
- The rms current satisfies $I_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\langle i^2 \rangle} $.
- For a sinusoidal current, $I = \sqrt{2}\, I_{\mathrm{rms}} $.
- Power dissipated in a resistor depends on $i^2R $, so the average of $i^2 $ is physically useful.
