# Example 21.1: Electric force versus gravitational force

## Problem
An alpha particle (the nucleus of a helium atom) has mass \(6.64 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}\) and charge \(q = +2e = 3.2 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}\). Compare the magnitude of the electric repulsion between two alpha particles with that of the gravitational attraction between them.

## Setup
**IDENTIFY and SET UP:** This problem involves Newton’s law for the gravitational force between particles and Coulomb’s law for the electric force between point charges. To compare these forces, we make our target variable the ratio
\[
\frac{F_e}{F_g}.
\]
We use Eq. (21.2) for the electric force and Eq. (13.1) for the gravitational force.

## Solution
**EXECUTE:** From Coulomb’s law and Newton’s law of gravitation,
\[
F_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{q^2}{r^2},
\qquad
F_g = G\frac{m^2}{r^2}.
\]
These are both inverse-square forces, so the \(r^2\) factors cancel when we take the ratio:
\[
\frac{F_e}{F_g}
=
\frac{\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{q^2}{r^2}}{G\frac{m^2}{r^2}}
=
\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 G}\frac{q^2}{m^2}.
\]
Substituting the values,
\[
\frac{F_e}{F_g}
=
\frac{9.0\times 10^9\ \text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2}{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ \text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{kg}^2}
\frac{(3.2\times 10^{-19}\ \text{C})^2}{(6.64\times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg})^2}
= 3.1\times 10^{35}.
\]

## Evaluation
This astonishingly large number shows that the gravitational force in this situation is completely negligible in comparison to the electric force. This is always true for interactions of atomic and subnuclear particles. But within objects the size of a person or a planet, the positive and negative charges are nearly equal in magnitude, and the net electric force is usually much smaller than the gravitational force.

## Key concepts used
- Coulomb’s law for the electric force between point charges
- Newton’s law of gravitation for the force between masses
- Ratio of two inverse-square forces
- Cancellation of the \(r^2\) dependence
- Comparison of electric and gravitational interaction strengths
