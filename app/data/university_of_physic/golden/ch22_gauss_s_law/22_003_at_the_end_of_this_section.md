# Example 22.3: at the end of this section

## Problem
Example 22.3 at the end of this section illustrates the use of Eq. (22.5). In Eq. (22.5) the electric flux is equal to the average value of the perpendicular component of the electric field, multiplied by the area of the surface.

## Setup
The general definition of electric flux is
\[
\Phi_E = \int E \cos \phi \, dA = \int E_\perp \, dA = \int \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A}
\]
where
\[
d\mathbf{A} = \hat{\mathbf{n}}\, dA.
\]

For a flat surface in a uniform electric field:
- If the surface is face-on to the electric field, then the angle between \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{A}\) is \(\phi = 0\).
- If the surface is tilted from a face-on orientation by an angle \(\phi\), then the angle between \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{A}\) is \(\phi\).
- If the surface is edge-on to the electric field, then \(\phi = 90^\circ\).

## Solution
For a flat surface in a uniform electric field, the electric flux through the surface equals the scalar product of the electric field and the area vector:
\[
\Phi_E = \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{A} = EA \cos \phi.
\]

Special cases:
- Face-on surface: \(\phi = 0\), so
  \[
  \Phi_E = \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{A} = EA.
  \]
- Tilted surface: \(\phi\) is the angle between \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{A}\), so
  \[
  \Phi_E = EA \cos \phi.
  \]
- Edge-on surface: \(\phi = 90^\circ\), so
  \[
  \Phi_E = EA \cos 90^\circ = 0.
  \]

## Evaluation
This is the same definition of electric flux that was introduced in Section 22.1, now expressed more mathematically. It shows that flux depends on the component of the electric field perpendicular to the surface and on the orientation of the surface relative to the field.

## Key concepts used
- Electric flux
- Vector area \(d\mathbf{A} = \hat{\mathbf{n}}\, dA\)
- Scalar product \(\mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{A}\)
- Perpendicular component of the electric field
- Dependence of flux on surface orientation
