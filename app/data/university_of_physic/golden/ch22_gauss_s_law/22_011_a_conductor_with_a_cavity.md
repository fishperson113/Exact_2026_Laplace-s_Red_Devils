# Example 22.11: A conductor with a cavity

## Problem
A solid conductor with a cavity carries a total charge of \(+7\ \text{nC}\). Within the cavity, insulated from the conductor, is a point charge of \(-5\ \text{nC}\). How much charge is on each surface (inner and outer) of the conductor?

## Setup
Figure 22.24 shows the situation. If the charge in the cavity is \(q\), the charge on the inner cavity surface must be \(-q\).

The conductor carries a total charge of \(q_C\), none of which is in the interior of the material. If \(-q\) is on the inner surface of the cavity, then there must be \(q_C + q\) on the outer surface of the conductor.

## Solution
Since the point charge in the cavity is \(-5\ \text{nC}\), the charge on the inner cavity surface must be \(+5\ \text{nC}\).

The conductor has total charge \(+7\ \text{nC}\). If \(+5\ \text{nC}\) is on the inner surface, then the remaining charge on the outer surface must be
\[
+7\ \text{nC} - (+5\ \text{nC}) = +2\ \text{nC}.
\]

## Evaluation
This result is consistent with electrostatic equilibrium: there can be no excess charge within the conducting material itself, so the induced charge associated with the cavity appears on the inner surface, and any remaining charge resides on the outer surface.

## Key concepts used
- In electrostatic equilibrium, the electric field inside the bulk conductor is zero.
- Gauss’s law implies that the charge induced on the inner surface of a cavity must be equal in magnitude and opposite in sign to the enclosed point charge.
- The conductor’s total charge is the sum of the charge on its inner and outer surfaces.
