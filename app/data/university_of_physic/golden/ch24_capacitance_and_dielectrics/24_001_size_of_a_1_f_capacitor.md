# Example 24.1: Size of a 1-F capacitor

## Problem
The parallel plates of a 1.0-F capacitor are 1.0 mm apart. What is their area?

## Setup
This problem uses the relationship among the capacitance \(C\), plate separation \(d\), and plate area \(A\) (the target variable) for a parallel-plate capacitor. We solve Eq. (24.2) for \(A\).

## Solution
From Eq. (24.2),
\[
A = \frac{Cd}{\varepsilon_0}.
\]

Substitute the given values:
\[
A = \frac{(1.0\ \mathrm{F})(1.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m})}{8.85 \times 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{F/m}}
\approx 1.1 \times 10^{8}\ \mathrm{m^2}.
\]

## Evaluation
This corresponds to a square about \(10\ \mathrm{km}\) on a side (about 6 miles on a side)! The volume of such a capacitor would be at least
\[
Ad \approx 1.1 \times 10^{5}\ \mathrm{m^3},
\]
equivalent to that of a cube about 50 m on a side. In fact, it’s possible to make 1-F capacitors a few centimeters on a side. The trick is to have an appropriate substance between the plates rather than a vacuum, so that, among other things, the plate separation \(d\) can be greatly reduced. We’ll explore this further in Section 24.4.

## Key concepts used
- Capacitance of a parallel-plate capacitor
- Relationship among \(C\), \(A\), and \(d\)
- Solving for plate area using \(A = Cd/\varepsilon_0\)
