# Example 25.10: Power in a short circuit

## Problem
For the short-circuit situation of Example 25.7, find the rates of energy conversion and energy dissipation in the battery and the net power output of the battery.

## Setup
**IDENTIFY and SET UP:** Our target variables are again the power inputs and outputs associated with the battery. Figure 25.25 shows the circuit. This is the same situation as in Example 25.8, but now the external resistance \(R\) is zero.

## Solution
**EXECUTE:** We found in Example 25.7 that the current in this situation is \(I = 6\ \text{A}\). From Eq. (25.19), the rate of energy conversion (chemical to electrical) in the battery is then
\[
EI = (12\ \text{V})(6\ \text{A}) = 72\ \text{W}.
\]

and the rate of dissipation of energy in the battery is
\[
I^2r = (6\ \text{A})^2(2\ \Omega) = 72\ \text{W}.
\]

The net power output of the source is
\[
P = EI - I^2r = 0.
\]

We get this same result from the expression
\[
P = V_{ab}I,
\]
because the terminal voltage \(V_{ab}\) of the source is zero.

## Evaluation
**EVALUATE:** With ideal wires and an ideal ammeter, so that \(R = 0\), all of the converted energy from the source is dissipated within the source. This is why a short-circuited battery is quickly ruined and may explode.

## Key concepts used
- Short circuit means the external resistance is zero: \(R = 0\).
- Battery power can be written as \(P = EI - I^2r\).
- The chemical-to-electrical conversion rate is \(EI\).
- Internal dissipation in the battery is \(I^2r\).
- For a short circuit, the terminal voltage is zero, so \(P = V_{ab}I = 0\).
