# Example 25.11: Mean free time in copper

## Problem
Calculate the mean free time between collisions in copper at room temperature.

## Setup
IDENTIFY and SET UP: We can obtain an expression for mean free time $t $ in terms of $m $, $n $, $e $, and $r $ by rearranging Eq. (25.24). From Example 25.1 and Table 25.1, for copper $r = 1.72 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega \cdot \text{m} $ and $n = 8.5 \times 10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3} $. In addition, $m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg} $ and $e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C} $ for electrons.

## Solution
EXECUTE: From Eq. (25.24), we get
$$ 
t = \frac{m}{n e^2 r}.
 $$

Substituting the given values,
$$ 
t = \frac{9.11 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}}{(8.5 \times 10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3})(1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C})^2(1.72 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega \cdot \text{m})}
= 2.4 \times 10^{-14}\ \text{s}.
 $$

## Evaluation
The mean free time is the average time between collisions for a given electron. Taking the reciprocal of this time, we find that each electron averages
$$ 
\frac{1}{t} = 4.2 \times 10^{13}
 $$
collisions per second.

## Key concepts used
- Mean free time between collisions in a conductor
- Rearrangement of the microscopic resistivity relation, Eq. (25.24)
- Electron number density $n $, charge $e $, electron mass $m $, and resistivity $r $
- Interpretation of mean free time as the average time between collisions
