# Sample Problem 26.04: A material has resistivity, a block of the material has resistance

## Problem
A rectangular block of iron has dimensions 1.2 cm × 1.2 cm × 15 cm. A potential difference is to be applied to the block between parallel sides and in such a way that those sides are equipotential surfaces (as in Fig. 26-8b). What is the resistance of the block if the two parallel sides are (1) the square ends (with dimensions 1.2 cm × 1.2 cm) and (2) two rectangular sides (with dimensions 1.2 cm × 15 cm)?

## Key ideas
The resistance $R $ of an object depends on how the electric potential is applied to the object. In particular, it depends on the ratio $L/A $, according to Eq. 26-16,
$$ 
R = \rho \frac{L}{A},
 $$
where $A $ is the area of the surfaces to which the potential difference is applied and $L $ is the distance between those surfaces.

## Solution
For a rectangular block, the resistance depends on which pair of opposite faces are held at different potentials.

### (1) Square ends, each $1.2\ \text{cm} \times 1.2\ \text{cm} $
Here,
$$ 
L = 15\ \text{cm} = 0.15\ \text{m},
 $$
and
$$ 
A = (1.2\ \text{cm})(1.2\ \text{cm}) = (0.012\ \text{m})^2 = 1.44\times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2.
 $$

Using the resistivity of iron,
$$ 
\rho = 1.0\times 10^{-7}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m},
 $$
we get
$$ 
R = \rho \frac{L}{A}
= (1.0\times 10^{-7})\frac{0.15}{1.44\times 10^{-4}}\ \Omega
= 1.0\times 10^{-4}\ \Omega.
 $$

### (2) Rectangular sides, each $1.2\ \text{cm} \times 15\ \text{cm} $
Now the separation between the equipotential sides is
$$ 
L = 1.2\ \text{cm} = 0.012\ \text{m},
 $$
and the area of each side is
$$ 
A = (1.2\ \text{cm})(15\ \text{cm}) = (0.012\ \text{m})(0.15\ \text{m}) = 1.8\times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2.
 $$

Thus,
$$ 
R = \rho \frac{L}{A}
= (1.0\times 10^{-7})\frac{0.012}{1.8\times 10^{-3}}\ \Omega
= 6.7\times 10^{-7}\ \Omega.
 $$

## Answer
$$ 
\boxed{(1)\ R = 1.0\times 10^{-4}\ \Omega}
 $$
$$ 
\boxed{(2)\ R = 6.7\times 10^{-7}\ \Omega}
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Resistance depends on geometry through $R=\rho L/A $.
- The applied potential difference must be considered with respect to the pair of equipotential surfaces.
- Changing which faces are used changes both $L $ and $A $, and therefore changes the resistance.
