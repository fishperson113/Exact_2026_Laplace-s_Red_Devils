# Sample Problem 26.05: Mean free time and mean free distance

## Problem
(a) What is the mean free time $t $ between collisions for the conduction electrons in copper?

## Key ideas
The mean free time $t $ of copper is approximately constant, and in particular does not depend on any electric field that might be applied to a sample of the copper. Thus, we need not consider any particular value of applied electric field. However, because the resistivity $r $ displayed by copper under an electric field depends on $t $, we can find the mean free time $t $ from Eq. 26-22:
$$ 
r = \frac{m}{ne^2 t}.
 $$

## Solution
That equation gives us
$$ 
t = \frac{m}{ne^2 r}.
 $$

The number of conduction electrons per unit volume in copper is
$$ 
n = 8.49 \times 10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3}.
 $$
We take the value of $r $ from Table 26-1.

The denominator then becomes
$$ 
(8.49 \times 10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3})(1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C})^2(1.69 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\!\cdot\!\text{m})
= 3.67 \times 10^{-17}\ \text{C}^2\Omega/\text{m}^2.
 $$

We converted units as
$$ 
\frac{\text{C}^2\Omega}{\text{m}^2}
= \frac{\text{C}^2(\text{V}/\text{A})}{\text{m}^2}
= \frac{\text{C}^2(\text{J}/\text{C})}{\text{m}^2(\text{C}/\text{s})}
= \frac{\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2}{\text{m}^2/\text{s}}
= \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{s}}.
 $$

Thus,
$$ 
t = \frac{m}{ne^2 r} = \frac{9.11 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}}{3.67 \times 10^{-17}\ \text{kg}/\text{s}}
= 2.48 \times 10^{-14}\ \text{s}.
 $$

## Answer
$$ 
\boxed{t = 2.5 \times 10^{-14}\ \text{s}}
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Mean free time of conduction electrons in a metal
- Resistivity relation $r = \dfrac{m}{ne^2 t} $
- Number density of conduction electrons in copper
- Unit conversion for electrical resistance and related quantities
