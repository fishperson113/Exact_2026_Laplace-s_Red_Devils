# Sample Problem 33.01: Light wave: rms values of the electric and magnetic fields

## Problem
When you look at the North Star (Polaris), you intercept light from a star at a distance of 431 ly and emitting energy at a rate of 2.2 × 10^3 times that of our Sun (P_sun = 3.90 × 10^26 W). Find the rms values of the electric and magnetic fields of the light reaching you.

## Key ideas
1. The rms value $E_{\mathrm{rms}} $ of the electric field in light is related to the intensity $I $ of the light via  
   $$ 
   I = \frac{E_{\mathrm{rms}}^2}{c \mu_0}.
    $$
2. Because the source is so far away and emits light with equal intensity in all directions, the intensity $I $ at any distance $r $ from the source is related to the source’s power $P_s $ via  
   $$ 
   I = \frac{P_s}{4\pi r^2}.
    $$
3. The magnitudes of the electric field and magnetic field of an electromagnetic wave at any instant and at any point in the wave are related by the speed of light $c $ according to  
   $$ 
   \frac{E}{B} = c.
    $$
   Thus, the rms values of those fields are also related by the same equation.

## Solution
Electric field: Putting the first two ideas together gives us
$$ 
E_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{I c \mu_0}
 $$
and
$$ 
I = \frac{P_s}{4\pi r^2} = \frac{E_{\mathrm{rms}}^2}{c\mu_0}.
 $$

By substituting $P_s = (2.2 \times 10^3)(3.90 \times 10^{26}\ \text{W}) $, $r = 431\ \text{ly} = 4.08 \times 10^{18}\ \text{m} $, and values for the constants, we find
$$ 
E_{\mathrm{rms}} = 1.24 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{V/m}.
 $$

Magnetic field: From Eq. 33-5, we write
$$ 
B_{\mathrm{rms}} = \frac{E_{\mathrm{rms}}}{c}
 $$
so
$$ 
B_{\mathrm{rms}} = \frac{1.24 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{V/m}}{3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}
= 4.1 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{T}.
 $$

Cannot compare the fields: Note that $E_{\mathrm{rms}} (= 1.2\ \text{mV/m}) $ is small as judged by ordinary laboratory standards, but $B_{\mathrm{rms}} (= 4.1\ \text{pT}) $ is quite small. This difference helps to explain why most instruments used for the detection and measurement of electromagnetic waves are designed to respond to the electric component. It is wrong, however, to say that the electric component of an electromagnetic wave is “stronger” than the magnetic component. You cannot compare quantities that are measured in different units. However, these electric and magnetic components are on an equal basis because their average energies, which can be compared, are equal.

## Answer
$$ 
E_{\mathrm{rms}} = 1.24 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{V/m} \approx 1.2\ \text{mV/m}
 $$
$$ 
B_{\mathrm{rms}} = 4.1 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{T} = 4.1\ \text{pT}
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Intensity of electromagnetic radiation from an isotropic source: $I = P_s / (4\pi r^2) $
- Intensity of a wave in terms of the rms electric field: $I = E_{\mathrm{rms}}^2 /(c\mu_0) $
- Relation between electric and magnetic fields in an electromagnetic wave: $E/B = c $
