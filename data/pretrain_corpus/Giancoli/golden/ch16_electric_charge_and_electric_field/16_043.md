# Problem 16.43

## Question
(III) A point charge Q rests at the center of an uncharged thin spherical conducting shell. (See Fig. 16-34.) What is the electric field E as a function of r (a) for r less than the inner radius of the shell, (b) inside the shell, and (c) beyond the shell? (d) How does the shell affect the field due to Q alone? How does the charge Q affect the shell?

## Final answer from source
(a) (b) 0; (c) (d) The shell causes the field to be 0 in the shell material. The charge polarizes the shell.

## Worked solution
Because the charge $Q $ is at the center of a conducting spherical shell, the situation is spherically symmetric. That means the electric field, if nonzero, must point radially and depend only on $r $.

### (a) Region $r $ less than the inner radius of the shell
Choose a spherical Gaussian surface of radius $r $ lying entirely inside the cavity, centered on $Q $.

By Gauss’s law,
$$ 
\oint \vec E \cdot d\vec A = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}.
 $$
The enclosed charge is just $Q $, so
$$ 
E(4\pi r^2)=\frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0},
 $$
hence
$$ 
E(r)=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2},
 $$
directed radially outward if $Q>0 $ and inward if $Q<0 $.

### (b) Inside the conducting shell material
In electrostatic equilibrium, the electric field inside a conductor is zero. Therefore, throughout the metal of the shell,
$$ 
E=0.
 $$

### (c) Beyond the shell
For a Gaussian surface outside the shell, the enclosed charge is still $Q $, because the shell is initially uncharged and the induced charges on the inner and outer surfaces sum to zero net charge.

Thus Gauss’s law again gives
$$ 
E(4\pi r^2)=\frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0},
 $$
so
$$ 
E(r)=\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2},
 $$
radially outward for $Q>0 $ and inward for $Q<0 $.

### (d) Effect of the shell and of the charge
- The shell makes the electric field zero in the conducting material.
- The charge $Q $ induces charge separation on the shell: $-Q $ appears on the inner surface and $+Q $ on the outer surface, so the shell is polarized.

So the source summary is consistent with the result: the shell cancels the field within its metal, but outside the shell the field is the same as that of a point charge $Q $ at the center.

## Key concepts used
- Gauss’s law
- Spherical symmetry
- Electrostatic equilibrium in conductors
- Induced charge and polarization on conducting shells
