# Problem 16.47

## Question
Measurements indicate that there is an electric field surrounding the Earth. Its magnitude is about at the Earth’s surface and points inward toward the Earth’s center. What is the magnitude of the electric charge on the Earth? Is it positive or negative? [Hint: The electric field outside a uniformly charged sphere is the same as if all the charge were concentrated at its center.]

## Final answer from source
negative.

## Worked solution
Because the Earth’s electric field is given to be the same as that of a uniformly charged sphere, we can treat the Earth as a point charge at its center for points outside the Earth.

For a point charge,
$$ 
E=\frac{k|Q|}{r^2}
 $$
so
$$ 
|Q|=\frac{Er^2}{k}.
 $$

Using the Earth’s radius $r \approx 6.37\times 10^6\ \text{m} $, and the given field magnitude near the surface $E \approx 150\ \text{N/C} $, we obtain
$$ 
|Q|=\frac{(150)(6.37\times 10^6)^2}{8.99\times 10^9}.
 $$

Compute:
$$ 
(6.37\times 10^6)^2 \approx 4.06\times 10^{13},
 $$
so
$$ 
|Q|\approx \frac{150(4.06\times 10^{13})}{8.99\times 10^9}
\approx 6.8\times 10^5\ \text{C}.
 $$

Direction determines the sign: the electric field points inward toward the Earth, so the field is directed toward the charge. Electric field lines point toward negative charge, hence the Earth’s charge must be negative.

Therefore, the Earth’s charge has magnitude
$$ 
|Q|\approx 6.8\times 10^5\ \text{C},
 $$
and it is negative.

## Key concepts used
- Electric field of a spherically symmetric charge distribution
- Point-charge field formula $E = k|Q|/r^2 $
- Direction of electric field lines and charge sign
- Using Earth’s radius as the field point location
