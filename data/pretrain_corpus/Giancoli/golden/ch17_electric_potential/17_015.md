# Problem 17.15

## Question
(II) An electric field greater than about causes air to break down (electrons are removed from the atoms and then recombine, emitting light). See Section 17-2 and Table 17-3. If you shuffle along a carpet and then reach for a doorknob, a spark flies across a gap you estimate to be 1 mm between your finger and the doorknob. Estimate the voltage between your finger and the doorknob. Why is no harm done? 496  Electric Potential

## Final answer from source
3000 V; only a small amount of charge was transferred.

## Worked solution
A spark across air occurs when the electric field is large enough to break down the air. For dry air, the breakdown field is on the order of

$$ 
E \approx 3\times 10^6 \ \text{V/m}.
 $$

The gap is estimated to be

$$ 
d = 1\ \text{mm} = 1\times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}.
 $$

Using the relation between field and potential difference for a roughly uniform field,

$$ 
\Delta V \approx Ed,
 $$

we get

$$ 
\Delta V \approx (3\times 10^6\ \text{V/m})(1\times 10^{-3}\ \text{m})
= 3\times 10^3\ \text{V}.
 $$

So the estimated voltage between your finger and the doorknob is

$$ 
\boxed{3.0\times 10^3\ \text{V} = 3000\ \text{V}}.
 $$

No harm is done because although the voltage is high, the amount of charge transferred in the discharge is very small. The spark lasts for a very short time and moves only a tiny quantity of charge, so the total energy delivered to your body is small.

## Key concepts used
- Electric field breakdown strength of air
- Relation between electric field and potential difference: $\Delta V \approx Ed $
- Unit conversion: $1\ \text{mm} = 10^{-3}\ \text{m} $
- High voltage does not necessarily imply large energy transfer; charge transferred in a static discharge can be small
