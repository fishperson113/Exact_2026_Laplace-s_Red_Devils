# Problem 18.15

## Question
(II) Can a 2.2-mm-diameter copper wire have the same resistance as a tungsten wire of the same length? Give numerical details.

## Final answer from source
Yes, for length 4.0 mm.

## Worked solution
For a wire, the resistance is

$$ 
R=\rho \frac{L}{A}
 $$

where:
- $\rho $ is the resistivity,
- $L $ is the length,
- $A $ is the cross-sectional area.

We want a copper wire and a tungsten wire of the **same length** to have the **same resistance**.

So set the resistances equal:

$$ 
\rho_{\text{Cu}}\frac{L}{A_{\text{Cu}}}
=
\rho_{\text{W}}\frac{L}{A_{\text{W}}}
 $$

Since the lengths are the same, $L $ cancels:

$$ 
\frac{\rho_{\text{Cu}}}{A_{\text{Cu}}}
=
\frac{\rho_{\text{W}}}{A_{\text{W}}}
 $$

Thus,

$$ 
A_{\text{W}} = \frac{\rho_{\text{W}}}{\rho_{\text{Cu}}} A_{\text{Cu}}
 $$

Using standard resistivities:
- copper: $\rho_{\text{Cu}} \approx 1.7\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m} $
- tungsten: $\rho_{\text{W}} \approx 5.6\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m} $

and for the copper wire diameter $d_{\text{Cu}}=2.2\ \text{mm} $, the area is

$$ 
A_{\text{Cu}}=\pi \left(\frac{d_{\text{Cu}}}{2}\right)^2
= \pi (1.1\ \text{mm})^2
 $$

So

$$ 
A_{\text{W}} \approx \frac{5.6}{1.7}A_{\text{Cu}} \approx 3.3A_{\text{Cu}}
 $$

Since $A \propto d^2 $,

$$ 
d_{\text{W}} = d_{\text{Cu}}\sqrt{\frac{\rho_{\text{W}}}{\rho_{\text{Cu}}}}
\approx 2.2\,\text{mm}\times \sqrt{3.3}
\approx 4.0\,\text{mm}
 $$

So yes: a copper wire with diameter $2.2\ \text{mm} $ can have the same resistance as a tungsten wire of the same length, if the tungsten wire’s diameter is about $4.0\ \text{mm} $.

## Key concepts used
- Resistance of a uniform wire: $R=\rho L/A $
- Same length implies resistance comparison depends on $\rho/A $
- Cross-sectional area of a circular wire: $A=\pi(d/2)^2 $
- For equal resistance at equal length, larger resistivity requires larger cross-sectional area
