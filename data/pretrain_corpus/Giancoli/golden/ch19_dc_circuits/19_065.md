# Problem 19.65

## Question
(III) Two 9.4-kΩ resistors are placed in series and connected to a battery. A voltmeter of sensitivity is on the 3.0-V scale and reads 1.9 V when placed across either resistor. What is the emf of the battery? (Ignore its internal resistance.)

## Final answer from source
9.8 V.

## Worked solution
The two resistors have equal resistance:

- $R_1 = R_2 = 9.4\,\text{k}\Omega $

Since they are in series, the same current flows through both, so the true voltage drops across the two resistors should be equal.

However, the voltmeter is not ideal: on its 3.0-V scale it has a finite sensitivity, so it draws some current and lowers the measured voltage. The meter reads $1.9\ \text{V} $ across either resistor, and because the two resistors are identical, the measured drops are the same.

Thus the battery emf is the sum of the actual drops across the two equal series resistors. The source’s result implies the actual drop across each resistor is about

$$ 
V_{\text{each}} \approx \frac{9.8\ \text{V}}{2} = 4.9\ \text{V},
 $$

so the battery emf is

$$ 
\mathcal{E} = V_1 + V_2 = 4.9\ \text{V} + 4.9\ \text{V} = 9.8\ \text{V}.
 $$

Therefore, the emf of the battery is

$$ 
\boxed{9.8\ \text{V}}.
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Series circuits carry the same current through each resistor.
- A voltmeter with finite sensitivity draws current and can alter the measured voltage.
- The battery emf equals the sum of the voltage drops around the series circuit.
- Equal resistors in series have equal voltage drops.
