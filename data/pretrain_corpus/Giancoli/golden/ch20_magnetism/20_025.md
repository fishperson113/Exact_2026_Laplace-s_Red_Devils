# Problem 20.25

## Question
(I) Jumper cables used to start a stalled vehicle often carry a 65-A current. How strong is the magnetic field 4.5 cm from one cable? Compare to the Earth’s magnetic field (5.0 * 10-5 T).

## Final answer from source
about 5.8 times larger.

## Worked solution
For a long straight current-carrying wire, the magnetic field at distance $r $ is

$$ 
B=\frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}
 $$

where
- $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A} $,
- $I = 65\ \text{A} $,
- $r = 4.5\ \text{cm} = 0.045\ \text{m} $.

Substitute:

$$ 
B=\frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(65)}{2\pi(0.045)}
 $$

Cancel $\pi $:

$$ 
B=\frac{2\times 10^{-7}\times 65}{0.045}
 $$

$$ 
B=\frac{1.3\times 10^{-5}}{0.045}
\approx 2.9\times 10^{-4}\ \text{T}
 $$

Now compare this with Earth’s magnetic field, $B_E = 5.0\times 10^{-5}\ \text{T} $:

$$ 
\frac{B}{B_E}=\frac{2.9\times 10^{-4}}{5.0\times 10^{-5}}
\approx 5.8
 $$

So the magnetic field from the jumper cable is about $5.8 $ times larger than Earth’s magnetic field.

## Key concepts used
- Magnetic field of a long straight current-carrying wire
- Superposition/comparison of magnetic field magnitudes
- Unit conversion from cm to m
- Ratio comparison with Earth’s magnetic field
