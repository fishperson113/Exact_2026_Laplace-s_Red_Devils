What can be stated from the physics is:

1. A bar moving through a magnetic field with current in it experiences a magnetic force
   $$ 
   F_B = I L B \sin\theta
    $$
   where:
   - $I $ is the current in the rod,
   - $L $ is the length of the rod in the magnetic field,
   - $B $ is the magnetic field strength,
   - $\theta $ is the angle between the current direction and the magnetic field.

2. To move the rod steadily to the right, the applied external force must balance the magnetic force in magnitude:
   $$ 
   F_{\text{ext}} = F_B
    $$
   and act to the right if the magnetic force acts to the left.

3. The external power required is
   $$ 
   P_{\text{ext}} = F_{\text{ext}}\,v
    $$
   where $v $ is the rod’s speed to the right.

4. In the ideal steady-motion case, this mechanical power is converted into electrical energy at the rate
   $$ 
   P = I\mathcal{E} = IBLv
    $$
   for a rod moving perpendicular to $B $.

So the result is:
- **(a)** The required force equals the magnetic force on the current-carrying rod.
- **(b)** The required power equals the applied force times the speed, $P=Fv $, equivalently $P=IBLv $ for the usual rod-in-field geometry.
