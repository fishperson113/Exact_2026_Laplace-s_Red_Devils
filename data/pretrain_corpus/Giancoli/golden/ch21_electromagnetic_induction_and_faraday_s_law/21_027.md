# Problem 21.27

## Question
(I) A transformer is designed to change 117 V into 13,500 V, and there are 148 turns in the primary coil. How many turns are in the secondary coil?

## Final answer from source
turns.

## Worked solution
For an ideal transformer, the voltages and turns satisfy

$$ 
\frac{V_s}{V_p}=\frac{N_s}{N_p}
 $$

where:
- $V_p = 117\ \text{V} $ is the primary voltage,
- $V_s = 13{,}500\ \text{V} $ is the secondary voltage,
- $N_p = 148 $ turns is the number of primary turns,
- $N_s $ is the number of secondary turns.

Solve for $N_s $:

$$ 
N_s = N_p \frac{V_s}{V_p}
 $$

Substitute the given values:

$$ 
N_s = 148 \times \frac{13{,}500}{117}
 $$

$$ 
N_s \approx 148 \times 115.38 \approx 1.71\times 10^4
 $$

So the secondary coil has about

$$ 
\boxed{1.71\times 10^4\ \text{turns}}
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Ideal transformer relation: $\dfrac{V_s}{V_p}=\dfrac{N_s}{N_p} $
- Step-up transformer has more turns on the secondary than on the primary
- Proportionality between voltage and number of turns
