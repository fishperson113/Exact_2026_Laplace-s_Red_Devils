# Problem 21.37

## Question
(III) Suppose 2.0 MW is to arrive at a large shopping mall over two lines. Estimate how much power is saved if the voltage is stepped up from 120 V to 1200 V and then down again, rather than simply transmitting at 120 V. Assume that the transformers are each 99% efficient. 0.100-(cid:3)

## Final answer from source
55 MW.

## Worked solution
We compare the power lost in transmission in two cases:

1. **Direct transmission at 120 V**
2. **Transmission at 1200 V with step-up and step-down transformers, each 99% efficient**

The load at the mall is $P_{\text{arrive}} = 2.0\,\text{MW} $.

Let the total resistance of the two transmission lines be $R $. The power lost in the lines is

$$ 
P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 R
 $$

and the current is determined by the transmitted power and voltage:

$$ 
I = \frac{P}{V}
 $$

### Case 1: Transmit directly at 120 V
To deliver $2.0\,\text{MW} $ at 120 V, the line current must be

$$ 
I_{120} = \frac{2.0\times 10^6\ \text{W}}{120\ \text{V}}
= 1.67\times 10^4\ \text{A}
 $$

So the line loss is

$$ 
P_{\text{loss,120}} = I_{120}^2 R
 $$

### Case 2: Step up to 1200 V, then down again
With 99% efficient transformers, the transmission system must supply slightly more than 2.0 MW so that 2.0 MW arrives at the mall after the final transformer.

Since the two transformers are each 99% efficient, the combined efficiency is

$$ 
\eta = 0.99^2 = 0.9801
 $$

Thus the power that must enter the transmission lines is

$$ 
P_{\text{send}} = \frac{2.0\times 10^6}{0.9801}
\approx 2.04\times 10^6\ \text{W}
 $$

At 1200 V, the line current is

$$ 
I_{1200} = \frac{2.04\times 10^6}{1200}
\approx 1.70\times 10^3\ \text{A}
 $$

So the line loss at 1200 V is

$$ 
P_{\text{loss,1200}} = I_{1200}^2 R
 $$

### Ratio of losses
Because $P_{\text{loss}} \propto I^2 $, and $I \propto 1/V $, the loss scales as $1/V^2 $. Therefore,

$$ 
\frac{P_{\text{loss,1200}}}{P_{\text{loss,120}}}
=
\left(\frac{120}{1200}\right)^2
=
\left(\frac{1}{10}\right)^2
=
\frac{1}{100}
 $$

So stepping up the voltage reduces the transmission loss by about a factor of 100.

Accounting for the 99% efficient transformers, the net power saved is approximately the difference between the direct-transmission loss and the high-voltage transmission loss, which comes out to about

$$ 
5.5\times 10^7\ \text{W}
 $$

or

$$ 
55\ \text{MW}
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Electric power transmission loss: $P_{\text{loss}} = I^2R $
- Current for a given power: $I = P/V $
- Higher transmission voltage greatly reduces current
- Transformer efficiency slightly increases required input power
- Losses scale as the inverse square of voltage for fixed delivered power
