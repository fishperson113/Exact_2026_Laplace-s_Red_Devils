# 5.3 Elasticity: Stress and Strain

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 5, Section 5.3

## Concept explanation
A deformation is a change in shape caused by an applied force. For small deformations, two key behaviors are observed: the object returns to its original shape when the force is removed, and the deformation is proportional to the applied force. This proportionality is Hooke’s law.

The section treats three kinds of small deformation:
- changes in length due to tension or compression,
- sideways shear deformation,
- changes in volume due to uniform compression.

For changes in length, the deformation depends on the applied force, the material, the original length, and the cross-sectional area. The material property is Young’s modulus. Large Young’s modulus means a material is harder to stretch or compress.

Stress is defined as force per area, and strain is defined as fractional change in length. In this form, the tension/compression relation is analogous to Hooke’s law.

Shear deformation occurs when forces act parallel to a surface and perpendicular to the object’s length. The proportionality constant is the shear modulus.

Bulk deformation describes uniform compression from forces applied evenly on all surfaces. The corresponding material property is the bulk modulus. Liquids and solids are much harder to compress than gases.

The section also notes that bones, tendons, arteries, lungs, skin, and other biological materials show elastic behavior, often with nonlinear stress-strain relations. Tendons have a toe region, linear region, and failure region. Arteries and lungs rely on elasticity for normal function.

## Key formulas
Hooke’s law:
$$ 
F = -kx
 $$
where $x $ is deformation and $F $ is the restoring force.

For changes in length:
$$ 
\Delta L = \frac{F L_0}{A Y}
 $$

Stress:
$$ 
\text{stress} = \frac{F}{A}
 $$

Strain:
$$ 
\text{strain} = \frac{\Delta L}{L_0}
 $$

Shear deformation:
$$ 
\frac{\Delta x}{L_0} = \frac{F}{A S}
 $$

Bulk deformation:
$$ 
\frac{\Delta V}{V_0} = \frac{F}{A B}
 $$

Equivalent Hooke’s-law form for length change:
$$ 
F = \left(\frac{A Y}{L_0}\right)\Delta L
 $$

## Variables and units
- $F $: applied force or restoring force, in newtons (N)
- $k $: spring constant, in newtons per meter (N/m)
- $x $: deformation or displacement, in meters (m)
- $\Delta L $: change in length, in meters (m)
- $L_0 $: original length, in meters (m)
- $A $: cross-sectional area, in square meters (m$^2 $)
- $Y $: Young’s modulus, in pascals (Pa = N/m$^2 $)
- $S $: shear modulus, in pascals (Pa = N/m$^2 $)
- $B $: bulk modulus, in pascals (Pa = N/m$^2 $)
- stress: force per area, in pascals (Pa)
- strain: fractional change in length, unitless
- $\Delta x $: shear deformation, in meters (m)
- $\Delta V $: change in volume, in cubic meters (m$^3 $)
- $V_0 $: original volume, in cubic meters (m$^3 $)

The section states that the ratio $F/A $ is stress, and $\Delta L/L_0 $ is strain. It also states that the force in the length-change relation is the applied force magnitude, with equal and opposite forces acting so the object does not accelerate.

## Worked example
**Example 5.3: The Stretch of a Long Cable**

A suspension cable has an unsupported span of 3020 m. Calculate the amount of stretch in the steel cable. The cable has a diameter of 5.6 cm and can withstand a maximum tension of the given value.

Strategy:
- Use $F $ equal to the maximum tension.
- Compute the cross-sectional area from the cable diameter.
- Use the length-change equation $\Delta L = \frac{F L_0}{A Y} $.

Discussion:
- The stretch is quite large in absolute terms but only about 0.6% of the unsupported length.
- Temperature effects may be important in this environment.

**Example 5.4: Calculating Deformation: How Much Does Your Leg Shorten When You Stand on It?**

A 70.0 kg man supports 62.0 kg of his mass on the femur, modeled as a uniform rod 40.0 cm long and 2.00 cm in radius.

Strategy:
- The force equals the supported weight.
- Use the cross-sectional area of the femur.
- Use the compression value of Young’s modulus for bone.

Discussion:
- The change in length is small, consistent with bones being rigid.

**Example 5.5: Calculating Force Required to Deform: That Nail Does Not Bend Much Under a Load**

Find the mass of the picture hanging from a steel nail when the nail bends only a very small amount.

Strategy:
- The force on the nail is the weight of the picture.
- Solve the shear deformation relation for the force.
- Use the shear modulus for steel and the nail’s radius and measured deformation.

Discussion:
- The mass is fairly large, yet the nail flexes only a tiny amount.

**Example 5.6: Calculating Change in Volume with Deformation: How Much Is Water Compressed at Great Ocean Depths?**

Calculate the fractional decrease in volume of seawater at 5.00 km depth, where the force per unit area is given.

Strategy:
- Use the bulk deformation relation.
- Solve for the fractional change in volume.
- Substitute the bulk modulus of water.

Discussion:
- The decrease in volume is measurable but very small, showing that liquids and solids are extraordinarily difficult to compress.
