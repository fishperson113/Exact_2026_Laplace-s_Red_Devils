# 6.3 Centripetal Force

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 6, Section 6.3

## Concept explanation
Any force or combination of forces can produce a centripetal, or radial, acceleration. Examples given include the tension in a rope on a tether ball, Earth’s gravitational force on the Moon, friction between roller skates and a rink floor, the force from a banked roadway on a car, and forces on the tube of a spinning centrifuge.

Any net force causing uniform circular motion is called a centripetal force. Its direction is toward the center of curvature, the same direction as the centripetal acceleration. By Newton’s second law, the net force is mass times acceleration, so for uniform circular motion the centripetal force has the same direction as the centripetal acceleration and the same dependence on mass and acceleration.

Using the centripetal-acceleration expressions from the preceding section, the centripetal force can be written in terms of mass, velocity, angular velocity, and radius of curvature. These expressions may be used interchangeably, depending on which quantities are known.

A centripetal force is always perpendicular to the velocity and points to the center of curvature. Solving the expression for centripetal force for radius shows that, for a given mass and speed, a larger centripetal force corresponds to a smaller radius of curvature, meaning a tighter curve.

For a car on a flat, unbanked curve, static friction provides the centripetal force. The required coefficient of friction can be found by setting the frictional force equal to the centripetal force. The example in the source shows that the required coefficient may be much smaller than typical tire-road values, and that mass cancels because friction is assumed proportional to the normal force, which is proportional to mass.

For a banked curve, the slope of the road helps the car negotiate the turn. For an ideally banked curve, the car can take the turn at a certain speed without friction. In that case, the horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force, while the vertical component balances the car’s weight. The ideal banking angle depends on speed and radius, and does not depend on the vehicle’s mass.

## Key formulas
$$ 
F_c = m a_c
 $$

$$ 
a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}
 $$

$$ 
a_c = r\omega^2
 $$

$$ 
F_c = m\frac{v^2}{r}
 $$

$$ 
F_c = mr\omega^2
 $$

$$ 
r = \frac{mv^2}{F_c}
 $$

For a flat curve with static friction providing the centripetal force:
$$ 
f_s^{\max} = \mu_s N
 $$

On level ground:
$$ 
N = mg
 $$

So for the unbanked curve example:
$$ 
F_c = \mu_s mg
 $$

For an ideally banked curve:
$$ 
F_c = N\sin\theta
 $$

$$ 
N\cos\theta = mg
 $$

Combining these gives:
$$ 
\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}
 $$

and equivalently:
$$ 
v = \sqrt{rg\tan\theta}
 $$

## Variables and units
- $F_c $: centripetal force, in newtons (N)
- $a_c $: centripetal acceleration, in meters per second squared (m/s$^2 $)
- $m $: mass, in kilograms (kg)
- $v $: speed, in meters per second (m/s)
- $r $: radius of curvature, in meters (m)
- $\omega $: angular velocity, in radians per second (rad/s)
- $f_s^{\max} $: maximum static friction force, in newtons (N)
- $\mu_s $: coefficient of static friction, unitless
- $N $: normal force, in newtons (N)
- $g $: acceleration due to gravity, in meters per second squared (m/s$^2 $)
- $\theta $: bank angle, in degrees or radians depending on context

Radians are unitless.

## Worked example
### Example 6.4: What Coefficient of Friction Do Car Tires Need on a Flat Curve?

A 900 kg car negotiates a 500 m radius curve at 25.0 m/s.

**(a) Calculate the centripetal force.**

Using
$$ 
F_c = m\frac{v^2}{r}
 $$
substitute the known values:
$$ 
F_c = 900\ \text{kg}\cdot \frac{(25.0\ \text{m/s})^2}{500\ \text{m}}
 $$

This gives the centripetal force exerted on the car.

**(b) Find the minimum static coefficient of friction on an unbanked curve.**

For a level curve, the frictional force supplies the centripetal force:
$$ 
F_c = f_s^{\max} = \mu_s N
 $$
and
$$ 
N = mg
 $$

So:
$$ 
F_c = \mu_s mg
 $$

Solving for the coefficient of static friction:
$$ 
\mu_s = \frac{F_c}{mg}
 $$

Substituting the known quantities yields:
$$ 
\mu_s = 0.13
 $$

The source notes that coefficients of friction are approximate, so the answer is given to two digits.

### Example 6.5: What Is the Ideal Speed to Take a Steeply Banked Tight Curve?

A curve of radius 100 m is banked at $65.0^\circ $, and the road is frictionless.

Using the ideal-bank relation:
$$ 
v = \sqrt{rg\tan\theta}
 $$

Substitute:
$$ 
v = \sqrt{(100\ \text{m})(9.80\ \text{m/s}^2)\tan 65.0^\circ}
 $$

Since
$$ 
\tan 65.0^\circ = 2.14
 $$
the speed is about:
$$ 
v \approx 165\ \text{km/h}
 $$

This is consistent with a very steeply banked, rather sharp curve.

## Key concepts used
- Centripetal force is the net force required for uniform circular motion.
- The force points toward the center of curvature.
- Newton’s second law connects force and centripetal acceleration.
- Centripetal acceleration can be written as $v^2/r $ or $r\omega^2 $.
- On an unbanked curve, static friction can supply the centripetal force.
- On an ideally banked curve, the normal force provides the centripetal force without friction.
- For banked curves, the ideal speed depends on radius, gravity, and bank angle, but not on mass.
