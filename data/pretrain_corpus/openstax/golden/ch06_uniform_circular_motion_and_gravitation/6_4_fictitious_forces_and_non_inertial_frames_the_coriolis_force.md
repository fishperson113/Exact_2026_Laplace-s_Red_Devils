# 6.4 Fictitious Forces and Non-inertial Frames: The Coriolis Force

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 6, Section 6.4

## Concept explanation
A fictitious force is an unreal force that appears when motion is described from an accelerating or rotating frame of reference. Examples include the backward push felt during airplane takeoff, the sideways “throw” felt in a turning car, the outward push felt on a merry-go-round, and the deflection of moving air in large-scale weather systems.

An inertial frame of reference is one in which all forces are real and have identifiable physical origins. Earth is very nearly an inertial frame for many everyday situations. A non-inertial frame of reference is accelerating or rotating, so fictitious forces may be introduced to explain observed motion.

In a turning car, a passenger may feel a force to the left when the car turns right. In the Earth frame, the passenger continues in a straight line while the car moves right; there is no real force to the left on the passenger. The force felt in the car frame is fictitious.

On a rotating merry-go-round, a rider feels a centrifugal force pulling outward. In Earth’s inertial frame, there is no real outward force; the rider’s inertia tends to carry the rider in a straight line, while the rotating platform supplies the centripetal force needed for circular motion.

The Coriolis force is another fictitious force used in rotating frames. If a ball is slid straight outward on a rotating merry-go-round, it follows a straight path in Earth’s frame but appears to curve to the right in the rotating frame. The Coriolis force is introduced to explain this curvature in the non-inertial frame.

Earth’s rotation also produces Coriolis effects, especially for large-scale motions such as winds and weather systems. Viewed from above the North Pole, Earth rotates counterclockwise. In the northern hemisphere, motion experiences a Coriolis deflection to the right; in the southern hemisphere, the deflection is to the left. Because Earth’s angular velocity is small, the Coriolis force is usually negligible for everyday motion but is important for large-scale atmospheric flows.

Tropical cyclones, hurricanes, and typhoons are low-pressure storm systems with strong winds and heavy rain. Air flows toward low pressure, but in the northern hemisphere the Coriolis force deflects this inward flow to the right, producing counterclockwise circulation at the surface. High-pressure systems rotate clockwise in the northern hemisphere because outward-flowing air is also deflected to the right. In the southern hemisphere the opposite rotation occurs.

In an inertial frame, inertia and real forces explain the motion without introducing fictitious forces. In a non-inertial frame, fictitious forces such as the centrifugal force and Coriolis force allow Newton’s laws to be applied, but these forces have no identifiable physical source.

## Variables and units
- Inertial frame of reference: frame in which all forces are real.
- Non-inertial frame of reference: accelerating or rotating frame.
- Centripetal force: real force directed toward the center of circular motion.
- Centrifugal force: fictitious outward force in a rotating frame.
- Coriolis force: fictitious force that deflects moving objects in a rotating frame.
- Angular velocity: the rate of rotation; Earth’s angular velocity is described as small in this section.
- Northern hemisphere: Coriolis deflection is to the right.
- Southern hemisphere: Coriolis deflection is to the left.

## Key concepts used
- Fictitious forces arise from non-inertial frames.
- Newton’s first law explains straight-line motion in an inertial frame.
- Centrifugal force is a fictitious force in rotating frames.
- Coriolis force explains curved paths in rotating frames.
- Earth is approximately inertial for many purposes, but rotation matters for large-scale motion.
- Northern hemisphere deflection is to the right; southern hemisphere deflection is to the left.
- Low-pressure systems draw air inward and can produce cyclonic rotation.
- Real forces have identifiable physical origins; fictitious forces do not.
