# 6.5 Newton’s Universal Law of Gravitation

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 6, Section 6.5

## Concept explanation
Newton’s universal law of gravitation states that every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force along the line joining them. The force is always attractive, depends only on the masses involved and the distance between them, and is directly proportional to the product of the masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

For large bodies, each object is treated as if its entire mass were concentrated at its center of mass. For two bodies with masses $m_1 $ and $m_2 $ separated by distance $r $ between their centers of mass, the gravitational force magnitude is
$$ 
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}.
 $$
The force on each object has the same magnitude, consistent with Newton’s third law.

At Earth’s surface, an object’s weight is the gravitational force between the object and Earth. Substituting $mg $ for the gravitational force and using Earth’s mass and radius gives the acceleration due to gravity near Earth’s surface, $g \approx 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2 $, independent of the object’s mass.

Newton’s law also explains the Moon’s orbit: the gravitational acceleration due to Earth at the Moon’s distance is nearly equal to the Moon’s required centripetal acceleration. This agreement is within less than 1% because the Moon’s orbit is slightly elliptical and Earth-Moon system motion about the common center of mass is not neglected.

The Moon’s gravity also produces ocean tides. Water on the side of Earth nearest the Moon is pulled more strongly than Earth is, and Earth is pulled more strongly than the water on the far side, producing two tidal bulges. The Sun also affects tides, but with about half the Moon’s effect. Spring tides occur when Earth, the Moon, and the Sun are aligned; neap tides occur when the Sun is at a right angle to the Earth-Moon alignment.

“Weightlessness” in orbit does not mean zero gravity. It means the astronaut is in free fall, accelerating with the acceleration due to gravity. Microgravity refers to an environment where the apparent net acceleration is small compared with that at Earth’s surface.

The universal gravitational constant $G $ was first determined accurately by Henry Cavendish in 1798. Measuring $G $ allowed the mass of Earth to be determined from Newton’s law of gravitation.

## Key formulas
$$ 
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
 $$

$$ 
g = \frac{G M_E}{r^2}
 $$

$$ 
mg = G \frac{m M_E}{r^2}
 $$

$$ 
a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}
 $$

$$ 
a_c = r\omega^2
 $$

$$ 
\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}
 $$

## Variables and units
- $F $: gravitational force magnitude, in newtons (N)
- $G $: universal gravitational constant, SI units
- $m_1, m_2, m $: masses, in kilograms (kg)
- $r $: distance between centers of mass, in meters (m)
- $M_E $: mass of Earth, in kilograms (kg)
- $g $: acceleration due to gravity, in meters per second squared (m/s$^2 $)
- $a_c $: centripetal acceleration, in meters per second squared (m/s$^2 $)
- $v $: speed, in meters per second (m/s)
- $\omega $: angular velocity, in radians per second (rad/s)
- $T $: period, in seconds (s)

## Worked example
**Example 6.6: Earth’s gravitational force is the centripetal force making the Moon move in a curved path**

(a) Find the acceleration due to Earth’s gravity at the distance of the Moon.

Use
$$ 
g = \frac{G M_E}{r^2},
 $$
with $r $ equal to the Earth-Moon distance, the distance from the center of Earth to the center of the Moon. This gives the acceleration due to gravity at the Moon’s orbit.

(b) Calculate the centripetal acceleration needed to keep the Moon in its orbit, assuming a circular orbit about a fixed Earth, and compare it with part (a).

Use
$$ 
a_c = r\omega^2
 $$
with
$$ 
\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}.
 $$
Given the Moon’s orbital period $T = 27.3\ \text{days} $, the centripetal acceleration can be found from the orbital radius and angular velocity.

Discussion: The centripetal acceleration of the Moon differs by less than 1% from the acceleration due to Earth’s gravity at the Moon’s distance. The agreement is approximate because the Moon’s orbit is slightly elliptical and Earth is not stationary. This supports the conclusion that Earth’s gravitational force causes the Moon to orbit Earth.
