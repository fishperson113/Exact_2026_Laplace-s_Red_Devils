# 7.2 Kinetic Energy and the Work-Energy Theorem

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 7, Section 7.2

## Concept explanation
Work transfers energy into or out of a system. The form that energy takes depends on the situation. For example, if a lawn mower is pushed at constant speed, the energy put into the mower is removed by friction and eventually leaves the system as heat transfer. In contrast, work done lifting a briefcase up stairs can be stored in the briefcase-Earth system and recovered later. Similarly, lifting stone blocks during the building of the pyramids stored energy in the stone-Earth system.

Net work is the sum of the work done on an object. For a force acting at an angle to the displacement, only the component of the force parallel to the displacement contributes to the work. In general, work can be found from the area under a force-versus-displacement graph. If the force is constant, the area is a rectangle; if the force varies, the total work is the sum of areas of thin strips under the curve.

In one-dimensional motion, when a force accelerates an object parallel to its displacement, the net work is the work done by the net force. In the example of a package pushed along a roller belt, gravity and the normal force do no work because they are perpendicular to the displacement. The net work comes from the applied force and friction.

The work-energy theorem states that the net work on a system equals the change in the quantity $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 $. This quantity is defined as translational kinetic energy. Kinetic energy is associated with translational motion of a particle, single body, or system of objects moving together. Since kinetic energy is proportional to speed squared, doubling speed increases kinetic energy by a factor of four.

## Key formulas
Net work for a force at angle $\theta $ to the displacement:
$$ 
W_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{net}}d\cos\theta
 $$

Work-energy theorem:
$$ 
W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K
 $$

Translational kinetic energy:
$$ 
K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
 $$

For constant force parallel to displacement in one dimension:
$$ 
W_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{net}}d
 $$

For a constant-acceleration relation used in the derivation:
$$ 
v^2 - v_0^2 = 2ad
 $$

## Variables and units
- $W_{\text{net}} $: net work, unit joule (J)
- $F_{\text{net}} $: net force, unit newton (N)
- $F $: force, unit newton (N)
- $d $: displacement, unit meter (m)
- $\theta $: angle between force vector and displacement vector, unitless angle
- $K $: kinetic energy, unit joule (J)
- $\Delta K $: change in kinetic energy, unit joule (J)
- $m $: mass, unit kilogram (kg)
- $v $: speed, unit meter per second (m/s)
- $v_0 $: initial speed, unit meter per second (m/s)
- $a $: acceleration, unit meter per second squared (m/s$^2 $)

## Worked example
Example 7.2: Calculating the Kinetic Energy of a Package

A $30.0 $-kg package is moving at $0.500\ \text{m/s} $. Its kinetic energy is
$$ 
K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
 $$
Substituting the known values:
$$ 
K = \frac{1}{2}(30.0\ \text{kg})(0.500\ \text{m/s})^2
 $$
$$ 
K = 3.75\ \text{J}
 $$

Example 7.3: Determining the Work to Accelerate a Package

A $30.0 $-kg package is pushed with a constant force of $120\ \text{N} $ through a distance of $0.800\ \text{m} $, while the average friction force is $5.00\ \text{N} $.

For the net force:
$$ 
F_{\text{net}} = 120\ \text{N} - 5.00\ \text{N} = 115\ \text{N}
 $$

The net work is:
$$ 
W_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{net}}d = (115\ \text{N})(0.800\ \text{m}) = 92.0\ \text{J}
 $$

The work done by the applied force is:
$$ 
W_{\text{app}} = (120\ \text{N})(0.800\ \text{m}) = 96.0\ \text{J}
 $$

The work done by friction is:
$$ 
W_f = -(5.00\ \text{N})(0.800\ \text{m}) = -4.00\ \text{J}
 $$

The total work is:
$$ 
W_{\text{tot}} = 96.0\ \text{J} + (-4.00\ \text{J}) = 92.0\ \text{J}
 $$

Example 7.4: Determining Speed from Work and Energy

Using the work-energy theorem,
$$ 
W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K
 $$
$$ 
92.0\ \text{J} = \frac{1}{2}m v^2 - \frac{1}{2}m v_0^2
 $$
The package starts from rest, so $v_0 = 0 $. Thus,
$$ 
92.0\ \text{J} = \frac{1}{2}(30.0\ \text{kg})v^2
 $$
Solving for $v $:
$$ 
v = \sqrt{\frac{2(92.0\ \text{J})}{30.0\ \text{kg}}} = 2.48\ \text{m/s}
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Work transfers energy into or out of a system.
- Net work is the sum of the work done by all forces.
- Only the force component parallel to the displacement does work.
- The area under a force-versus-displacement graph represents work.
- Gravity and the normal force do no work when perpendicular to the displacement.
- Friction does negative work when it opposes the motion.
- The work-energy theorem relates net work to the change in kinetic energy.
- Translational kinetic energy is $K=\frac{1}{2}mv^2 $.
