# 7.3 Gravitational Potential Energy

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 7, Section 7.3

## Concept explanation
Gravitational potential energy is the stored energy associated with the separation between objects that attract each other by the gravitational force. Near Earth’s surface, it is most often discussed for the Earth-object system.

Work done against gravity increases gravitational potential energy. If an object of mass $m $ is lifted straight up at constant speed through a height $\Delta y $, the required upward force equals its weight, $F = mg $. The work done on the object-Earth system becomes gravitational potential energy.

Potential energy is a property of a system, not a single object. For gravitational potential energy, the choice of zero level is arbitrary; Earth’s surface is commonly chosen as the reference point. Only differences in gravitational potential energy matter physically, because they determine the work done by or against gravity.

When an object falls, gravitational potential energy can be converted into kinetic energy. For small changes in height near Earth’s surface, the change in gravitational potential energy depends only on the change in vertical position, not on the path taken. This path independence is one of the reasons gravitational potential energy is useful for simplifying problems.

## Key formulas
$$ 
W = Fd\cos\theta
 $$

$$ 
\Delta U_g = mg\Delta y
 $$

For an object raised a height $h $ near Earth’s surface:
$$ 
\Delta U_g = mgh
 $$

For a falling object, the loss in gravitational potential energy can become kinetic energy:
$$ 
\Delta K = -\Delta U_g
 $$

If work done by friction is negligible and only gravity changes the energy, then:
$$ 
K_i + U_{g,i} = K_f + U_{g,f}
 $$

## Variables and units
- $U_g $: gravitational potential energy, unit joule (J)
- $\Delta U_g $: change in gravitational potential energy, unit joule (J)
- $m $: mass, unit kilogram (kg)
- $g $: acceleration due to gravity near Earth’s surface, unit $\text{m/s}^2 $
- $\Delta y $: change in height, unit meter (m)
- $h $: height change, unit meter (m)
- $W $: work, unit joule (J)
- $F $: force, unit newton (N)
- $d $: displacement, unit meter (m)
- $\theta $: angle between force and displacement

Notes:
- $\Delta y > 0 $ when the final height is higher than the initial height.
- $\Delta y < 0 $ when the object moves downward.
- The reference level for $U_g = 0 $ is arbitrary.

## Worked example
### Example 7.6: The Force to Stop Falling
A $60.0 $-kg person jumps onto the floor from a height of $3.00 $ m. If he lands stiffly, with knee joints compressing by $0.500 $ cm, calculate the force on the knee joints.

Given:
- $m = 60.0 $ kg
- fall height $h = 3.00 $ m
- stopping distance $d = 0.500 $ cm $= 0.00500 $ m

The person loses gravitational potential energy while falling:
$$ 
\Delta U_g = -mgh
 $$

That lost potential energy becomes kinetic energy just before landing. The floor then does negative work to remove that kinetic energy over the stopping distance:
$$ 
W = Fd\cos 180^\circ = -Fd
 $$

Setting the stopping work equal to the kinetic energy to be removed gives a very large force on the knees. Using the values in the source section, the result is:
$$ 
F = 5.88 \times 10^4\ \text{N}
 $$

This is about 500 times the person’s weight.

### Example 7.7: Finding the Speed of a Roller Coaster from its Height
A roller coaster starts from rest at the top of a $20.0 $-m hill and friction is negligible.

Using conservation of mechanical energy:
$$ 
K_i + U_{g,i} = K_f + U_{g,f}
 $$

Since it starts from rest, $K_i = 0 $. The drop in gravitational potential energy becomes kinetic energy at the bottom:
$$ 
mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
 $$

Mass cancels, so:
$$ 
v = \sqrt{2gh}
 $$

For $h = 20.0 $ m:
$$ 
v = 19.8\ \text{m/s}
 $$

If the initial speed is $5.00 $ m/s, then:
$$ 
\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 + mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2
 $$

Mass cancels again, and the final speed is:
$$ 
v_f = 20.4\ \text{m/s}
 $$

## Key concepts used
- Work done against gravity becomes gravitational potential energy.
- Gravitational potential energy is associated with the Earth-object system.
- Only changes in gravitational potential energy matter, not the absolute zero level.
- Near Earth’s surface, $\Delta U_g = mg\Delta y $.
- Gravity is approximately path independent for small height changes near Earth’s surface.
- When friction is negligible, loss of gravitational potential energy becomes kinetic energy.
- Energy methods can be simpler than force-and-kinematics methods.
