# 7.4 Conservative Forces and Potential Energy

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 7, Section 7.4

## Concept explanation
A conservative force is a force, like gravity or an ideal spring force, for which the work done by or against the force depends only on the starting and ending points of the motion, not on the path taken.

Potential energy is energy a system has due to position, shape, or configuration. It is stored energy that is completely recoverable.

For any conservative force, we can define a corresponding potential energy. The work done against a conservative force to reach a final configuration depends only on that configuration, not on the path followed, and is equal to the potential energy added.

A spring provides a key example. When a spring obeys Hooke’s law, the force needed to stretch or compress it increases linearly with displacement from its undeformed position. Because the force is conservative, the work done in deforming the spring is stored as spring potential energy.

If only conservative forces act on a system, the work-energy theorem leads to conservation of mechanical energy. In that case, kinetic energy and potential energy can change form, but their sum remains constant. This principle applies only when nonconservative effects such as friction are negligible.

Mechanical energy is the total kinetic plus potential energy of a system.

## Key formulas
- Hooke’s law force magnitude:
  $$ 
  F = kx
   $$
  where $k $ is the spring’s force constant and $x $ is the deformation.

- Work done in stretching or compressing a Hooke’s-law spring:
  $$ 
  W = \frac{1}{2}kx^2
   $$

- Spring potential energy:
  $$ 
  U = \frac{1}{2}kx^2
   $$

- Work-energy theorem:
  $$ 
  W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K
   $$

- For only conservative forces:
  $$ 
  W_{\text{cons}} = -\Delta U
   $$

- Conservation of mechanical energy:
  $$ 
  K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f
   $$

- Mechanical energy:
  $$ 
  E_{\text{mech}} = K + U
   $$

## Variables and units
- $F $: force, newtons (N)
- $k $: spring force constant, N/m
- $x $: displacement from undeformed position, meters (m)
- $W $: work, joules (J)
- $U $: potential energy, joules (J)
- $K $: kinetic energy, joules (J)
- $E_{\text{mech}} $: mechanical energy, joules (J)
- $i, f $: initial and final values
- Height is denoted by $h $ in the worked example, measured in meters (m)
- Spring compression is denoted by $x $, measured in meters (m)

## Worked example
A 0.100-kg toy car is propelled by a compressed spring. The track rises 0.180 m above the starting point. The spring is compressed 4.00 cm and has force constant $k = 250.0\ \text{N/m} $. Assuming friction is negligible, find:

(a) the car’s speed just after it leaves the spring  
(b) the car’s speed at the top of the slope

Using conservation of mechanical energy, the spring’s initial potential energy is converted first to kinetic energy and then partly to gravitational potential energy.

- For part (a), taking the initial height as zero and using that the car starts from rest and the spring is initially compressed, the energy equation reduces to the conversion of spring potential energy into kinetic energy.
- For part (b), the same initial spring potential energy is partly converted into gravitational potential energy and partly into kinetic energy at the top of the slope.
