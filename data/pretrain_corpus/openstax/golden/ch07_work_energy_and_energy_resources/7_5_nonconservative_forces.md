# 7.5 Nonconservative Forces

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 7, Section 7.5

## Concept explanation
Forces are either conservative or nonconservative. A nonconservative force is one for which work depends on the path taken. Friction is a good example of a nonconservative force. Because the work done against friction depends on the length of the path between the starting and ending points, there is no potential energy associated with nonconservative forces.

An important characteristic of nonconservative forces is that the work they do adds or removes mechanical energy from a system. Friction converts mechanical energy into thermal energy that dissipates, removing energy from the system. Even if thermal energy is retained or captured, it cannot be fully converted back to work, so it is lost or not recoverable in that sense.

Mechanical energy may not be conserved when nonconservative forces act. For example, when a car is brought to a stop by friction on level ground, it loses kinetic energy, which is dissipated as thermal energy, reducing its mechanical energy.

## Key formulas
The work-energy theorem states that the net work on a system equals the change in its kinetic energy.

When both conservative and nonconservative forces act, the net work is the sum of the work by nonconservative forces and the work by conservative forces.

The work done by conservative forces comes from a loss of gravitational potential energy, so the change in mechanical energy is equal to the work done by nonconservative forces:

$$ 
\Delta E_{\text{mech}} = W_{\text{nc}}
 $$

Rearranging gives:

$$ 
W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta E_{\text{mech}}
 $$

If $W_{\text{nc}} > 0 $, mechanical energy increases. If $W_{\text{nc}} < 0 $, mechanical energy decreases. If $W_{\text{nc}} = 0 $, mechanical energy is conserved.

For the sliding baseball player on level ground, friction removes the initial kinetic energy by doing negative work:

$$ 
W_f = -fd
 $$

and the work-energy relation becomes:

$$ 
-fd = \Delta K
 $$

For the incline case, the change in mechanical energy is written using gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy:

$$ 
W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta K + \Delta U
 $$

## Variables and units
- $W_{\text{nc}} $: total work done by nonconservative forces, units of joules (J)
- $W_f $: work done by friction, units of joules (J)
- $\Delta E_{\text{mech}} $: change in mechanical energy, units of joules (J)
- $\Delta K $: change in kinetic energy, units of joules (J)
- $\Delta U $: change in potential energy, units of joules (J)
- $f $: friction force, units of newtons (N)
- $d $: distance traveled, units of meters (m)
- $m $: mass, units of kilograms (kg)
- $v $: speed, units of meters per second (m/s)

## Worked example
A 65.0-kg baseball player slides to a stop on level ground with an initial speed of 6.00 m/s. The force of friction is constant at 450 N. The player stops when friction removes all of the initial kinetic energy.

Using the work-energy relation for friction:

$$ 
-fd = \Delta K
 $$

Since the final speed is zero,

$$ 
\Delta K = 0 - \frac{1}{2}mv^2
 $$

so

$$ 
-fd = -\frac{1}{2}mv^2
 $$

and therefore

$$ 
d = \frac{\frac{1}{2}mv^2}{f}
 $$

Substituting the values gives the stopping distance.

A second example considers the same player sliding up an incline. The friction force is still 450 N, but the player also gains gravitational potential energy as he moves uphill. The work done by friction reduces the initial kinetic energy to the final mechanical energy at the stopping point. The source states this can be written by including both the kinetic and potential energy changes, and solving for the distance traveled along the hill.
