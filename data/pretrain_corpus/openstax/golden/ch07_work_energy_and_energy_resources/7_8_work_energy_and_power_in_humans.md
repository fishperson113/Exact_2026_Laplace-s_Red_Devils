# 7.8 Work, Energy, and Power in Humans

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 7, Section 7.8

## Concept explanation
Our bodies act as energy conversion machines. The chemical energy stored in food is converted into work, thermal energy, and stored chemical energy in fatty tissue. The fraction going into each form depends on how much we eat and on physical activity.

The rate at which the body uses food energy to sustain life and perform activities is called the metabolic rate. The total energy conversion rate of a person at rest is the basal metabolic rate (BMR). The BMR is divided among body systems; the largest fraction goes to the liver and spleen, followed by the brain. During vigorous exercise, the energy consumption of skeletal muscles and the heart increases markedly.

About 75% of the calories burned in a day go into basic functions. The BMR depends on age, gender, total body weight, and muscle mass. Athletes have a greater BMR because muscle burns more calories than body fat.

Energy consumption is directly proportional to oxygen consumption because digestion is fundamentally the oxidation of food. Therefore, oxygen use can be measured to determine energy use during activities. Approximately 20 kJ of energy are produced for each liter of oxygen consumed, independent of food type.

Useful work is work done on the outside world, such as lifting weights. It requires a force exerted through a distance on the outside world, so it excludes internal work such as the heart pumping blood. Useful work includes climbing stairs and accelerating to a full run because these involve forces exerted on the outside world. Forces exerted by the body are nonconservative and can change the mechanical energy of the system acted upon.

If a person needs more energy than they consume, the body draws upon chemical energy stored in fat. Exercise can therefore reduce body fat, although the amount of exercise needed to burn a given amount of energy can be large. When the body uses more energy than it consumes, the missing energy comes from metabolizing body fat.

All bodily functions require energy. Many small muscle actions during quiet activity become thermal energy, as do the activities of the heart, lungs, and digestive tract. Shivering is an involuntary response to low body temperature in which muscles work against one another to produce thermal energy and do no work. A large fraction of energy is also used to maintain electrical potentials in living cells; this bioelectrical energy ultimately becomes mostly thermal energy, though some is used in chemical processes such as those in the kidneys and liver and in fat production.

## Key formulas
- Metabolic rate: rate at which the body uses food energy.
- Basal metabolic rate (BMR): total energy conversion rate at rest.
- Energy from oxygen use:
  - approximately **20 kJ per liter of O₂ consumed**
- For activity energy use:
  - **energy = power × time**
- For fat loss calculations:
  - body fat energy content assumed to be **39 kJ/g**

## Variables and units
- **Power**: watts (W)
- **Energy**: joules (J) or kilojoules (kJ); calories (kcal) also appear in the source
- **Time**: seconds (s), minutes (min), or days (as stated)
- **Oxygen consumption**: liters of O₂ per minute (L O₂/min) or milliliters per minute (mL/min)
- **BMR**: watts (W)
- **Body fat energy content**: 39 kJ/g
- Example activity values from the source:
  - Cycling at moderate speed: **400 W**
  - Energy used per day in the example: **12,000 kJ/day** and **13,000 kJ/day**
  - Extra energy to work off: **1000 kJ**

## Worked example
**Example 7.13: Calculating Weight Loss from Exercising**

If a person who normally requires an average of **12,000 kJ** (3000 kcal) of food energy per day consumes **13,000 kJ per day**, they will steadily add body fat. How much bicycling per day is required to work off this extra **1000 kJ**?

**Solution**

Table 7.5 states that **400 W** are used when cycling at a moderate speed. The time required to work off **1000 kJ** at this rate is found from energy = power × time.

If the person uses **13,000 kJ** but consumes only **12,000 kJ**, the amount of fat loss is determined by assuming the energy content of fat is **39 kJ/g**.

## Key concepts used
- Chemical energy in food is transformed into work, thermal energy, and stored fat.
- Basal metabolic rate is the energy use at rest.
- Oxygen consumption is proportional to energy consumption.
- Useful work is work done on the outside world, not internal body work.
- Body fat is used when energy expenditure exceeds energy intake.
- Much of the body’s energy use becomes thermal energy.
- Electrical potentials in cells require significant energy expenditure.
