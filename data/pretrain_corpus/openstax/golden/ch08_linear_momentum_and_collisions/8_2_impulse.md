# 8.2 Impulse

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 8, Section 8.2

## Concept explanation
The effect of a force on an object depends on both how large the force is and how long it acts. A large force acting for a short time can produce the same change in momentum as a smaller force acting for a longer time.

The change in momentum is equal to the average net external force multiplied by the time interval over which the force acts. This quantity is called impulse. Impulse is the same as the change in momentum.

An understanding of impulse explains many real-world safety features. Dashboard padding and airbags increase the time over which the force acts during a sudden stop, reducing the force on occupants while producing the same momentum change. Car crumple zones reduce the force on the car in a collision by increasing collision time. Rolling after a jump, or landing on a soft mat, increases the time over which the ground force acts and reduces the force on the body.

For a force that varies with time, the average effective force can be used to produce the same impulse as the actual time-varying force. The area under a force-versus-time graph equals the impulse, and therefore equals the change in momentum.

## Key formulas
$$ 
\Delta \vec{p} = \vec{F}_{\text{ave}} \Delta t
 $$

$$ 
\vec{J} = \Delta \vec{p}
 $$

Where force varies with time, the impulse is also equal to the area under the force-time curve.

## Variables and units
- $\Delta \vec{p} $: change in momentum, units of $\text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s} $
- $\vec{F}_{\text{ave}} $: average net external force, units of newtons (N)
- $\Delta t $: time interval over which the force acts, units of seconds (s)
- $\vec{J} $: impulse, units of $\text{N} \cdot \text{s} $, equivalent to $\text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s} $

## Worked example
### Example 8.3 Calculating Magnitudes of Impulses: Two Billiard Balls Striking a Rigid Wall
Two identical billiard balls strike a rigid wall with the same speed, and are reflected without any change of speed. The first ball strikes perpendicular to the wall. The second ball strikes the wall at an angle of perpendicular and bounces off at an angle of perpendicular to the wall.

(a) Determine the direction of the force on the wall due to each ball.

(b) Calculate the ratio of the magnitudes of impulses on the two balls by the wall.

**Solution summary for (a):**
- The first ball reverses its momentum perpendicular to the wall, so the force on the ball from the wall is along the negative direction normal to the wall.
- By Newton’s third law, the force on the wall due to the first ball is along the positive normal direction.
- The second ball changes only its momentum component normal to the wall; its component along the wall does not change.
- Therefore, the force on the wall due to the second ball is also normal to the wall in the positive direction.

**Solution summary for (b):**
- Impulse equals change in momentum.
- For the first ball, the impulse magnitude is based on reversal of the full normal momentum component.
- For the second ball, only the normal component of momentum changes sign; the parallel component is unchanged.
- The ratio of the magnitudes of the impulses is determined from the change in the normal momentum component.

## Key concepts used
- Impulse is equal to change in momentum.
- The effect of a force depends on both magnitude and duration.
- Average effective force can replace a time-varying force over the same time interval.
- The impulse from a force-time graph equals the area under the curve.
- Newton’s second law relates force and momentum change.
- Newton’s third law determines the direction of the force on the wall from the force on the ball.
