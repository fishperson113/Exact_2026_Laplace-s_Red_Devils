# 8.5 Inelastic Collisions in One Dimension

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 8, Section 8.5

## Concept explanation
An inelastic collision is one in which the internal kinetic energy changes; it is not conserved. In such collisions, forces between the colliding objects may remove or add internal kinetic energy. Internal work can transform internal kinetic energy into heat transfer, or it can convert stored energy into internal kinetic energy.

A collision in which the objects stick together is called a perfectly inelastic collision. This type of collision reduces internal kinetic energy to the minimum possible while still conserving momentum.

Momentum is conserved in these collisions when the net external force on the system is zero. Internal kinetic energy, however, need not be conserved.

Collisions are important in sports. In tennis, momentum is more important than force. A heavier racquet can have an advantage over a lighter one. The impact location on the racquet also matters: a “sweet spot” minimizes vibration and impact and can give the ball more velocity.

## Key formulas
Conservation of momentum for a two-object system:
$$ 
m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}
 $$

For a perfectly inelastic collision, the two objects stick together and share the same final velocity:
$$ 
v_{1f} = v_{2f}
 $$

Change in internal kinetic energy:
$$ 
\Delta K = K_f - K_i
 $$

Coefficient of restitution:
$$ 
e = \frac{\text{speed after collision}}{\text{speed before collision}}
 $$

For a ball bouncing vertically:
$$ 
e = \sqrt{\frac{h}{H}}
 $$
where $h $ is the bounce height and $H $ is the drop height.

## Variables and units
- $m_1, m_2 $: masses of the two objects, in kg
- $v_{1i}, v_{2i} $: initial velocities, in m/s
- $v_{1f}, v_{2f} $: final velocities, in m/s
- $K_i $: initial kinetic energy, in J
- $K_f $: final kinetic energy, in J
- $\Delta K $: change in kinetic energy, in J
- $e $: coefficient of restitution, unitless
- $h $: bounce height, in m
- $H $: drop height, in m

For a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects move together after impact and momentum is conserved, but internal kinetic energy decreases.

## Worked example
### Example 8.5: Calculating Velocity and Change in Kinetic Energy: Inelastic Collision of a Puck and a Goalie

A 70.0-kg ice hockey goalie, initially at rest, catches a 0.150-kg hockey puck moving at 35.0 m/s.

**(a) Find the recoil velocity of the goalie-puck system.**

Using conservation of momentum:
$$ 
m_{\text{puck}} v_{\text{puck},i} + m_{\text{goalie}} v_{\text{goalie},i}
= (m_{\text{puck}} + m_{\text{goalie}})v_f
 $$

Since the goalie is initially at rest:
$$ 
(0.150)(35.0) = (70.0 + 0.150)v_f
 $$

Solving gives:
$$ 
v_f = 0.0749\ \text{m/s}
 $$

This recoil velocity is small and in the same direction as the puck’s original velocity.

**(b) How much kinetic energy is lost?**

Initial kinetic energy is that of the puck only:
$$ 
K_i = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.150)(35.0)^2 = 91.9\ \text{J}
 $$

Final kinetic energy of the combined system:
$$ 
K_f = \frac{1}{2}(70.15)(0.0749)^2 \approx 0.197\ \text{J}
 $$

Change in kinetic energy:
$$ 
\Delta K = K_f - K_i \approx 0.197 - 91.9 = -91.7\ \text{J}
 $$

The negative sign shows that energy was lost, mostly converted to thermal energy and sound.

### Example 8.6: Calculating Final Velocity and Energy Release: Two Carts Collide

Two carts collide inelastically on a frictionless air track. Cart 1, with mass 0.350 kg, carries a compressed spring and has initial velocity $v_{1i} $. Cart 2 has mass 0.500 kg and initial velocity $v_{2i} $. After the collision, cart 1 recoils with a velocity of $v_{1f} $. The problem asks for the final velocity of cart 2 and the energy released by the spring.

Because the track is frictionless and the spring force is internal, momentum is conserved:
$$ 
m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}
 $$

The final velocity $v_{2f} $ is found by solving this equation. Then the internal kinetic energy before and after the collision is compared to determine the increase in internal kinetic energy.

The section states that the internal kinetic energy in this collision increases by 5.46 J, and that energy was released by the spring.
