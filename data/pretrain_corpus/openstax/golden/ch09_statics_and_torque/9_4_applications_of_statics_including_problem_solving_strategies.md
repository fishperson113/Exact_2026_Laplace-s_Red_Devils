# 9.4 Applications of Statics, Including Problem-Solving Strategies

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 9, Section 9.4

## Concept explanation
Statics applies to a wide range of situations, from raising a drawbridge to posture and back strain. Since statics is a special case of Newton’s laws, the general problem-solving strategies for Newton’s laws also apply.

For a system in static equilibrium:
- the acceleration of the system is zero, and
- accelerated rotation does not occur.

The basic equilibrium conditions are:
- the net external force must be zero, and
- the net torque must also be zero.

A free-body diagram is particularly important. Carefully label all forces, including their relative magnitudes, directions, and points of application whenever these are known.

When torque is involved, choose the pivot point to simplify the solution. Any pivot point may be chosen, but the most useful ones make the torque from unknown forces zero. Torque is zero if the force is applied at the pivot or along a line through the pivot point. Always choose a convenient coordinate system for projecting forces.

After solving, check whether the answer is reasonable by examining its magnitude, direction, and units.

The section illustrates these ideas with a pole vaulter holding a uniform pole. If the pole’s center of gravity lies halfway between the hands, each hand exerts half the pole’s weight, and the equilibrium conditions are satisfied. If the center of gravity is not midway between the hands, the forces are not equal. The hand closer to the center of gravity supports more weight. If the right hand is moved directly under the center of gravity, it supports all the weight.

The same reasoning applies to other systems, such as supporting a load or a uniform table with four legs, where each leg supports one-fourth of the weight when the support is symmetric about the center of gravity.

Engineers and architects use these equilibrium ideas to design stable structures that withstand wind, earthquakes, and other forces.

## Key formulas
- Static equilibrium conditions:
  - net external force equals zero
  - net torque equals zero
- Torque:
  - torque is zero if the force is applied at the pivot
  - torque is zero if the force acts along a line through the pivot
- For a uniform pole supported symmetrically about its center of gravity, the support forces are equal and each can be half the weight
- For a uniform table with four legs, each leg supports one-fourth of the weight

## Variables and units
- Acceleration: zero in static equilibrium
- Force: measured in newtons, N
- Torque: depends on force and lever arm; zero when the lever arm is zero or the line of action passes through the pivot
- Weight: a force, measured in newtons, N
- Center of gravity, cg: the point through which the weight effectively acts
- Pivot point: the point chosen for taking torques
- Lever arm: the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force
- In the example:
  - pole mass = 5.00 kg
  - hand separation = 0.900 m
  - center of gravity is 0.600 m from the left hand
  - force from the left hand and force from the right hand are unknowns to be solved from equilibrium

## Worked example
### Example 9.2: What Force Is Needed to Support a Weight Held Near Its CG?
For the situation with a pole vaulter holding a uniform pole, calculate:
- (a) the force exerted by the right hand
- (b) the force exerted by the left hand

Given:
- the hands are 0.900 m apart
- the center of gravity of the pole is 0.600 m from the left hand

Strategy:
- Use a free-body diagram for the pole.
- The first condition for equilibrium cannot be used directly because two of the three forces are unknown and the hand forces are not equal in this case.
- Use the second condition for equilibrium by choosing the pivot point at the left hand so that the torque from the left hand is zero.

Solution for (a):
- Take torques about the left hand.
- The gravitational force produces a counterclockwise torque.
- The right-hand force produces a clockwise torque.
- Set the algebraic sum of torques equal to zero:
  - counterclockwise torques + clockwise torques = 0
- Using the torque definition and the given values gives:
  - right-hand force = 29.4 N

Solution for (b):
- Use the first condition for equilibrium: the net force is zero.
- The upward and downward forces must balance.
- Solving gives:
  - left-hand force = 19.6 N

Discussion:
- The right-hand force is exactly half of the left-hand force in the configuration shown, because the right-hand force acts twice as far from the center of gravity as the left-hand force.
