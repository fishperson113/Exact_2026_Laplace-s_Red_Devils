# 10.4 Rotational Kinetic Energy: Work and Energy Revisited

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 10, Section 10.4

## Concept explanation
Work and energy in rotational motion are analogous to work and energy in translational motion.

For a net force exerted perpendicular to the radius of a disk, and remaining perpendicular as the disk rotates, the net work done is the product of the force and the arc length traveled. Using the relations between arc length, torque, and angle, this becomes the rotational work expression.

Torque is analogous to force, and angle is analogous to distance. The rotational work equation is valid in general, even though it was derived for a special case.

To obtain rotational kinetic energy, use the rotational work-energy relationship. Net work changes the rotational kinetic energy of a system, just as net work changes translational kinetic energy. The rotational kinetic energy of an object depends on its moment of inertia and angular velocity.

Flywheels can store large amounts of rotational kinetic energy. This energy can later be converted into translational kinetic energy, gravitational potential energy, or other forms. The section also emphasizes that conservation of energy includes rotational motion.

For rolling objects, some gravitational potential energy becomes rotational kinetic energy and some becomes translational kinetic energy. If an object rolls without slipping, the rotational part reduces the amount of energy available for translation, so it moves more slowly than if it slid without rotating. Objects with mass distributed farther from the axis of rotation generally have larger moments of inertia and can store more rotational kinetic energy for the same angular speed.

## Key formulas
$$ 
W = Fd
 $$

$$ 
W = \tau \theta
 $$

$$ 
\tau = rF
\quad \text{(for a force perpendicular to the radius)}
 $$

$$ 
K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2
 $$

$$ 
W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K_{\text{rot}}
 $$

$$ 
\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = \text{rotational kinetic energy}
 $$

For conservation of energy in systems with rotational motion:
$$ 
\text{initial energy} = \text{final energy}
 $$
with translational and rotational contributions included as appropriate.

For rolling without slipping:
$$ 
v = r\omega
 $$

For a solid cylinder:
$$ 
I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2
 $$

For a thin rod rotated about one end:
$$ 
I = \frac{1}{3}ML^2
 $$

For a disk:
$$ 
I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2
 $$

## Variables and units
- $W $: work, measured in joules (J)
- $F $: force, measured in newtons (N)
- $d $: distance or arc length, measured in meters (m)
- $\tau $: torque, measured in newton-meters (N·m)
- $\theta $: angle, measured in radians (rad)
- $r $: radius or lever arm, measured in meters (m)
- $I $: moment of inertia, measured in kg·m$^2 $
- $\omega $: angular velocity, measured in rad/s
- $K_{\text{rot}} $: rotational kinetic energy, measured in joules (J)
- $M $: mass, measured in kilograms (kg)
- $R $: radius, measured in meters (m)
- $L $: length, measured in meters (m)
- $v $: speed, measured in m/s

## Worked example
### Example 10.8: Calculating the Work and Energy for Spinning a Grindstone

A person spins a large grindstone by exerting a force of 200 N through a rotation of $\frac{1}{2} $ revolution. The force is kept perpendicular to the grindstone’s 0.320-m radius, and friction is negligible.

**(a) Work done**
$$ 
\tau = rF = (0.320\ \text{m})(200\ \text{N}) = 64.0\ \text{N·m}
 $$

The rotation angle is
$$ 
\theta = \frac{1}{2}\text{ rev} = \pi\ \text{rad}
 $$

So the work is
$$ 
W = \tau\theta = (64.0\ \text{N·m})(\pi\ \text{rad}) = 201\ \text{J}
 $$

**(b) Final angular velocity**
Using the rotational kinematics relationship and the disk moment of inertia, the final angular velocity is found to be:
$$ 
\omega = 6.14\ \text{rad/s}
 $$

**(c) Final rotational kinetic energy**
$$ 
K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = 201\ \text{J}
 $$

This equals the work done, confirming that the work went into rotational kinetic energy.

### Example 10.9: Calculating Helicopter Energies

A small rescue helicopter has four blades, each 4.00 m long and 50.0 kg. Each blade can be approximated as a thin rod rotated about one end. The helicopter’s total loaded mass is 1000 kg.

**(a) Rotational kinetic energy of the blades at 300 rpm**
$$ 
\omega = 300\ \text{rpm} = 31.4\ \text{rad/s}
 $$

For one blade:
$$ 
I = \frac{1}{3}ML^2
 $$
and for four blades the total moment of inertia is four times this value.

The rotational kinetic energy is:
$$ 
K_{\text{rot}} = 1.27\times 10^5\ \text{J}
 $$

**(b) Translational kinetic energy of the helicopter at 20.0 m/s**
$$ 
K_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = 2.00\times 10^5\ \text{J}
 $$

The ratio is:
$$ 
\frac{K_{\text{trans}}}{K_{\text{rot}}} = 0.380
 $$

**(c) Height equivalent to the blade rotational energy**
Equating rotational kinetic energy to gravitational potential energy gives:
$$ 
mgh = K_{\text{rot}}
 $$
so the helicopter could be raised to:
$$ 
h = 53.7\ \text{m}
 $$

### Example 10.10: Calculating the Speed of a Cylinder Rolling Down an Incline

A solid cylinder rolls down a 2.00-m-high incline from rest. Its mass is 0.750 kg and its radius is 4.00 cm.

Using conservation of energy with rolling without slipping:
$$ 
mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2
 $$
with
$$ 
I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2,\quad v=r\omega
 $$

After substitution and simplification, the final speed is:
$$ 
v = 5.42\ \text{m/s}
 $$

This result is valid for any solid cylinder because the mass and radius cancel.

## Key concepts used
- Rotational work is analogous to translational work.
- Torque is the rotational analog of force.
- Angle is the rotational analog of distance.
- Rotational kinetic energy is $\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 $.
- Net work changes rotational kinetic energy.
- Conservation of energy applies to systems with rotational and translational motion.
- Rolling without slipping links linear and angular speed by $v=r\omega $.
- Moment of inertia depends on how mass is distributed relative to the axis of rotation.
- Objects with larger rotational inertia can store more rotational energy at the same angular speed.
