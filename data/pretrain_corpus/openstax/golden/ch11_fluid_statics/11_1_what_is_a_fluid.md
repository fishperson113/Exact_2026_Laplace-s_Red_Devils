# 11.1 What Is a Fluid

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 11, Section 11.1

## Concept explanation
Matter most commonly exists as a solid, liquid, gas, or plasma; these are the common phases of matter. Solids have a definite shape and a specific volume. Liquids have a definite volume but take the shape of their container. Gases have neither a definite shape nor a specific volume because their molecules move to fill the container. Plasmas also have neither definite shape nor volume.

Liquids, gases, and plasmas are considered fluids because they yield to shearing forces, whereas solids resist them. How easily a fluid yields to shearing forces depends on its viscosity.

The microscopic arrangement of atoms explains these differences:

- In solids, atoms are in close contact and remain near fixed neighbors. They vibrate but do not easily change positions. Solids resist stress and compression.
- In liquids, atoms are also in close contact, but they can slide over one another and change neighbors. Liquids flow and remain in an open container, but they resist compression because their atoms are closely packed.
- In gases, particles are far apart compared with their size and move freely. Gases flow and are relatively easy to compress because there is much space and little force between particles.
- In plasmas, electrons, protons, and ions are also widely separated and move freely, similar to gases.

The major distinction emphasized in the section is that gases are easily compressed, whereas liquids are not.

## Variables and units
- Viscosity: a quantity that describes how strongly a fluid resists shearing and how easily it flows
- Shearing force: a force that tends to make layers of a material slide past one another
- Compression: reduction in volume under applied force
- Solid, liquid, gas, plasma: common phases of matter
