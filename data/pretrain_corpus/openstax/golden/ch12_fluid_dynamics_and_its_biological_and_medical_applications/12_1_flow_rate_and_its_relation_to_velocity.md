# 12.1 Flow Rate and Its Relation to Velocity

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 12, Section 12.1

## Concept explanation
Flow rate is the volume of fluid passing a location through an area during a time interval. It is defined by the volume of fluid divided by the elapsed time:
$$ 
Q = \frac{V}{t}
 $$
where $V $ is volume and $t $ is elapsed time.

The SI unit of flow rate is cubic meters per second $(\text{m}^3/\text{s}) $. Other units are also commonly used, such as liters per minute $(\text{L/min}) $. In this text, metric units are used as convenient for the situation. A liter $(\text{L}) $ is $1/1000 $ of a cubic meter or $1000 $ cubic centimeters $(\text{cm}^3) $.

Flow rate and velocity are related but are not the same quantity. Flow rate depends on both the fluid speed and the cross-sectional area of the conduit. For a uniform pipe or similar conduit,
$$ 
Q = Av
 $$
where $A $ is the cross-sectional area and $v $ is the average velocity.

For an incompressible fluid, the flow rate must be the same at all points along a tube:
$$ 
Q_1 = Q_2
 $$
This is the equation of continuity. It means that when the cross-sectional area decreases, the speed must increase, and when the cross-sectional area increases, the speed must decrease. This relationship is valid for all liquids, since liquids are essentially incompressible. Gases are compressible, so the equation must be used with caution for gases when compression or expansion is important.

For branching flow, the total flow rate is conserved across branches, so the general continuity relation involves the sum of the flow rates in the branches.

## Key formulas
$$ 
Q = \frac{V}{t}
 $$

$$ 
Q = Av
 $$

$$ 
Q_1 = Q_2
 $$

For branching flow:
$$ 
\text{total flow rate in one section} = \text{total flow rate in the next section}
 $$

## Variables and units
- $Q $: flow rate, units $\text{m}^3/\text{s} $, $\text{L/min} $, or other volume/time units
- $V $: volume, units $\text{m}^3 $, $\text{cm}^3 $, or $\text{L} $
- $t $: time, units $\text{s} $, $\text{min} $, etc.
- $A $: cross-sectional area, units $\text{m}^2 $
- $v $: average velocity or speed, units $\text{m/s} $

Useful unit relation:
- $1\,\text{L} = 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3 = 1000\,\text{cm}^3 $

## Worked example
**Example 12.1: Calculating Volume from Flow Rate: The Heart Pumps a Lot of Blood in a Lifetime**

How many cubic meters of blood does the heart pump in a 75-year lifetime, assuming the average flow rate is $5.00\ \text{L/min} $?

**Strategy**

Time and flow rate are given, so the volume can be calculated from the definition of flow rate.

**Solution**

Solve
$$ 
Q = \frac{V}{t}
 $$
for volume:
$$ 
V = Qt
 $$

Substitute the known values for a 75-year lifetime and a flow rate of $5.00\ \text{L/min} $, converting units as needed, to find the total volume pumped.

**Discussion**

This amount is about 200,000 tons of blood. For comparison, this is equivalent to about 200 times the volume of water contained in a 6-lane 50-m lap pool.

## Key concepts used
- Flow rate is volume per unit time.
- Flow rate has units of volume/time.
- Flow rate and fluid speed are related by $Q = Av $.
- Incompressible fluids obey continuity: the flow rate is the same throughout the tube.
- When area decreases, speed increases; when area increases, speed decreases.
- Liquids are essentially incompressible, while gases may require caution in applying continuity.
- Branching flow conserves total flow rate across sections.
