# 12.5 The Onset of Turbulence

Source: OpenStax College Physics 2e, Chapter 12, Section 12.5

## Concept explanation
Flow in a very smooth tube or around a smooth, streamlined object will be laminar at low velocity. At high velocity, even flow in a smooth tube or around a smooth object will experience turbulence. At intermediate velocities, flow may oscillate back and forth indefinitely between laminar and turbulent.

An occlusion, or narrowing, of an artery, is likely to cause turbulence because of the irregularity of the blockage, as well as the complexity of blood as a fluid. Turbulence in the circulatory system is noisy and can sometimes be detected with a stethoscope. Turbulent sounds at the onset of blood flow when cuff pressure becomes sufficiently small are called Korotkoff sounds. Aneurysms create significant turbulence and can sometimes be detected with a stethoscope. Heart murmurs are sounds produced by turbulent flow around damaged and insufficiently closed heart valves. Ultrasound can also be used to detect turbulence as a medical indicator.

An indicator called the Reynolds number can reveal whether flow is laminar or turbulent. For flow in a tube of uniform diameter, the Reynolds number is a unitless quantity related to the onset of turbulence. Experiments have revealed that for Reynolds number below about 2000, flow is laminar. For values between about 2000 and 3000, flow is unstable and can be laminar, but small obstructions and surface roughness can make it turbulent, and it may oscillate randomly between being laminar and turbulent. For Reynolds number above about 3000, flow is turbulent. The blood flow through most of the body is a quiet, laminar flow. The exception is in the aorta, where the speed of the blood flow rises above a critical value and becomes turbulent.

Each branching of larger vessels into smaller vessels increases the total cross-sectional area of the tubes through which the blood flows. In that manner, the resistance of the branchings is reduced so that pressure is not entirely lost. Moreover, because average velocity of the blood in the smaller vessels is reduced, the blood can exchange substances with the cells in the capillaries and alveoli in particular.

## Key formulas
Reynolds number for flow in a tube of uniform diameter:

$$ 
\text{Re} = \frac{2 \rho r v}{\eta}
 $$

where:
- $\rho $ is the fluid density
- $r $ is the tube radius
- $v $ is the fluid speed
- $\eta $ is the fluid viscosity

Interpretation:
- $\text{Re} < 2000 $: laminar flow
- $2000 \lesssim \text{Re} \lesssim 3000 $: unstable flow
- $\text{Re} > 3000 $: turbulent flow

## Variables and units
- Reynolds number, $\text{Re} $: unitless
- Fluid density, $\rho $: mass density, units not specified in the source section
- Tube radius, $r $: length
- Fluid speed, $v $: speed
- Fluid viscosity, $\eta $: viscosity

## Key concepts used
- Laminar flow at low velocity in smooth tubes or around smooth objects
- Turbulent flow at high velocity
- Intermediate flow can be unstable and alternate between laminar and turbulent
- Reynolds number as the criterion for determining flow regime
- Thresholds for laminar, unstable, and turbulent flow
- Medical examples of turbulence: artery occlusion, Korotkoff sounds, aneurysms, heart murmurs
- Branching vessels increase total cross-sectional area and reduce flow velocity
