# Example 22.1: Electric flux through a disk

## Problem
A disk of radius 0.10 m is oriented with its normal unit vector at 30° to a uniform electric field of magnitude 2.0 × 10^3 N/C (Fig. 22.7). Since this isn’t a closed surface, it has no “inside” or “outside,” so the direction of **n̂** must be specified.

(a) What is the electric flux through the disk?  
(b) What is the flux through the disk if it is turned so that **n̂** is perpendicular to **E**?  
(c) What is the flux through the disk if **n̂** is parallel to **E**?

## Setup
This problem is about a flat surface in a uniform electric field, so we can apply the ideas of this section. We calculate the electric flux using Eq. (22.1):

$$ 
\Phi_E = EA\cos\phi
 $$

where $\phi $ is the angle between **n̂** and **E**.

The area of the disk is

$$ 
A = \pi r^2 = \pi(0.10\ \text{m})^2 = 0.0314\ \text{m}^2
 $$

## Solution
### (a)
The angle between **n̂** and **E** is $30^\circ $, so

$$ 
\Phi_E = EA\cos\phi
= (2.0 \times 10^3\ \text{N/C})(0.0314\ \text{m}^2)\cos 30^\circ
 $$

$$ 
\Phi_E = 54\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}
 $$

### (b)
The normal to the disk is now perpendicular to **E**, so

$$ 
\phi = 90^\circ,\qquad \cos\phi = 0
 $$

Thus,

$$ 
\Phi_E = 0
 $$

### (c)
The normal to the disk is parallel to **E**, so

$$ 
\phi = 0^\circ,\qquad \cos\phi = 1
 $$

Thus,

$$ 
\Phi_E = EA
= (2.0 \times 10^3\ \text{N/C})(0.0314\ \text{m}^2)
 $$

$$ 
\Phi_E = 63\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}
 $$

## Evaluation
As a check on the results, note that the answer to part (b) is smaller than that to part (a), which is in turn smaller than that to part (c). This is consistent with the changing angle between the surface normal and the electric field.

## Key concepts used
- Electric flux through a flat surface in a uniform electric field
- $\Phi_E = EA\cos\phi $
- Surface area of a disk: $A = \pi r^2 $
- Dependence of flux on the angle between **n̂** and **E**
