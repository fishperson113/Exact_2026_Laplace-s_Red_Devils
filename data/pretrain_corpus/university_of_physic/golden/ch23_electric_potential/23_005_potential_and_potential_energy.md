# Example 23.5: Potential and potential energy

## Problem
Compute the potential energy associated with a point charge if it is placed at points a, b, and c in Fig. 23.13.

## Setup
The potential energy $U $ associated with a point charge $q $ at a location where the electric potential is $V $ is

$$ 
U = qV.
 $$

We use the values of $V $ from Example 23.4.

## Solution
At the three points we find

$$ 
U_c = qV_c = 0
 $$

$$ 
U_b = qV_b = (14.0 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{C})(1930\ \text{J/C}) = 7.7 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{J}
 $$

$$ 
U_a = qV_a = (14.0 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{C})(-900\ \text{J/C}) = -3.6 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{J}
 $$

All of these values correspond to $U $ and $V $ being zero at infinity.

## Evaluation
Note that zero net work is done on the point charge if it moves from point $c $ to infinity by any path. In particular, let the path be along the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the other two charges $q_1 $ and $q_2 $ in Fig. 23.13. As shown in Example 21.8 (Section 21.5), at points on the bisector, the direction of $\vec{E} $ is perpendicular to the bisector. Hence the force on the point charge is perpendicular to the path, and no work is done in any displacement along it.

## Key concepts used
- Electric potential energy of a charge in an electric potential: $U = qV $
- Work done by the electric force is zero when the force is perpendicular to the displacement
- Zero potential energy and zero electric potential are taken at infinity
