# Example 28.3: Magnetic field of a single wire

## Problem
A long, straight conductor carries a 1.0-A current. At what distance from the axis of the conductor does the resulting magnetic field have magnitude $B = 0.5 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{T} $ (about that of the earth’s magnetic field in Pittsburgh)?

## Setup
**IDENTIFY and SET UP:** The length of a “long” conductor is much greater than the distance from the conductor to the field point. Hence we can use the ideas of this section. The geometry is the same as that of Fig. 28.6, so we use Eq. (28.9). All of the quantities in this equation are known except the target variable, the distance $r $.

$$ 
B = 0.5 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{T}
 $$

## Solution
**EXECUTE:** We solve Eq. (28.9) for $r $:

$$ 
r = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi B}
= \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T}\cdot\text{m/A})(1.0\ \text{A})}{2\pi(0.5 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{T})}
= 4 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}
= 4\ \text{mm}
 $$

## Evaluation
**EVALUATE:** As we saw in Example 26.14, currents of an ampere or more are typical of those found in the wiring of home appliances. This example shows that the magnetic fields produced by these appliances are very weak even very close to the wire; the fields are proportional to $1/r $, so they become even weaker at greater distances.

## Key concepts used
- Magnetic field of a long, straight current-carrying wire
- Use of $B = \mu_0 I / (2\pi r) $
- Solving for distance from a specified magnetic field strength
